# Constructionist Epistemology in Dialogic RTA

---

## A Note on Terminology

This methodology uses the term **Dialogic Reflexive Thematic Analysis** rather than "AI-Augmented RTA." The terminological choice is not cosmetic — it reflects an epistemological position.

"AI-Augmented" implies a single analytical agent (the researcher) supported by a tool (AI). It positions AI as an add-on to an otherwise unchanged method. "Dialogic" recognizes that analytical meaning is constructed through multi-layered dialogue — between researcher and AI, researcher and data, and researcher and their own interpretive commitments — and that this dialogic process is constitutive of the analysis, not incidental to it.

In practice, every coding decision involves multiple simultaneous dialogues. When a researcher says "varför alltid samma antal koder?" (why always the same number of codes?), they are simultaneously addressing AI (correcting its output), engaging with data (reassessing the segment's semantic richness), and reflecting on their own analytical standards (what should code density be driven by?). These dialogues are not separable. The analysis is their product.

This is consistent with Bakhtin's (1981, 1986) concept of heteroglossia — the simultaneous coexistence of multiple voices in discourse. The analytical voice in Dialogic RTA is never singular. It carries traces of AI's computational patterns, the researcher's interpretive commitments, participants' constructed accounts, and the dialogue through which these were negotiated. For how the dialogic process is preserved and reflected upon, see `dialogic_reflexivity.md`.

## The Continuum

Reflexive Thematic Analysis requires the researcher to position their work on the epistemological continuum between essentialism and constructionism. This is not a formality — it determines what the analysis can claim, what counts as a finding, and how AI's role is understood.

**Essentialist (realist) position:** Language reflects reality. Meaning exists independently of the researcher and can be discovered through systematic analysis. Participants report experiences that correspond to objective states. The researcher's task is accurate representation.

**Constructionist (relativist) position:** Language constructs reality. Meaning is socially produced through interaction, institutional context, and available discourse. Participants do not report pre-existing experiences — they construct accounts shaped by the social resources available to them. The researcher's task is interpretation of these constructions.

Most reflexive TA positions itself toward the constructionist end, though pure constructionism is as rare as pure essentialism. The key question is not "which box?" but "what does my position allow me to claim about my data?"

## What Constructionism Means for Analysis

A constructionist position has direct analytical consequences that shape every phase of the work.

**Codes are constructions, not discoveries.** The researcher does not find codes hiding in the data. Codes are interpretive labels actively constructed through the researcher's engagement with participant accounts. Another researcher, with different theoretical commitments and contextual knowledge, might construct different codes from the same data. In constructionist RTA, this is expected and methodologically coherent — it would only be problematic under essentialist assumptions where codes correspond to objective patterns.

**Meaningfulness is not frequency.** Braun and Clarke's white-collar workers example is instructive: if most participants report starting work at 9:00 a.m., this is a pattern but not necessarily a meaningful one. Constructionist analysis asks what patterns reveal about how meaning is socially produced — not which patterns recur most often. A rare but analytically productive construction may matter more than a common but shallow one.

**Context is constitutive, not background.** Institutional setting, professional culture, language, and conversational dynamics do not merely influence what participants say — they shape what it is possible to say and think. When a teacher in the Swedish education system discusses AI, the available meanings are structured by Swedish pedagogical traditions, institutional policies, collegial norms, and the specific social dynamics of the focus group. These are not confounding variables to control for — they are the conditions of meaning-making itself.

**The researcher is inside the analysis.** Constructionism means the researcher's own frameworks, assumptions, and interpretive commitments participate in meaning construction. Reflexivity is not an add-on quality criterion — it is an epistemological necessity. The researcher documents not just what they found but how their own position shaped what they could find.

## Positioning Your Analysis

Before beginning any analytical phase, the researcher must articulate their epistemological position in writing. This is not a reflexive exercise to be completed and filed — it is a methodological artefact that shapes every subsequent decision and becomes part of the audit trail.

The positioning requires the researcher to address four questions:

### What is the nature of reality in this study?

The researcher must be explicit about whether participants' accounts are treated as reports of objective experience or as socially constructed understandings shaped by institutional discourse, professional culture, and available language. Constructionist analysis does not deny that participants have experiences — it holds that how those experiences are understood, articulated, and made meaningful is socially produced.

### What is the relationship between language and experience?

Byrne (2022, p. 1395) frames the distinction clearly: essentialist positions treat language as "a simple reflection of our articulated meanings and experiences," while constructionist positions understand "language as implicit in the social production and reproduction of both meaning and experience." The researcher must decide: when a teacher says *"det är kaos"* (it's chaos), are they reporting an internal state, or are they performing a culturally available construction of professional experience that simultaneously shapes what the experience *is*?

### What counts as meaningful?

Braun and Clarke (2012, p. 37) are explicit that recurrence alone does not make a pattern meaningful. The researcher must articulate what meaningfulness means in the context of their specific research questions and epistemological position. For constructionist analysis, meaningful patterns are those that reveal something about how meaning is socially produced — not those that recur most often.

### The critical question: How can computational pattern recognition serve constructionist analysis?

This is the central methodological tension that the researcher must theorize before beginning analysis. AI operates through statistical probabilities trained on vast datasets — what Ozuem et al. (2025) describe as processes that "disregard the relational, situated, and socially constructed nature of meaning." The researcher must articulate:

- What AI does that is **compatible** with constructionism (systematic coverage, surfacing patterns the researcher's selective attention might miss, provoking interpretive response)
- What AI does that **threatens** constructionism (reifying patterns as discoveries, flattening contextual nuance, privileging frequency over meaningfulness)
- How the dialogic process **resolves** this tension (AI proposes, researcher constructs; dialogue forces explicit articulation of interpretive reasoning)

The researcher's answers to these four questions form the epistemological foundation for the analysis. They should be documented as part of the project setup and revisited if the researcher's understanding shifts during analytical work.

## The Constructionist-Computational Tension

This is the central epistemological challenge of Dialogic RTA, and addressing it coherently is a methodological contribution in its own right.

AI operates through statistical pattern recognition across training data. It identifies lexical regularities, co-occurrence patterns, and structural similarities. This computational logic has specific epistemological characteristics that sit uneasily with constructionism:

**AI tends toward essentialist assumptions.** When AI proposes a code, it treats the code as corresponding to a pattern that exists in the data — a discoverable regularity. Constructionism holds that the pattern does not exist independently of the interpretive act that constructs it. The researcher must maintain awareness that AI's proposals reflect computational pattern-matching, not interpretive construction.

**AI cannot access constitutive context.** The institutional, cultural, and linguistic conditions that make meaning possible are not available to AI through text alone. When a Swedish teacher says *"vi har inte tid"* (we don't have time), AI processes the semantic content. The researcher recognises this as potentially performing professional identity, invoking shared cultural narrative about teacher workload, managing accountability, or describing material reality — and that these functions are not mutually exclusive but contextually determined.

**AI's patterns reflect training data, not situated meaning.** As Ozuem et al. (2025) argue, AI risks "reifying interpretations as though they were objective or generalisable, thus flattening the nuance and plurality that constructionist thematic analysis seeks to preserve." AI does not construct meaning from this data — it recognises patterns that resemble patterns in other data it has processed. The difference matters epistemologically.

**AI produces agreement, not interpretation.** When asked to evaluate a code or theme, AI tends toward confirmation — what the overview document calls "computational agreeableness." Under constructionist epistemology, where meaning is contested and plural, uncritical agreement is analytically empty. The researcher needs challenge, alternative readings, and productive disagreement — precisely what AI is least inclined to provide without explicit prompting.

## Orienting the Dialogic Partnership

Constructionist Dialogic RTA requires that both analytical partners — the researcher and AI — are oriented toward the project's epistemological commitments before analysis begins. The researcher achieves this through the positioning work described above. AI requires explicit orientation.

This is not prompt engineering. It is an epistemological practice: ensuring that the dialogic starting point is shaped by the project's framework rather than by AI's training-data defaults. Without orientation, AI's initial proposals will reflect generic analytical patterns (benefits/barriers, adoption/resistance, thematic structures common in its training data), and the researcher's corrective work begins from a lower starting point.

### What AI needs to understand

The orientation should communicate:

**The epistemological position and its implications.** AI needs to understand that this is constructionist analysis — that codes and themes are constructions, not discoveries; that frequency does not equal meaningfulness; that context is constitutive. Without this, AI defaults to essentialist-compatible pattern identification.

**The research questions as relevance criteria.** Research questions guide what is analytically interesting — they are not predetermined categories to be coded toward. AI's tendency is to code broadly; orientation toward specific research questions provides the filter that constructionist analysis requires.

**AI's role as dialogic partner.** AI proposes, the researcher constructs. AI's outputs are starting points for interpretive engagement, not findings. The researcher may accept, modify, or reject any proposal based on contextual knowledge, epistemological commitment, and reflexive judgement. This is not a limitation of AI — it is how the method works.

**What AI cannot access.** The contextual knowledge that makes constructionist analysis possible — institutional culture, conversational dynamics, cultural connotation, linguistic nuance, the weight of what is *not* said — is not available to AI through text alone. The researcher's contextual knowledge is not a supplement to AI's analysis. It is the interpretive resource that transforms computational pattern identification into constructionist meaning construction.

### Evaluating the orientation

After orienting AI, the researcher should verify that AI has understood its role. Red flags include: AI claiming it will "find" themes (rather than propose patterns for interpretive evaluation), AI not acknowledging the limitations of computational pattern recognition, or AI framing its outputs in terms of accuracy or validity rather than as proposals for dialogic engagement.

The orientation exchange — what was communicated, how AI responded, and the researcher's evaluation — should be documented as part of the project's epistemological record.

## Working Constructionist with AI

The tension is real but not disabling. Constructionist epistemology does not prohibit AI use — it requires that AI be positioned as a dialogic partner whose computational proposals undergo interpretive evaluation.

**AI proposes, the researcher constructs.** AI's pattern identification serves as raw material for the researcher's interpretive work. The code that appears in the analysis is never AI's proposal — it is the researcher's construction, informed by (and sometimes provoked by) AI's proposal but shaped by contextual knowledge, epistemological commitment, and reflexive judgement.

**Treating AI proposals as data about AI.** When AI consistently proposes certain types of codes — benefits/barriers structures, adoption/resistance frameworks, generic thematic patterns — this reveals something about AI's training data and computational tendencies, not about the data being analysed. The researcher can use this meta-awareness productively: AI's predictable proposals become a reference point against which the researcher's own interpretive work gains definition.

**Double reflexivity as epistemological practice.** Traditional reflexivity monitors the researcher's influence on the analysis. Constructionist Dialogic RTA requires monitoring AI's influence as well — not as a technical concern but as an epistemological one. When the researcher accepts an AI-proposed code, the reflexive question is: did I accept this because it captures situated meaning, or because AI's confident framing made a generic pattern seem adequate?

**The constructionist advantage.** Constructionist epistemology is arguably better suited to dialogic analysis than essentialism. An essentialist researcher using AI faces the risk that AI's patterns are mistaken for objective findings. A constructionist researcher already expects that all codes are constructions — adding AI as one source of proposals does not fundamentally alter the epistemological logic, provided the researcher maintains interpretive authority over the construction process.

**The dialogic advantage.** The dialogic form itself strengthens constructionist analysis in a way that solitary coding does not. The researcher who must articulate *to AI* why a code should change, why a variant matters, why the research question requires a different focus, is producing a more explicitly reasoned analysis than the researcher who makes the same decisions silently. The dialogue externalises interpretive reasoning — making it available for scrutiny, documentation, and reflexive review. For how this dialogic process is preserved, see `dialogic_reflexivity.md`.

## Implications Across Phases

The constructionist position operates differently at each analytical phase. At every phase, the dialogic process between researcher and AI produces epistemically significant moments that require preservation (see `dialogic_reflexivity.md` for the cross-cutting practice of logging and reflecting on these moments).

**Phase 2a (Initial Coding):** Codes are the researcher's constructions, not AI's discoveries. The four evaluation questions — situated meaning or lexical pattern? inductively grounded or training-data derived? honouring participant meaning? what am I noticing that AI is not? — operationalise constructionist epistemology at the level of individual coding decisions.

**Phase 2b (Critical Review):** Review evaluates whether codes reflect genuine interpretive engagement or computational pattern-matching accepted without construction. The "proving the obvious" heuristic is particularly relevant: expected patterns are the ones most likely to have been accepted without adequate constructionist scrutiny.

**Phase 3 (Generating Themes):** Themes are interpretive constructions that organise codes into meaningful patterns. AI's tendency to propose thematic structures that mirror common frameworks in its training data (benefits/barriers, adoption/resistance) directly threatens constructionist integrity. The researcher asks: does this thematic structure reflect how meaning is constructed in *this* data, or does it reflect how AI expects data like this to be organised?

**Phase 4 (Reviewing Themes):** The confirmation bias risk — AI validating its own earlier proposals — is an epistemological concern under constructionism. If meaning is constructed, then a theme validated only by the same computational logic that produced it has not been constructed at all. External challenge, alternative readings, and deliberate attempts to break the thematic structure are epistemologically necessary.

**Phase 5 (Defining and Naming):** Theme names carry epistemological weight. Constructionist theme names should capture the researcher's interpretive construction — what this analysis, with these commitments, in this context, found meaningful. AI-generated theme names tend toward generic descriptive labels that could apply to any analysis of similar data. The constructionist test: does this name reflect a specific interpretive construction, or a default categorisation?

**Phase 6 (Writing):** The written report *is* the analysis. Under constructionism, there is no analysis "behind" the text — the text constructs the analytical argument. AI-assisted writing risks producing fluent prose that describes rather than constructs, that categorises rather than interprets. The researcher's own analytical voice — with its specific texture, commitments, and interpretive depth — is the medium through which constructionist analysis becomes visible.

## Documenting the Epistemological Dialogue

The epistemological positioning described in this document produces its own dialogic process that must be documented. This includes:

**The researcher's written position.** The answers to the four positioning questions, articulated before analysis begins. This is a living document — the researcher may revise their understanding as analytical work deepens it, but revisions should be documented rather than silently replacing the original.

**The AI orientation exchange.** What was communicated to AI, how AI responded, and the researcher's evaluation of that response. This records the epistemological starting point of the dialogic partnership.

**Epistemological tensions encountered during analysis.** Moments where the constructionist-computational tension became practically relevant — where AI's proposals violated constructionist principles and the researcher intervened. These are epistemically significant events in the sense described in `dialogic_reflexivity.md`, and should be logged and reflected upon through the practices described there.

This documentation is not administrative overhead. It is the evidence that the analysis was conducted within a coherent epistemological framework — and that the framework was actively maintained rather than merely declared.

---

## Readiness Check

Before proceeding to Phase 1, the researcher should be able to confirm:

- Their epistemological position is articulated in writing and addresses all four positioning questions
- They can explain what this position allows them to claim about their data — and what it prevents them from claiming
- They have oriented AI toward the project's epistemological commitments and evaluated AI's understanding
- They can articulate how the dialogic process resolves the constructionist-computational tension in their specific project
- They have documented the positioning and orientation as part of the project's epistemological record
- They understand that dialogic reflexivity (see `dialogic_reflexivity.md`) is an ongoing practice, not a one-time setup
