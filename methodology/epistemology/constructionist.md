# Section 3.1.1: Essentialist versus Constructionist Epistemologies
## A Dialogic Approach - Preparing Both Human and AI

**NEW TERMINOLOGY**: "Dialogic Reflexive Thematic Analysis" (not "AI-Augmented")
- Emphasizes the dialogue/conversation between human and AI
- Captures the interpretive exchange, not just assistance
- Grounded in constructionist view that meaning emerges through dialogue

## Part 1: FOR THE HUMAN RESEARCHER - Thinking Through Your Epistemological Position

### Step 1.1: Understanding the Continuum

**Read this first** (from Byrne 2022, p. 1395):

> "Ontological and epistemological considerations would usually be determined when a study is first being conceptualized. However, these considerations may become salient again when data analysis becomes the research focus..."

**The Question**: Where do YOU position your research on this continuum?

```
ESSENTIALISM ←―――――――――――――――――――→ CONSTRUCTIONISM
(realist)                                    (relativist)

Language REFLECTS reality  ←→  Language CONSTRUCTS reality
Unidirectional             ←→  Bidirectional
Meaning is fixed           ←→  Meaning is socially produced
```

**Essentialist answer would be**: 
"Teachers' experiences of AI in education exist as objective facts that I can discover and report."

**Constructionist answer would be**: 
"Teachers' experiences of AI are shaped by their social interactions, institutional contexts, and the language they use to describe these experiences."

#### Q2: What is the relationship between language and experience?

**Essentialist view** (from Byrne, p. 1395):
> "language is a simple reflection of our articulated meanings and experiences"

**Constructionist view** (from Byrne, p. 1395):
> "language as implicit in the social production and reproduction of both meaning and experience"

**YOUR ANSWER**:
```

are they simply reporting inner states? Or are these terms actively shaping how they

```

#### Q3: What does "meaningful" mean in your analysis?

From Braun & Clarke (via Byrne, p. 1395):

**Recurrence alone ≠ Meaningful**

> "in researching white-collar workers' experiences of sociality at work... If most or all reported that they started work at around 9:00 a.m., this would be a pattern in the data, but it would not necessarily be a meaningful or important one."

**YOUR ANSWER**:
```
[What makes something MEANINGFUL in your data? 
- Just that teachers mention it often?
- That they express strong feelings about it?
- That it helps answer your research questions?
- That it reveals something about the SOCIAL CONSTRUCTION of AI use in education?]
```

#### Q4: The Critical Question About AI

**If you adopt CONSTRUCTIONIST epistemology**, you face this tension (from Ozuem et al. 2025):

> "LLMs operate on statistical probabilities and are trained on vast datasets to detect patterns that often disregard the relational, situated, and socially constructed nature of meaning"

**SO**: How can you use AI's computational pattern recognition WITHOUT violating constructionist principles?

**YOUR ANSWER**:
```
[This is THE central question of your methodology. 
Think about:
- What does AI do that is COMPATIBLE with constructionism?
- What does AI do that THREATENS constructionism?
- How will you maintain constructionist integrity while using AI?]
```

## Part 3: FOR THE AI - Familiarization with Epistemological Position

### Why This Matters (from Naeem et al. 2023, 2024)

Naeem argues that AI needs familiarization with:
- Research context
- Theoretical framework  
- Methodological approach
- **Philosophical underpinnings** ← THIS IS KEY

**Critical finding** from Naeem (p. 5):
> "researchers need to familiarize themselves with the theoretical, contextual and philosophical backgrounds of their research in order to justify their choice of relevant quotations"

**Our addition**: AI also needs this familiarization to function as a dialogic partner

### The AI Familiarization Prompt Template

**When to use**: At the START of your analysis (Phase 1), after YOU have clarified your own epistemological position

**Purpose**: Not to make AI "understand" constructionism (it can't), but to:
1. Orient AI toward the types of patterns that matter in your framework
2. Establish shared vocabulary for your dialogue
3. Create a foundation for interpretive exchange

---

### PROMPT: Familiarizing AI with Your Epistemological Position

```markdown
I am conducting a Reflexive Thematic Analysis of focus group data about teachers' 
AI use in Swedish education. Before we begin analysis, I need to familiarize you 
with my epistemological position so we can engage in productive dialogue.

EPISTEMOLOGICAL POSITION: Social Constructionism

This means:
1. Reality and meaning are socially produced through language and interaction
2. Teachers' experiences of AI are not pre-existing facts to "discover" but are 
   actively constructed through:
   - Their professional discourse
   - Institutional contexts (Swedish education system)
   - Cultural frameworks (Nordic pedagogical traditions)
   - Language itself (they speak in Swedish, which shapes available meanings)

3. My role as researcher is not to find "the truth" but to interpret how teachers 
   construct meaning about AI in their specific context

IMPLICATIONS FOR OUR DIALOGUE:

When I ask you to identify patterns or propose themes, understand that:

- RECURRENCE ≠ MEANINGFULNESS
  A pattern that appears frequently is not automatically significant. I'm interested 
  in patterns that reveal how teachers SOCIALLY CONSTRUCT their experiences.

- CONTEXT IS PARAMOUNT
  You operate on statistical probabilities from training data. I operate from deep 
  immersion in:
  * Swedish educational culture
  * The specific institutions (BSG, MÅG, NE)
  * The focus group dynamics
  * Language nuances (Swedish → English translation)
  
- YOUR ROLE IS TO PROPOSE, NOT DETERMINE
  When you identify a pattern, you are making a PROPOSAL that I will interpret 
  through my constructionist lens. I may:
  * Accept it
  * Modify it based on contextual knowledge
  * Reject it as computationally interesting but contextually meaningless
  
  This is not a failure of your pattern recognition - it's the nature of 
  constructionist analysis.

SPECIFIC GUIDANCE FOR YOU:

✓ DO attend to:
- How teachers TALK about AI (the language they use)
- Tensions, contradictions, ambivalences in their accounts
- Differences across groups/contexts
- Underlying assumptions in their discourse

✗ DON'T assume:
- Frequency = importance
- Teachers are reporting objective facts
- Your patterns are "findings" (they're starting points for interpretation)
- One "correct" interpretation exists

RESEARCH QUESTIONS (to guide your pattern attention):
[Insert your specific research questions here]

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK:
[If you have one - e.g., teacher professional identity theory, AI literacy frameworks, etc.]

CONFIRM UNDERSTANDING:
Please summarize back to me:
1. What is my epistemological position?
2. What is your role in this dialogic analysis?
3. What should you pay attention to when I share data?
```

### Expected AI Response (and How to Evaluate It)

AI should respond something like:

> "I understand that you're conducting a constructionist analysis where:
> 
> 1. **Epistemology**: You view teachers' experiences as socially constructed rather 
>    than objectively real. Language doesn't just describe their experiences - it 
>    shapes them.
> 
> 2. **My role**: I'm a dialogic partner who proposes patterns based on computational 
>    analysis, but YOU make interpretive decisions based on contextual knowledge and 
>    constructionist principles.
> 
> 3. **Focus**: I should attend to the LANGUAGE teachers use, tensions in their 
>    accounts, contextual differences, and underlying assumptions - not just frequency 
>    of topics.
> 
> I'll flag patterns for your consideration, understanding that you may accept, 
> modify, or reject them based on factors I can't access (cultural context, group 
> dynamics, Swedish language nuances, etc.)."

**RED FLAGS** in AI response:
- If AI claims it will "find the themes" (NO - it proposes, you decide)
- If AI doesn't acknowledge its computational limitations
- If AI talks about "accuracy" or "validity" in positivist terms

## Part 8: EXAMPLE - How This Might Look in Practice

### Example Case Study

**Research Context**: 
Three focus groups (BSG, MÅG, NE) with Swedish teachers about AI use in education

**Epistemological Position** (draft):

```markdown
This analysis adopts a social constructionist epistemology, positioning teachers' 
experiences of AI not as objective realities awaiting discovery, but as actively 
constructed through the language, institutional contexts, and professional cultures 
available to them. 

When teachers in the MÅG focus group express concerns about "lärarnas välmående" 
(teacher wellbeing) in relation to AI integration, this is understood not as a 
transparent report of an internal psychological state, but as a socially constructed 
phenomenon shaped by:

- Swedish educational discourse around teacher stress and workload
- The specific institutional context of their school
- The conversational dynamics of the focus group itself
- The cultural frameworks available in Swedish for discussing technology and wellbeing

This constructionist stance creates a specific tension with the use of AI in analysis. 
AI's computational pattern recognition operates through statistical probabilities 
derived from vast training datasets, potentially overlooking the "relational, situated, 
and socially constructed nature of meaning" (Ozuem et al., 2025, p. X). 

To address this tension, this analysis employs a dialogic approach in which AI 
functions not as a neutral analytical tool, but as an interlocutor whose computational 
proposals are subjected to interpretive evaluation grounded in:
- Deep immersion in Swedish educational contexts
- Understanding of linguistic nuances in Swedish-language data
- Appreciation of the institutional specificity of each focus group setting
- Reflexive awareness of the researcher's own role in meaning construction

This approach treats AI pattern identification as a starting point for interpretive 
dialogue, not as findings in themselves.
```

**AI Familiarization Prompt** (draft):

```markdown
I'm beginning a Reflexive Thematic Analysis of three focus groups with Swedish 
teachers about AI use in education. My epistemological position is CONSTRUCTIONIST.

Key context you need:
- Data: Focus group transcripts in Swedish (BSG, MÅG, NE schools)
- Participants: Teachers with varying roles (core curriculum, wellbeing, management)
- Topic: Their experiences implementing and using AI in teaching
- Language: All data in Swedish; analysis/reporting in English

CONSTRUCTIONIST PRINCIPLES:

1. Teachers' experiences are not objective facts but socially constructed through:
   - Swedish educational culture (strong teacher autonomy, consensus-based)
   - Institutional contexts (different school cultures at BSG, MÅG, NE)
   - Professional discourse (how Swedish teachers talk about technology/pedagogy)
   - Focus group dynamics (who speaks, what's safe to say, group consensus-building)

2. Language doesn't just describe experience - it shapes it. When teachers use 
   terms like "arbetsbelastning" (workload), "välmående" (wellbeing), or "digital 
   kompetens" (digital competence), these aren't neutral descriptors - they're 
   culturally specific constructions.

YOUR ROLE as dialogic partner:
- Identify computational patterns across the three transcripts
- Propose potential thematic clusters for my consideration
- Flag interesting contradictions, tensions, or variations
- BUT: I make all interpretive decisions based on contextual knowledge you lack

WHAT YOU CAN'T ACCESS (so defer to me):
- Swedish language nuances and cultural connotations
- Nordic pedagogical traditions and Swedish education policy context
- Specific institutional histories and cultures of these schools
- Non-verbal dynamics from the focus groups (tone, pauses, laughter)
- My own positionality as researcher in Swedish higher education

WHAT I NEED FROM YOU:
- Identification of recurring themes across groups
- Noticing when one group's discourse differs from others
- Highlighting tensions/contradictions in teacher accounts
- Proposing relationships between different code clusters

CONFIRMATION:
Please confirm your understanding of:
1. My constructionist epistemology
2. Your role as pattern-proposer (not theme-determiner)
3. The importance of Swedish/Nordic educational context
```
