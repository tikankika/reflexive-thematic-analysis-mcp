# Constructionist Epistemology in AI-Augmented RTA

---

## The Continuum

Reflexive Thematic Analysis requires the researcher to position their work on the epistemological continuum between essentialism and constructionism. This is not a formality — it determines what the analysis can claim, what counts as a finding, and how AI's role is understood.

**Essentialist (realist) position:** Language reflects reality. Meaning exists independently of the researcher and can be discovered through systematic analysis. Participants report experiences that correspond to objective states. The researcher's task is accurate representation.

**Constructionist (relativist) position:** Language constructs reality. Meaning is socially produced through interaction, institutional context, and available discourse. Participants do not report pre-existing experiences — they construct accounts shaped by the social resources available to them. The researcher's task is interpretation of these constructions.

Most reflexive TA positions itself toward the constructionist end, though pure constructionism is as rare as pure essentialism. The key question is not "which box?" but "what does my position allow me to claim about my data?"

---

## What Constructionism Means for Analysis

A constructionist position has direct analytical consequences that shape every phase of the work.

**Codes are constructions, not discoveries.** The researcher does not find codes hiding in the data. Codes are interpretive labels actively constructed through the researcher's engagement with participant accounts. Another researcher, with different theoretical commitments and contextual knowledge, might construct different codes from the same data. In constructionist RTA, this is expected and methodologically coherent — it would only be problematic under essentialist assumptions where codes correspond to objective patterns.

**Meaningfulness is not frequency.** Braun and Clarke's white-collar workers example is instructive: if most participants report starting work at 9:00 a.m., this is a pattern but not necessarily a meaningful one. Constructionist analysis asks what patterns reveal about how meaning is socially produced — not which patterns recur most often. A rare but analytically productive construction may matter more than a common but shallow one.

**Context is constitutive, not background.** Institutional setting, professional culture, language, and conversational dynamics do not merely influence what participants say — they shape what it is possible to say and think. When a teacher in the Swedish education system discusses AI, the available meanings are structured by Swedish pedagogical traditions, institutional policies, collegial norms, and the specific social dynamics of the focus group. These are not confounding variables to control for — they are the conditions of meaning-making itself.

**The researcher is inside the analysis.** Constructionism means the researcher's own frameworks, assumptions, and interpretive commitments participate in meaning construction. Reflexivity is not an add-on quality criterion — it is an epistemological necessity. The researcher documents not just what they found but how their own position shaped what they could find.

## The Constructionist-Computational Tension

This is the central epistemological challenge of AI-augmented qualitative analysis, and addressing it coherently is a methodological contribution in its own right.

AI operates through statistical pattern recognition across training data. It identifies lexical regularities, co-occurrence patterns, and structural similarities. This computational logic has specific epistemological characteristics that sit uneasily with constructionism:

**AI tends toward essentialist assumptions.** When AI proposes a code, it treats the code as corresponding to a pattern that exists in the data — a discoverable regularity. Constructionism holds that the pattern does not exist independently of the interpretive act that constructs it. The researcher must maintain awareness that AI's proposals reflect computational pattern-matching, not interpretive construction.

**AI cannot access constitutive context.** The institutional, cultural, and linguistic conditions that make meaning possible are not available to AI through text alone. When a Swedish teacher says *"vi har inte tid"* (we don't have time), AI processes the semantic content. The researcher recognises this as potentially performing professional identity, invoking shared cultural narrative about teacher workload, managing accountability, or describing material reality — and that these functions are not mutually exclusive but contextually determined.

**AI's patterns reflect training data, not situated meaning.** As Ozuem et al. (2025) argue, AI risks "reifying interpretations as though they were objective or generalisable, thus flattening the nuance and plurality that constructionist thematic analysis seeks to preserve." AI does not construct meaning from this data — it recognises patterns that resemble patterns in other data it has processed. The difference matters epistemologically.

**AI produces agreement, not interpretation.** When asked to evaluate a code or theme, AI tends toward confirmation — what the overview document calls "computational agreeableness." Under constructionist epistemology, where meaning is contested and plural, uncritical agreement is analytically empty. The researcher needs challenge, alternative readings, and productive disagreement — precisely what AI is least inclined to provide without explicit prompting.

## Working Constructionist with AI

The tension is real but not disabling. Constructionist epistemology does not prohibit AI use — it requires that AI be positioned as a heuristic partner whose computational proposals undergo interpretive evaluation.

**AI proposes, the researcher constructs.** AI's pattern identification serves as raw material for the researcher's interpretive work. The code that appears in the analysis is never AI's proposal — it is the researcher's construction, informed by (and sometimes provoked by) AI's proposal but shaped by contextual knowledge, epistemological commitment, and reflexive judgement.

**Treating AI proposals as data about AI.** When AI consistently proposes certain types of codes — benefits/barriers structures, adoption/resistance frameworks, generic thematic patterns — this reveals something about AI's training data and computational tendencies, not about the data being analysed. The researcher can use this meta-awareness productively: AI's predictable proposals become a reference point against which the researcher's own interpretive work gains definition.

**Double reflexivity as epistemological practice.** Traditional reflexivity monitors the researcher's influence on the analysis. AI-augmented constructionist analysis requires monitoring AI's influence as well — not as a technical concern but as an epistemological one. When the researcher accepts an AI-proposed code, the reflexive question is: did I accept this because it captures situated meaning, or because AI's confident framing made a generic pattern seem adequate?

**The constructionist advantage.** Constructionist epistemology is arguably better suited to AI augmentation than essentialism. An essentialist researcher using AI faces the risk that AI's patterns are mistaken for objective findings. A constructionist researcher already expects that all codes are constructions — adding AI as one source of proposals does not fundamentally alter the epistemological logic, provided the researcher maintains interpretive authority over the construction process.

## Implications Across Phases

The constructionist position operates differently at each analytical phase:

**Phase 2a (Initial Coding):** Codes are the researcher's constructions, not AI's discoveries. The four evaluation questions — situated meaning or lexical pattern? inductively grounded or training-data derived? honouring participant meaning? what am I noticing that AI is not? — operationalise constructionist epistemology at the level of individual coding decisions.

**Phase 2b (Critical Review):** Review evaluates whether codes reflect genuine interpretive engagement or computational pattern-matching accepted without construction. The "proving the obvious" heuristic is particularly relevant: expected patterns are the ones most likely to have been accepted without adequate constructionist scrutiny.

**Phase 3 (Generating Themes):** Themes are interpretive constructions that organise codes into meaningful patterns. AI's tendency to propose thematic structures that mirror common frameworks in its training data (benefits/barriers, adoption/resistance) directly threatens constructionist integrity. The researcher asks: does this thematic structure reflect how meaning is constructed in *this* data, or does it reflect how AI expects data like this to be organised?

**Phase 4 (Reviewing Themes):** The confirmation bias risk — AI validating its own earlier proposals — is an epistemological concern under constructionism. If meaning is constructed, then a theme validated only by the same computational logic that produced it has not been constructed at all. External challenge, alternative readings, and deliberate attempts to break the thematic structure are epistemologically necessary.

**Phase 5 (Defining and Naming):** Theme names carry epistemological weight. Constructionist theme names should capture the researcher's interpretive construction — what this analysis, with these commitments, in this context, found meaningful. AI-generated theme names tend toward generic descriptive labels that could apply to any analysis of similar data. The constructionist test: does this name reflect a specific interpretive construction, or a default categorisation?

**Phase 6 (Writing):** The written report *is* the analysis. Under constructionism, there is no analysis "behind" the text — the text constructs the analytical argument. AI-assisted writing risks producing fluent prose that describes rather than constructs, that categorises rather than interprets. The researcher's own analytical voice — with its specific texture, commitments, and interpretive depth — is the medium through which constructionist analysis becomes visible.
