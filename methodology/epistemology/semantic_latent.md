# Semantic versus Latent Coding in AI-Augmented RTA

---

## The Principle

Semantic and latent coding are not competing approaches — they operate at different levels of interpretation on the same data. Most reflexive TA uses both, and the same data extract can and should be coded at both levels when both yield analytical insight. The question is not which to use but what balance serves the analysis, and how AI's capabilities differ sharply between the two levels.

Byrne's practice is instructive: "Both semantic and latent coding were utilised. No attempt was made to prioritise semantic coding over latent coding or vice-versa. Rather, semantic codes were produced when meaningful semantic information was interpreted, and latent codes were produced when meaningful latent information was interpreted." The data determines the level, not a predetermined rule.

---

## The Levels

**Semantic coding** captures explicit, surface meanings — what participants said. Codes describe the content as communicated. The interpretive work involves identifying what is meaningful in what was expressed and labelling it accurately. Someone else reading the transcript could point to the specific words or phrases that ground the code.

**Latent coding** goes beneath the surface to identify underlying assumptions, ideas, tensions, or ideological positions that shape or inform the semantic content. Codes interpret what is not explicitly stated but is present in the logic, framing, or implications of what was said. The interpretive leap is greater — another researcher might not identify the same latent pattern, because latent coding involves the researcher's own analytical construction more actively.

**Braun and Clarke's jelly analogy:** If data is an uneven blob of jelly, semantic analysis describes the surface — its form and contours. Latent analysis identifies the features that gave it that particular form — the structural conditions underneath.

---

## The Interpretive Spectrum

The boundary between semantic and latent is not sharp. It is a continuum of interpretive depth:

**Surface semantic:** Cataloguing what was said with minimal interpretation. *"Teacher mentions ChatGPT."* This is description, not yet analysis.

**Deep semantic:** Interpreting the significance of what was said while staying grounded in explicit content. *"Teacher values AI for enabling differentiation they previously could not achieve."* The participant said this — the code captures its meaning faithfully.

**Light latent:** Interpreting underlying meaning that is strongly implied though not explicitly stated. *"Teacher constructs AI as professional enabler — achieving goals previously felt impossible."* The participant did not use these words, but the interpretation is tightly grounded in what they expressed.

**Deep latent:** Identifying ideological assumptions, discursive functions, or structural conditions that participants may not consciously articulate. *"Efficiency discourse naturalises AI adoption as solution to manufactured time scarcity."* This requires significant interpretive work and draws on the researcher's theoretical resources.

Where the researcher's codes fall on this spectrum should cohere with their epistemological commitments. Experiential orientation tends toward semantic-predominant coding with light latent elements. Critical orientation tends toward latent-predominant coding with semantic foundation. Balanced approaches use both levels freely, determined by what each data segment offers.

## Double-Coding: The Core Practice

Byrne demonstrates double-coding with an educator who says: *"If someone's struggling with schoolwork and it's getting them down, it's common sense that determines what we say to them."*

**Semantic code:** "Educators rely on common sense when attending to wellbeing issues" — captures what was said.

**Latent code:** "Common sense inadequate for wellbeing promotion" — interprets what the statement implies about the gap between current practice and what might be needed.

Both codes are analytically productive. The semantic code represents the participant's account. The latent code identifies a tension the participant may not have explicitly articulated. Together, they capture more than either could alone.

Double-coding is not mandatory for every segment — some data is well served by semantic coding alone, other data demands latent interpretation. The researcher's judgement, informed by the analytical goals, determines when each level adds genuine insight versus unnecessary complexity.

---

## AI and the Semantic-Latent Distinction

This is where AI's capabilities and limitations are most sharply differentiated.

**AI is relatively competent at semantic coding.** Identifying explicit content, labelling what was said, and recognising surface-level patterns are tasks well suited to computational text processing. AI can reliably identify that a participant discussed time pressure, expressed positive attitudes toward AI, or described changes in assessment practice. The risk at the semantic level is not that AI gets it wrong but that AI stays at surface description — cataloguing without identifying what is analytically meaningful.

**AI is weak at latent coding.** Identifying underlying assumptions, interpretive tensions, and ideological positions requires contextual knowledge, theoretical sensitivity, and interpretive creativity that AI cannot reliably produce. When AI proposes latent codes, they tend to fall into two patterns:

*Generic theoretical labels.* AI applies familiar analytical concepts from its training data — "professional identity negotiation," "technology anxiety," "institutional resistance" — without grounding them in the specific data. These labels sound analytical but are interchangeable across any similar dataset. They categorise rather than interpret.

*Disguised semantic codes.* AI restates the semantic content in slightly more abstract language and labels it latent. *"Teacher expresses concern about AI"* becomes *"Underlying concern about AI."* The word "underlying" does not make a code latent — it requires genuine interpretive depth.

**The implication:** AI can serve as a useful starting point for semantic coding, where the researcher evaluates and refines AI's proposals. For latent coding, AI's proposals should be treated with particular scepticism. The researcher's own latent interpretations — grounded in contextual knowledge, theoretical sensitivity, and deep data familiarity — are where the analysis does its most distinctive work. This is not a limitation to work around but a feature of the method: latent coding is where the researcher's interpretive contribution is most visible and most valuable.

## The Constructionist Dimension

Under constructionist epistemology, even semantic coding is an act of construction. The researcher does not simply "find" surface meanings — they decide what is meaningful, how to label it, and where segment boundaries fall. Semantic codes are not neutral descriptions but interpretive choices about what matters.

This has a specific implication for AI-augmented work: AI's semantic proposals are not "objective" baselines that the researcher then interprets. They are one set of constructions — shaped by training data patterns — that the researcher evaluates against their own. Accepting an AI semantic code is still an interpretive decision, not a neutral starting point.

Latent coding makes the constructive nature of analysis more visible. The researcher is explicitly constructing interpretations that go beyond what participants said. Under constructionism, this is not a methodological weakness but the point of the analysis — the researcher brings interpretive resources (theoretical knowledge, contextual understanding, reflexive awareness) that enable them to construct meaning that neither the participants nor AI could produce alone.

---

## Coherence Check

The semantic-latent balance completes the four epistemological commitments. The researcher should verify coherence across all four:

**Constructionist + Experiential + Inductive + Semantic-predominant** — the classic experiential RTA. Codes stay close to participant constructions, with latent elements where data clearly warrants deeper interpretation. The analysis represents how participants construct their experiences.

**Constructionist + Critical + Deductive + Latent-predominant** — the classic critical RTA. Codes interrogate discursive mechanisms and ideological assumptions, grounded in semantic foundation. The analysis examines how discourse constructs particular realities.

**Constructionist + Experiential + Theoretically sensitised + Balanced** — a common and coherent middle position. Semantic codes capture what participants express; latent codes interpret significance and underlying tensions. Theory informs attention without determining codes. Both levels serve the analysis depending on what the data offers.

An incoherent combination to watch for: claiming experiential orientation with predominantly latent coding that consistently overrides participant meaning through heavy theoretical interpretation. If the researcher finds their latent codes regularly reframing what participants said in terms participants would not recognise, the analysis has drifted toward critical orientation regardless of what was claimed.

## Implications Across Phases

**Phase 2a (Initial Coding):** The four evaluation questions from Phase 2a apply differently at each level. "Does this code capture situated meaning or lexical pattern?" is primarily a semantic-level question. "What am I noticing that AI is not?" often surfaces latent-level insights — the researcher's contextual knowledge enables interpretive depth that AI cannot reach.

**Phase 2b (Critical Review):** Review should assess whether the semantic-latent balance reflects genuine analytical judgement or default patterns. Common drift: coding becomes predominantly semantic over time as the researcher fatigues and stops doing the harder interpretive work of latent coding. Phase 2b catches this.

**Phase 3 (Generating Themes):** Themes typically integrate both levels — a theme may be grounded in semantic patterns but named and defined through latent interpretation. AI's theme proposals tend to organise semantic content without latent depth. The researcher adds interpretive substance.

**Phase 6 (Writing):** Analytical writing moves between levels. Data extracts provide semantic grounding; the researcher's interpretive prose provides latent depth. AI-assisted writing tends to stay semantic — describing what participants said without interpreting what it means. The researcher's voice carries the latent analytical work.
