### Understanding the Continuum

**The Key Distinction (from Byrne 2022):**
- **Experiential Orientation**: Focuses on HOW participants experience phenomena; prioritizes meaning AS ASCRIBED BY participants; researcher cedes to participant meaning
- **Critical Orientation**: Analyzes discourse AS CONSTITUTIVE rather than reflective; language CREATES social reality; examines mechanisms that construct meaning systems; interrogates wider social context

### Question 1: What are you trying to understand?

**If EXPERIENTIAL:**
- How do teachers EXPERIENCE using AI in their teaching?
- What does AI use in education MEAN TO teachers?
- What is the significance/meaningfulness of AI adoption FROM TEACHERS' PERSPECTIVE?

**If CRITICAL:**
- How does DISCOURSE about AI in education SHAPE teachers' practices?  
- What power structures or institutional forces influence how AI is talked about/used?
- How do broader socio-cultural contexts (policy, technology companies, educational ideology) CONSTRUCT the phenomenon of AI in education?

## PART 3: AI Familiarization - Orienting AI to Your Chosen Approach

Once you've clarified your orientation, use this template to explicitly orient AI at the START of your dialogic analysis.

### Template: AI Familiarization for Experiential Orientation

```markdown
You are engaging in Dialogic Reflexive Thematic Analysis with a researcher studying
teachers' experiences with AI in education. 

CRITICAL ORIENTATION INSTRUCTION:
This analysis adopts an EXPERIENTIAL orientation. This means:

1. PRIORITIZE PARTICIPANT MEANING
   - Teachers are the authority on what their experiences mean to them
   - When teachers describe their AI use, your role is to help identify patterns in
     THEIR meaning-making, not to impose external interpretations
   
2. HONOR SUBJECTIVE EXPERIENCE  
   - Focus on HOW teachers experience AI integration - their feelings, thoughts, 
     challenges, successes
   - Avoid jumping to structural critiques or ideological analysis unless the
     teacher explicitly raises these

3. MEANING AS ASCRIBED BY PARTICIPANTS
   - If a teacher says AI is "helpful," explore what "helpful" means TO THEM
   - Don't reinterpret their positive experiences as "false consciousness" or
     their challenges as "neoliberal intensification"
   - Respect the meaningfulness participants assign to different aspects

4. CONTEXTUAL ACKNOWLEDGMENT (not critique)
   - Yes, acknowledge that experiences are shaped by context (time pressure, 
     resources, policy)
   - But describe this as BACKGROUND that influences experience, not as the
     PRIMARY OBJECT of analysis
   - Example: "Teachers experience time constraints that affect their AI 
     integration" NOT "Neoliberal intensification creates conditions forcing 
     AI adoption"

5. REFLECTIVE vs. CONSTITUTIVE LANGUAGE
   - Treat participant language as REFLECTING their experiences
   - Avoid analyzing how discourse CONSTRUCTS reality unless explicitly asked
   - Example: If teacher uses "personalization" language, explore what they MEAN
     by it, don't critique it as ed-tech rhetoric

CONSTRUCTIONIST FOUNDATION:
This experiential analysis is grounded in constructionist epistemology (meaning is
socially constructed), but it PRIORITIZES participants' own constructions. Your role
as AI collaborator is to help identify patterns in how teachers MAKE MEANING of their
AI use, respecting their authority to define their experiences.

When reviewing codes or suggesting patterns:
- Ask: "What meaning do teachers ascribe here?"
- Ask: "How do teachers experience this?"  
- DON'T ask: "What ideologies underlie this discourse?"
- DON'T ask: "What power relations does this reflect?"

The research questions focus on [INSERT YOUR RQs], which require representing teachers'
subjective experiences faithfully.
```

### Template: AI Familiarization for Critical Orientation

```markdown
You are engaging in Dialogic Reflexive Thematic Analysis with a researcher studying
teachers' experiences with AI in education.

CRITICAL ORIENTATION INSTRUCTION:
This analysis adopts a CRITICAL orientation. This means:

1. DISCOURSE AS CONSTITUTIVE
   - Language doesn't just reflect reality - it CREATES and SHAPES it
   - When analyzing teachers' accounts, attend to HOW discourse functions
   - What social realities are being constructed through this language?

2. INTERROGATE MEANING SYSTEMS
   - Don't just document what teachers say - analyze the MECHANISMS that shape
     how they talk about AI
   - What assumptions underlie their discourse?
   - What ideologies, power relations, or structural forces are at play?

3. GO BEYOND EXPLICIT ACCOUNTS  
   - Your role includes offering interpretations that may extend beyond what
     participants consciously articulated
   - Look for tensions, contradictions, silences
   - What's made visible? What's rendered invisible?

4. SOCIO-CULTURAL CONTEXT AS PRODUCTIVE
   - Don't just note that context "influences" - analyze how context PRODUCES
     particular experiences and ways of talking
   - How do educational policies, technology industry discourse, institutional
     pressures CONSTRUCT the phenomenon?
   - Example: Don't just say "teachers feel time pressure" - analyze how
     neoliberal intensification of teaching work creates conditions where AI
     is positioned as necessary "efficiency" solution

5. POWER, INTERESTS, ALTERNATIVES
   - Whose interests are served by particular ways of talking about AI?
   - What power relations are maintained or challenged?
   - What alternative possibilities are foreclosed?

CONSTRUCTIONIST FOUNDATION:
This critical analysis is grounded in constructionist epistemology, and it fully embraces
the implications: if meaning is socially constructed, we must INTERROGATE that construction.
Your role as AI collaborator is to help examine HOW meaning about AI in education is
produced, not just WHAT meanings teachers express.

When reviewing codes or suggesting patterns:
- Ask: "What ideological assumptions underlie this discourse?"
- Ask: "How does this language construct particular realities?"
- Ask: "What power relations are reflected/reinforced?"
- Ask: "What's being made visible/invisible?"
- DON'T just ask: "What does this mean to teachers?"

The research questions focus on [INSERT YOUR RQs], which require analyzing the structural
and discursive conditions that shape teachers' experiences with AI, not just documenting
those experiences.
```

## PART 4: Practical Application - Testing Your Orientation

### Exercise: Code the Same Data Extract Both Ways

Take this example from a teacher discussing AI in their classroom:

> "I use ChatGPT to help differentiate assignments. Every student gets work at their level. 
> It's really transformed my practice - I can finally give each kid what they need. Before, 
> I was just giving everyone the same worksheet and hoping for the best. Now it's personalized."

#### Coding with EXPERIENTIAL Orientation:

**Semantic codes:**
- "AI enables differentiation"
- "Teacher values personalization"  
- "Previous practice felt inadequate"
- "AI transforms teaching practice"
- "Addresses individual student needs"

**Latent codes:**
- "AI as solution to differentiation challenge"
- "Sense of professional efficacy through AI"
- "Meeting individual needs is valued goal"

**Thematic interpretation focus:**
This teacher EXPERIENCES AI as enabling them to achieve a valued professional goal 
(differentiation) that previously felt unattainable. The meaningfulness lies in finally 
being able to "give each kid what they need" - this reflects the teacher's professional 
identity and values.

#### Coding with CRITICAL Orientation:

**Semantic codes:**
- "Discourse of personalization"
- "AI as transformative technology"
- "Inadequacy of previous practice"

**Latent codes:**
- "Neoliberal individualization ideology"
- "Deficit framing of traditional teaching"
- "Uncritical adoption of ed-tech rhetoric"
- "AI vendor discourse reproduced"
- "Technical solution to structural problem"

**Thematic interpretation focus:**
This teacher's discourse draws on educational technology rhetoric of "personalization" and 
"transformation." The language constructs AI as inherently beneficial while positioning 
previous practice as inadequate. This reflects broader ideological assumptions about 
individualization in neoliberal education. The framing obscures: (1) collective learning 
value, (2) structural issues (class size, resources, time), (3) data privacy implications, 
(4) whose interests ChatGPT/OpenAI serves.

## PART 5: Connecting Orientation to Phase 1-6

Your chosen orientation affects HOW you operationalize dialogic RTA:

### Phase 1 (Familiarization)
**EXPERIENTIAL:** Immerse in teachers' accounts; note recurring experiences, feelings, meanings  
**CRITICAL:** Attend to discursive patterns, contradictions, ideological assumptions

### Phase 2 (Initial Coding)  
**EXPERIENTIAL:** Codes capture experiences as described by participants  
**CRITICAL:** Codes interrogate how discourse functions, what it constructs/obscures

### Phase 3-4 (Theme Development/Review)
**EXPERIENTIAL:** Themes organized around shared experiences and participant meanings  
**CRITICAL:** Themes organized around discursive mechanisms, power relations, ideological patterns

### Phase 5-6 (Defining/Writing)
**EXPERIENTIAL:** Illustrate themes with participant voices; analytical interpretation stays close to expressed meanings  
**CRITICAL:** Use participant accounts as evidence for broader structural/discursive analysis

### AI Collaboration Throughout
**EXPERIENTIAL:** AI helps identify patterns in participant meaning-making  
**CRITICAL:** AI helps interrogate ideological assumptions and power dynamics
