# Experiential versus Critical Orientation in Dialogic RTA

---

## The Distinction

Orientation determines what the researcher does with participant accounts — whether they are treated as windows into experience or as discourse to be interrogated. This choice shapes what the analysis can say and what kind of knowledge it produces.

**Experiential orientation:** The researcher prioritises how participants experience the phenomenon under study. Participant accounts are treated as meaningful expressions of lived experience. The researcher cedes interpretive authority to participants regarding what their experiences mean to them, while still actively constructing the analysis. The question is: *what does this mean to them?*

**Critical orientation:** The researcher treats discourse as constitutive rather than reflective. Participant accounts are not transparent reports of experience but discursive constructions shaped by power relations, institutional forces, and ideological positions. The researcher interrogates what accounts do — what they make visible, what they obscure, whose interests they serve. The question is: *what does this discourse construct, and how?*

Most analyses position themselves primarily toward one end while acknowledging elements of the other. The choice is not binary but directional, and it must cohere with the epistemological position established in the constructionist framework.

---

## What Each Orientation Sees

The same data extract produces different analytical objects depending on orientation. Consider a teacher who says: *"AI helps me personalise learning for each student. It's really transformed my practice."*

**Experiential reading:** This teacher values AI for enabling differentiation — a professional goal they find meaningful. They experience AI as transformative. The analysis represents this experience, explores what "personalisation" and "transformation" mean within the teacher's professional world, and examines how this experience relates to other teachers' accounts.

**Critical reading:** This teacher's discourse draws on educational technology rhetoric of "personalisation" and "transformation." The language constructs AI as inherently beneficial while positioning previous practice as inadequate. The analysis examines what ideological assumptions structure this framing — individualisation, efficiency, technological progress — and what alternatives it renders invisible.

Neither reading is wrong. They produce different knowledge. The experiential reading tells us how teachers live with AI. The critical reading tells us how discourse about AI structures what it is possible to think and say. The researcher must decide which knowledge their research questions require.

## Experiential Orientation: Depth and Limits

An experiential orientation centres participant meaning-making. Under constructionist epistemology, this does not mean treating accounts as transparent windows into objective experience — it means treating participants' constructions of their experience as the primary analytical object.

**What it does well:** Experiential analysis produces rich accounts of how a phenomenon is lived. It captures the textures of experience — ambivalence, contradiction, meaning, struggle — in ways that honour participant voice. For understudied phenomena, where the field needs to understand how something is experienced before it can theorise why, experiential work provides essential groundwork.

**What it cannot do:** Experiential analysis does not interrogate the conditions that produce experience. It can describe that teachers feel overwhelmed by AI integration, but it does not examine how institutional pressures, policy discourses, or market forces construct that overwhelm. It represents what participants say without asking what makes it possible — or impossible — to say certain things.

**The experiential-constructionist coherence:** Constructionism holds that meaning is socially constructed. An experiential orientation within constructionism prioritises *participants'* constructions — taking their meaning-making seriously as the object of analysis. This is coherent: the researcher studies how participants construct meaning, not what objective reality looks like. The constructionist dimension means the researcher recognises these constructions as situated and contingent, even while representing them faithfully.

---

## Critical Orientation: Depth and Limits

A critical orientation treats discourse as productive — not just describing reality but actively constructing it. The analytical interest is in mechanisms: how language, institutional contexts, and power relations produce particular ways of understanding.

**What it does well:** Critical analysis reveals what experiential analysis takes for granted. It identifies ideological assumptions, examines whose interests particular framings serve, and makes visible the structural conditions that shape individual experience. It connects micro-level accounts to macro-level forces.

**What it cannot do:** Critical analysis risks overriding participant meaning. When a teacher says AI is helpful and the analysis reframes this as "reproduction of techno-solutionist discourse," something of the participant's own understanding is lost. Critical orientation must guard against treating participants as cultural dupes who cannot see what the analyst sees.

**The critical-constructionist coherence:** If meaning is socially constructed, examining the mechanisms of that construction is a natural analytical move. Critical orientation fully embraces the constructionist premise — it does not just acknowledge that meaning is constructed but makes the construction process itself the object of analysis.

## Orientation and AI: Specific Implications

The choice of orientation has direct consequences for how AI functions as analytical partner.

**Under experiential orientation**, AI's tendency to categorise and abstract works against the analytical goal. Experiential analysis needs texture, specificity, and fidelity to participant meaning. AI tends to flatten individual accounts into generic categories — "teachers express concerns about workload" — where the experiential analyst needs to preserve *how* each teacher constructs their specific experience of workload. The researcher's corrective role is to push back toward specificity and situated meaning whenever AI generalises.

**Under critical orientation**, AI's tendency toward surface-level description works against the analytical goal differently. Critical analysis needs interpretive depth — identifying ideological assumptions, discursive mechanisms, and power relations. AI tends to describe what participants said rather than interrogate what the discourse does. However, AI can be useful as a first-pass identifier of discursive patterns (recurring metaphors, framing devices, lexical choices) that the researcher then interprets critically.

**Both orientations face the same core risk:** AI produces analysis that looks like the chosen orientation without doing its actual work. AI can produce text that sounds experiential ("teachers experience AI as transformative") without genuine engagement with lived meaning, or text that sounds critical ("this reflects neoliberal efficiency discourse") without genuine interrogation of discursive mechanisms. The orientation label without the analytical substance is precisely the kind of "proving the obvious" the methodology is designed to prevent.

---

## Hybrid Positions

Most analyses are not purely experiential or purely critical. A predominantly experiential analysis may note structural conditions shaping experience without making them the primary analytical object. A predominantly critical analysis may ground its discourse analysis in careful representation of what participants actually said.

The key is clarity about what predominates and why. An experiential analysis that occasionally notes institutional pressures is methodologically coherent. An analysis that claims experiential orientation but consistently reinterprets participant meaning through critical theoretical frameworks is not — it has a critical orientation that has not been acknowledged.

The researcher should be able to articulate: *My analysis primarily asks [experiential question / critical question], and when elements of [the other orientation] appear, they serve [specific analytical purpose] rather than displacing the primary orientation.*

---

## Orientation Across Phases

**Phase 2a–2b (Coding):** Experiential coding stays close to participant language, capturing expressed meaning. Critical coding may use more theoretical language, identifying discursive functions and ideological positions. Both are valid — the orientation determines which register predominates.

**Phase 3–4 (Themes):** Experiential themes are organised around shared experiences and participant meanings ("Teachers experience AI as both threat and opportunity"). Critical themes are organised around discursive mechanisms and structural patterns ("Efficiency discourse positions AI adoption as inevitable"). The researcher ensures theme structure reflects the chosen orientation rather than defaulting to whatever structure AI proposes.

**Phase 5–6 (Defining and Writing):** Experiential writing foregrounds participant voice, with data extracts carrying significant weight. Critical writing foregrounds analytical argument, with data extracts serving as evidence for discursive claims. In both cases, the written text must do the analytical work the orientation promises — not merely label itself as experiential or critical.
