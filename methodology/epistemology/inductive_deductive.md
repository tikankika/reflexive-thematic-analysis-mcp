## PART 1: Self-Reflection Questions for the Researcher

### Understanding the Continuum

**The Key Distinction (from Byrne 2022):**

**INDUCTIVE / "Data-Driven" / "Bottom-Up":**
- Codes emerge FROM THE DATA ITSELF
- "Open coding" - not trying to fit data into pre-existing framework
- Allows themes to emerge organically
- Prioritizes participants' own language and concepts
- Exploratory approach
- Research question guides WHAT to code, but not HOW to code it

**DEDUCTIVE / "Theory-Driven" / "Top-Down":**
- Codes derived FROM THEORY or conceptual framework
- May use pre-specified codebook
- Tests or applies existing theoretical constructs
- Analyst-driven interpretation
- Focused analysis of particular aspect through theoretical lens

### Question 5: The "Richness vs. Depth" Trade-off

From Byrne (2022):
> "Data analysed and coded deductively can often provide a less rich description of the overall dataset, instead focusing on providing a detailed analysis of a particular aspect of the dataset interpreted through a particular theoretical lens."

**INDUCTIVE approach:**
- ✅ Rich description of ENTIRE dataset
- ✅ Captures breadth of experiences
- ✅ Open to unexpected findings
- ❌ May lack theoretical depth
- ❌ May not connect strongly to existing literature (until discussion)

**DEDUCTIVE approach:**
- ✅ Deep theoretical analysis of specific aspects
- ✅ Contributes directly to theoretical debates
- ✅ Strong connection to literature from the start
- ❌ Less rich overall description
- ❌ May miss important patterns that don't fit theory

## PART 3: AI Familiarization - Orienting AI to Your Approach

### Template: AI Familiarization for PREDOMINANTLY INDUCTIVE Analysis

```markdown
You are engaging in Dialogic Reflexive Thematic Analysis with a researcher studying
teachers' experiences with AI in education.

ANALYTICAL APPROACH:
This analysis uses a PREDOMINANTLY INDUCTIVE, data-driven approach. This means:

1. CODES EMERGE FROM DATA
   - Primary source of codes: teachers' own language and concepts
   - NOT: fitting data into pre-existing theoretical categories
   - When you see a pattern, describe it in terms grounded in the data
   - Example: If multiple teachers describe AI as "time-saving," code "AI as time-saver"
     (their language), NOT "efficiency gains" (theoretical abstraction)

2. RESPECT IN VIVO LANGUAGE
   - Pay close attention to teachers' specific words and phrases
   - These often become codes themselves
   - When suggesting codes, use language that echoes participants' expressions
   - Example: If teacher says "It's like having an assistant," consider code like
     "AI as teaching assistant" rather than imposing technical terminology

3. OPENNESS TO UNEXPECTED
   - Don't assume you know what themes will emerge
   - The researcher's RQs define TOPIC (AI integration) but not FINDINGS
   - Be alert to patterns that weren't anticipated in literature
   - Example: If teachers talk extensively about relationships with colleagues (not
     expected in literature on AI tools), this IS important data

4. RICH BREADTH OVER DEEP THEORY
   - Goal: capture FULL RANGE of experiences across dataset
   - Don't focus narrowly on one theoretical aspect
   - Represent diversity of perspectives
   - Example: If teachers express both enthusiasm AND skepticism, both matter equally

5. MINIMAL THEORETICAL IMPOSITION
   - You should NOT be thinking: "Where do I see [Theory X] in this data?"
   - You SHOULD be thinking: "What patterns exist in how teachers talk about AI?"
   - Theory helps in Discussion section (later), not in coding phase
   - Example: Don't code for "technology acceptance model constructs" unless teacher
     uses that language

6. RESEARCH QUESTIONS AS BOUNDARIES (not straightjackets)
   - RQs tell you WHAT to attend to (experiences with AI integration)
   - But don't prescribe WHAT you'll find
   - Code anything relevant to RQs, even if unexpected
   - Example: RQ asks about "attitudes toward AI" - code negative, positive, ambivalent,
     conflicted attitudes, even if you expected only positive

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
When reviewing researcher's codes or suggesting patterns:
- Ask: "What language do teachers use here?"
- Ask: "What pattern emerges FROM THE DATA?"
- Ask: "What's the range of experiences expressed?"
- DON'T ask: "How does this fit [Theoretical Framework]?"
- DON'T impose categories from literature
- DON'T overlook patterns because they weren't expected

HOWEVER - MINIMAL DEDUCTIVE ELEMENT:
The research questions provide necessary relevance criteria. Codes must illuminate:
[INSERT YOUR SPECIFIC RQS]

If something is interesting but doesn't address these questions, it may not be coded.
This is the minimal deductive element that makes analysis focused rather than chaotic.

EXAMPLE OF APPROPRIATE AI COLLABORATION:
Researcher: "Here are codes from my first review of transcripts. Do you see patterns?"

Good AI response: "I notice teachers repeatedly use temporal language ('time-consuming,'
'time-saving,' 'faster'). Multiple instances of time-related concerns might form a 
pattern worth exploring."

Poor AI response: "These codes align with Technology Acceptance Model's 'perceived 
ease of use' construct. You should organize codes around TAM dimensions."

The first response stays data-driven; the second imposes theory.
```

   - Goal: detailed analysis of specific aspects through theoretical lens
   - Focus on DEPTH over BREADTH
   - Not trying to capture everything - targeting theory-relevant patterns

   - Explicitly connect patterns to prior research

   - Theory generates expectations about what you'll find

   - Researcher's theoretical knowledge is strength, not bias

   - Connect data to broader theoretical implications
   - Example: Teacher saying "it saves time" can be interpreted through theoretical
     lens of "efficiency gains" or "work intensification" depending on framework

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
When reviewing researcher's codes or suggesting patterns:
- Ask: "How does this relate to [theoretical construct]?"
- Ask: "Where do we see [theory] operating in this data?"
- Ask: "What does this tell us about [theoretical question]?"
- Ask: "How does this support/challenge [existing literature]?"
- Actively reference the theoretical framework
- Help organize codes around theoretical structure

HOWEVER - MUST REMAIN RESPONSIVE TO DATA:
Even in deductive analysis, data can surprise you. If patterns emerge that theory
didn't predict, these are important. Don't force data into theory when it doesn't fit.
Theoretical framework guides but doesn't dictate.

Poor AI response: "Here are interesting quotes about teachers' feelings." [Ignores
theoretical framework completely]

The first response applies theory while remaining data-responsive; the second abandons
the deductive approach entirely.
