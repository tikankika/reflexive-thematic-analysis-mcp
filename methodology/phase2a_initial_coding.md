## Grounding Phase 2 in Your Theoretical Framework

Before beginning the practical work of coding, it's essential to understand how your theoretical assumptions from Section 3.1 will actively shape your coding decisions. Byrne (2022, p. 1395) emphasizes that these theoretical continua aren't just abstract positions to declare—they fundamentally determine *how* you interpret and code your data.

### How Constructionist Epistemology Shapes Your Coding

Your constructionist position means that meaning is socially produced and reproduced through language—not simply reflected by it. This has direct implications for coding:

**Meaningfulness matters more than frequency.** As Braun and Clarke (2012, p. 37) illustrate: if researchers interview white-collar workers about their workday and most report starting work at 9:00 a.m., this would be a pattern in the data, but not necessarily a meaningful or important one. The criterion for noteworthy codes isn't just recurrence—it's meaningfulness in relation to your research questions.

**Degrees of conviction matter.** There may be varying degrees of conviction in how participants express different issues, which helps you identify the salience and meaningfulness of different patterns. One statement made with strong emotion or hesitation may be more significant than something mentioned repeatedly but casually.

**Context shapes meaning.** A constructionist approach means attending to how language constructs meaning in context, not just what words are used. The same words can mean different things depending on how they're said, who's saying them, and what surrounds them.

For your focus group data, this means paying attention not just to what teachers say about AI in education, but how they construct their relationship to it through their language—the hesitations, the qualifications, the contradictions.

### How Experiential Orientation Shapes Your Coding

Your experiential orientation means prioritizing participants' own meaning-making and lived experiences. As Byrne (2022, p. 1396) notes, "the researcher would cede to the meaning and meaningfulness ascribed by the participant."

**This means**:
- Asking "what does THIS participant think this means?" rather than imposing external theoretical interpretations
- Focusing on participants' accounts of their experiences (the "meanings made") rather than analyzing the socio-cultural mechanisms that produce those meanings (the "meaning making")
- Honoring participant voice even when it conflicts with your own interpretations or theoretical expectations

In your research, this means that when teachers talk about their experiences with AI tools, you're interested in *how they experience this*, *what it means to them*, *how it affects their practice from their perspective*—not in imposing a critical analysis of power structures or policy frameworks (unless participants themselves explicitly make those connections).

### How Predominantly Inductive Approach Shapes Your Coding

Byrne adopted "a predominantly inductive approach...meaning data was open-coded and respondent/data-based meanings were emphasized" (2022, p. 1397). Note the word *predominantly*—as Braun and Clarke clarify, coding rarely falls cleanly into purely inductive or deductive, and usually uses a combination of both.

**What "predominantly inductive" means practically**:

You're not starting with a predetermined list of codes or theoretical framework to apply to the data. Instead, you're open-coding—allowing codes to emerge from what participants actually say and how they say it. Your codes should be grounded in participants' language and concerns, not in theoretical categories you bring to the data.

However, some deductive element is inevitable and appropriate. Your research questions provide a frame for what's relevant to code. You need some criteria to identify whether information might be conducive to addressing your research questions. As Braun and Clarke note, it's arguably not possible to conduct an exclusively inductive analysis.

For your project, this means letting participants' own framings and concerns guide your initial coding, while ensuring those codes will ultimately help you answer your research questions about teachers' AI usage in education.

### How Semantic and Latent Coding Work Together

Byrne (2022, p. 1397-1398) used both semantic and latent coding, with no attempt to prioritize one over the other. This is a key aspect of reflexive TA that's often misunderstood.

**Semantic codes** identify explicit, surface meanings—what the participant directly said. The production of semantic codes is descriptive: you're presenting the content of the data as communicated by the participant.

**Latent codes** go beyond the descriptive level to identify underlying meanings, assumptions, ideas, or ideologies that may shape or inform what participants say. This becomes interpretive—you're actively construing deeper levels of meaning.

**Critically**: The same piece of data can be coded both semantically AND latently.

Byrne provides this excellent example (p. 1402):

> One participant stated: "...if someone's struggling a bit with their schoolwork and it's getting them down a bit, it's common sense that determines what we say to them or how we approach them. And it might help to talk, but I don't know that it has a lasting effect"

**Semantic code**: "educators rely on common sense when attending to wellbeing issues"
**Latent code**: "common sense inadequate for wellbeing promotion"

The semantic code describes what the participant explicitly said. The latent code interprets what this might mean—the implicit acknowledgment that common sense approaches might not be sufficient.

For your data, you might code semantically what teachers say about AI tools, while also coding latently the underlying anxieties, assumptions, or ideologies that seem to inform how they talk about AI.

### What Makes a "Good" Code in Reflexive TA?

Drawing from Byrne's worked example, good codes have several characteristics:

**Codes should be brief but sufficiently detailed.** They're succinct shorthand labels, but with enough detail to stand alone without needing to refer back to the data extract. 

Byrne (p. 1399-1400) provides examples of poor vs. good codes:

**Poor code**: "the wellbeing guidelines are not relatable"
- This lacks context. Not relatable to whom? 

**Better code**: "the wellbeing guidelines are not relatable for students"
- Now it's clear who finds them unrelatable and the code can stand alone.

**Poor code**: "positivity regarding the wellbeing guidelines" 
- Unclear whose positivity—the participant's? Their colleagues'? Students'?

**Better codes** (after revision in later iterations):
- "student positivity regarding the wellbeing curriculum"
- "educator positivity regarding the wellbeing curriculum"

These revised codes are specific and clear about perspective.

**Codes should capture underlying commonality.** A code should represent the pattern that links different data items together—what's the common thread you're identifying?

**Codes should be relevant to your research questions.** Not everything interesting in your data needs to be coded—only what helps answer your research questions.

**Codes should apply to multiple data items.** As Braun and Clarke note, codes should pertain to more than one data item. If a code only applies to a single data extract, it's too specific (though that data might be subsumed under a broader code).

However, don't confuse "applies to multiple items" with "appears frequently." Remember your constructionist epistemology—meaningfulness matters more than frequency.

## The Recursive Nature of Coding in RTA

Before discussing practical approaches, it's crucial to understand that coding in reflexive TA is fundamentally recursive and iterative. Byrne emphasizes this repeatedly:

> "RTA is a recursive process and it is rare that a researcher would follow a linear path through the six phases... it is very common for the researcher to follow a particular train of thought when coding, only to encounter an impasse where several different interpretations of the data come to light" (p. 1400)

Your codes WILL evolve. You WILL return to earlier transcripts and recode them based on new understandings. You WILL collapse some codes together, split others apart, and completely reconceptualize some patterns. This is not a failure of method—this IS the method.

Byrne tracked his code evolution across multiple iterations using a spreadsheet (Table 1, p. 1401). He provides examples of how codes changed:

**Data item one**: Initially coded as "non-wellbeing educator received no wellbeing CPD" → Later revised to broader "insufficient training in wellbeing curriculum" when the specific distinction became unnecessary.

**Data item three**: Initially coded descriptively as "no assessment of students' wellbeing" → Revised to more interpretive "lack of clarity in assessing student wellbeing" after re-evaluation.

**Data item five**: Initially coded very specifically "training in attending to students' day-by-day wellbeing required" → Revised to subsumed under broader existing code "more training is needed for wellbeing promotion" because the specific code only applied to one data item.

The key is tracking and documenting these changes so your analytical process remains transparent.

## Two Viable Approaches to Initial Coding in AI-Augmented RTA

The emerging literature on AI-augmented qualitative research (Naeem et al. 2025, NguyenTrung 2025) suggests two viable approaches to initial coding, both of which can maintain the reflexive principles of Braun and Clarke's RTA if implemented thoughtfully:

**Approach A: Human-First Coding** (traditional Braun & Clarke)
**Approach B: AI-First Coding** (emerging AI-augmented approach)

The key distinction is the *sequencing* of human and AI engagement, but both require human interpretive authority, critical reflexivity, and theoretical grounding.

Your successful test with the MÅG transcript suggests AI-first might work well for your project, but understanding both approaches helps you make an informed choice and implement either effectively.

## Approach A: Human-First Coding (Traditional RTA)

This is Byrne's approach and the standard Braun & Clarke method. You do the initial coding yourself, then potentially use AI for refinement and checking.

### Step 1: Systematic Human Coding

**The basic process**: Work through your transcript systematically, attending to each section with equal consideration, and identifying aspects that are interesting and potentially informative for your research questions.

Byrne describes his process (p. 1399): "The preliminary iteration of coding was conducted using the 'comments' function in Microsoft Word (2016). This allowed codes to be noted in the side margin, while also highlighting the area of text assigned to each respective code."

For your project, you've found a method that works well—coding directly in the markdown transcript using hashtags. For example:

```markdown
[Transcript section in Swedish]
#misstanke_om_äkthet (teachers question whether AI-generated text is authentic)
#"skrivbordsprodukt" (in vivo code - participant's exact phrase)
#oro_för_kvalitet (concern about quality)
```

**Your proven coding conventions**:
- Swedish language codes (preserves meaning)
- Hashtags for easy identification: #code_name
- Underscores for multi-word codes: #work_life_balance
- In vivo codes in quotes: #"participant's exact words"
- Brief analytical comments in parentheses after code
- Both semantic and latent codes where appropriate

**The systematic nature is important**: Don't skip sections that seem less interesting initially. Don't be selective. Work through the entire transcript. You'll likely find that your understanding evolves, and sections that seemed unimportant on first reading become more relevant later.

**How many codes?** There's no prescribed number. Braun and Clarke emphasize you should code liberally at this stage—you'll consolidate and refine later. What matters is achieving sufficient depth to examine patterns in the data and diversity of participants' positions.

**Semantic and latent coding**: Code at both levels when meaningful. Don't force every data item to be coded both ways, but be open to it. Sometimes the semantic level is most informative, sometimes latent interpretation adds crucial depth, often both matter.

### Step 2: Multiple Iterations

This initial coding is just the first pass. Byrne was explicit about multiple iterations being necessary.

**Minimum three passes recommended**:

**First iteration**: Initial open coding
- Approach with fresh eyes
- Code liberally, inductively
- Preserve participants' language (in vivo codes)
- Don't worry about refinement yet

**Second iteration**: Consolidation and refinement
- Look for codes that are essentially capturing the same pattern (collapse them)
- Check consistency—are you applying codes consistently across the transcript?
- Clarify code definitions
- Look for patterns you might have missed

**Third iteration**: Quality and theory check
- Do codes align with your theoretical position?
- Is there appropriate semantic/latent balance?
- Are codes relevant to research questions?
- Do codes stand alone clearly?

Byrne tracked each iteration in a spreadsheet, documenting what changed and why. This transparency is important—it shows your analytical reasoning and provides "waypoints" if you need to backtrack.

### Step 3: Using AI for Refinement (Optional but Useful)

After your human coding is substantially complete, AI can serve as a reflexive tool to check your work and push your thinking further. This is different from AI-first coding—here, AI is scaffolding your analysis, not generating initial codes.

**Potential AI scaffolding uses**:

**Gap identification**: Ask AI to identify aspects or patterns you might have missed
```
I have coded this focus group transcript focusing on teachers' experiences with AI tools. 
Here are my main codes: [list your codes]

Based on the transcript, are there patterns, perspectives, or aspects I may have overlooked? 
Consider:
- Different viewpoints expressed by different participants
- Implicit assumptions underlying what people say
- Tensions or contradictions
- Contextual factors that might be important
```

**Code clarity check**: Ask AI to assess whether your codes are clear and informative
```
Review these codes for clarity and whether they can stand alone:

Code: #misstanke_om_äkthet
Associated data: [relevant quotes]

Questions:
- Is what this code captures clear without seeing the data?
- Is it too broad or too narrow?
- Does it capture the underlying pattern effectively?
```

**Consistency verification**: Ask AI to help identify if similar codes should be collapsed or kept separate
```
I have these codes that seem related:
- #tidsbrist (time shortage)
- #för_lite_tid (too little time)  
- #press_på_lärare (pressure on teachers)

Should these be:
1. Kept separate (capturing genuinely different patterns)
2. Collapsed into one (same underlying pattern)
3. Organized hierarchically (one is subcategory of another)
```

**Latent dimension exploration**: Ask AI to help explore potential deeper meanings
```
I have this semantic code: #lärare_använder_AI_för_administrativt
(teachers use AI for administrative tasks)

Help me explore potential latent meanings:
- What might this pattern reveal about how teachers are constructing their relationship to AI?
- What assumptions might underlie this usage pattern?
- What is not being said explicitly but might be implied?
```

**Critical point**: DO NOT passively accept AI suggestions. Each suggestion requires your critical evaluation:
- Does this align with my theoretical position?
- Is this honoring participant meaning or imposing external framework?
- Is this supported by the actual data or is AI over-interpreting?
- Does this capture something I genuinely see in the data or is it leading me astray?

Document your decisions—which suggestions you accepted, modified, or rejected and why. This reflexive documentation is crucial.

## Approach B: AI-First Coding

This is the approach your test suggested might work well. The key insight from emerging literature (NguyenTrung 2025, Naeem et al. 2025) is that AI-first coding doesn't reduce reflexivity IF you engage in rigorous critical evaluation of every AI-generated code.

In fact, AI-first can potentially *enhance* reflexivity by making your interpretive decisions more explicit. When you accept, modify, or reject each AI suggestion, you're articulating your interpretive reasoning in a way that purely human coding might not require you to do as explicitly.

### Why AI-First Can Work for Your Project

**Your specific context suggests AI-first is viable**:
- Large dataset (three focus groups)
- You've completed thorough Phase 1 familiarization with your data
- You have strong theoretical grounding to guide evaluation
- Your initial test with MÅG showed promising results
- Whole-transcript systematic coverage is valuable for focus group data
- Efficiency matters given your project scope

**The theoretical justification**: AI-first isn't abandoning human interpretation—it's resequencing it. Instead of human interpretation happening during initial code generation, it happens during critical evaluation of AI codes. The interpretive work is the same (or arguably more explicit), just positioned differently in the process.

### Step 1: Setting Up AI for Theoretically-Grounded Coding

Before AI codes anything, you need to orient it to your theoretical framework. This isn't just prompt engineering—it's ensuring AI's initial codes will be shaped by your epistemological and methodological commitments.

**Provide AI with**:

**Your epistemological position**:
```
I'm conducting reflexive thematic analysis from a constructionist epistemology:
- Meaning is socially constructed through language, not just reflected by it
- Meaningfulness matters more than frequency  
- Context shapes meaning
- Language actively constructs reality
```

**Your methodological orientation**:
```
Experiential orientation:
- Prioritize participants' own meaning-making
- Focus on lived experience from their perspective
- Cede to participants' interpretations
- Avoid imposing external critical frameworks

Predominantly inductive approach:
- Data-driven, open-coding
- Let patterns emerge from participants' language
- Research questions provide relevance frame but not predetermined codes
```

**Your coding approach**:
```
Code both semantic and latent levels:
- Semantic: explicit, surface meanings
- Latent: underlying assumptions, ideologies, deeper meanings
- Same data can be coded both ways

Language and format:
- Codes in Swedish (preserve participant meaning)
- Hashtag format: #code_name
- Multi-word codes with underscores: #work_life_balance
- In vivo codes in quotes: #"participant's exact phrase"
```

**Your research questions**: 
[Provide your actual RQs]

**Context about the data**:
```
This is a focus group transcript with [X] teachers from [context] discussing [topic].
Key contextual information: [relevant background]
```

This thorough orientation helps ensure AI's initial codes will be more aligned with your theoretical and methodological commitments.

### Step 2: AI Generates Systematic Initial Codes

Have AI code the entire transcript systematically, generating both semantic and latent codes where appropriate, and preserving in vivo quotes.

Your prompt might look like:

```
Using the theoretical framework and coding approach I've outlined, please systematically code this focus group transcript. For each section:

1. Identify semantic codes (explicit meanings)
2. Identify latent codes where deeper meanings/assumptions are evident
3. Preserve in vivo codes (participants' exact meaningful phrases)
4. Provide brief rationale for each code

Format:
[Transcript section]

CODES:
#code_name (semantic): Brief explanation
#→latent_code (latent): Underlying meaning/assumption
#"in_vivo" (in vivo): Participant's exact phrase

RATIONALE: Why these codes capture meaning

[Continue through entire transcript]
```

The goal is systematic coverage—AI codes the entire transcript, not just sections you'd notice yourself. This can reveal patterns you might have missed.

### Step 3: Critical Human Evaluation (THE ESSENTIAL WORK)

This is where human interpretive authority is exercised. You're not passively accepting AI codes—you're critically evaluating EVERY code.

**For each code, ask yourself**:

**Theoretical alignment**:
- Does this code align with constructionist epistemology?
- Does it prioritize participant meaning (experiential orientation)?
- Is it inductively derived from data or is AI imposing a framework?
- Is the semantic/latent distinction appropriate?

**Data fidelity**:
- Is this actually supported by what participants said?
- Or is AI hallucinating, over-interpreting, or missing context?
- Does this capture participants' language and meaning?
- Is the surrounding context properly considered?

**Code quality** (Byrne's criteria):
- Brief but sufficiently detailed?
- Stands alone without needing the data extract?
- Captures underlying commonality?
- Relevant to research questions?
- Applies (or could apply) to multiple data items?

**Your interpretive lens**:
- When I read this data, do I see what AI sees?
- What am I noticing that AI isn't capturing?
- Where is my interpretation different and why?

**Document your decisions** using a simple framework:

```markdown
AI CODE: #[code]
Data: [relevant quote]

DECISION: Accept / Modify / Reject

RATIONALE:
[Your reasoning—why this works, doesn't work, or needs adjustment]

If Modify: 
New code: #[revised_code]
Why: [explanation]

If Reject:
Why problematic: [explanation]
Your alternative: #[your_code]
```

This documentation serves multiple purposes:
- Makes your interpretive reasoning explicit
- Tracks where you agreed/disagreed with AI
- Maintains transparency for reflexive TA
- Creates audit trail of analytical decisions
- Reveals your interpretive patterns and potential biases

### Step 4: Adding Human Interpretive Layer

Even well-evaluated AI codes often miss dimensions that require human interpretive work:

**What AI typically cannot capture**:

**Subtle contextual and cultural cues**: 
- Tone, hesitation, emphasis in speech
- Group dynamics in focus group interaction
- Cultural and local context (Swedish education system, policy background)
- Implicit references participants would understand but aren't explicit in text

**Emotional weight and conviction**:
- Which statements carry strong feeling vs. casual mentions?
- Where does participant show uncertainty, conflict, strong commitment?
- The *way* something is said (tentatively, emphatically, reluctantly)

**Latent meanings requiring cultural/theoretical knowledge**:
- Shared assumptions in Swedish teacher context
- Implications that require insider understanding
- Ideological dimensions that aren't surface-visible

**Contradictions and tensions**:
- Where participants hold conflicting views
- Internal struggles in reasoning
- "Yes, but..." moments that reveal complexity

**Add codes that capture these dimensions**:

Example: AI generates semantic code #tidsbrist (time shortage)

Your human interpretive layer adds:
```markdown
#→tidsbrist_som_retorisk_resurs (time shortage as rhetorical resource)

Rationale: Participant says "we don't have time" but then describes 
extensive efforts. "Time shortage" functions rhetorically here—it's 
being used to manage accountability/justify decisions, not just 
describing objective reality. This requires understanding the cultural 
context of teacher discourse and the rhetorical functions of such claims.
AI sees the surface claim but misses this latent function.
```

This is where your deep familiarity from Phase 1 (familiarization) becomes crucial. You know things about this data that AI cannot know.

### Step 5: Iterative Refinement

Just like human-first coding, AI-first coding requires multiple iterations. Your first critical evaluation pass won't be your last.

After evaluating all AI-generated codes:

**Second pass**: Consolidation
- Which codes that you accepted are essentially the same pattern? (Collapse them)
- Which need splitting into more specific codes?
- Are codes being applied consistently?

**Third pass**: Adding what AI missed
- Code sections where AI didn't capture the full meaning
- Add latent dimensions AI couldn't see
- Add cultural/contextual codes

**Fourth pass**: Quality and theory final check
- All codes meet Byrne's quality criteria?
- Theoretical alignment maintained throughout?
- Relevant to research questions?
- Ready for theme generation?

Throughout all iterations, track changes just as Byrne did. Document what codes evolved, how, and why.

## Common Elements: Reflexive Documentation

Regardless of which approach you use, reflexive documentation is essential for RTA.

### Research Journal/Memo Writing

After each coding session, write a reflexive memo. Byrne notes the importance of documenting "thoughts and feelings regarding both the data and the analytical process" (p. 1399).

**What to capture in reflexive memos**:

**Analytical process**:
- What did you focus on in this session?
- What patterns are you starting to see?
- What surprised you?
- What was difficult to code and why?

**Coding decisions**:
- Key codes generated
- Difficult decisions you made
- Provisional connections between codes

**Reflexive insights**:
- How are you influencing the interpretation?
- What assumptions are you bringing?
- Where are you uncertain?
- What do you need to think more about?

**Theoretical alignment check**:
- Are you maintaining constructionist approach?
- Is experiential orientation being honored?
- Is inductive emphasis preserved?
- Appropriate semantic/latent balance?

**For AI-first specifically**:
- Where did you disagree with AI and why?
- What did AI miss that you added?
- What biases or patterns did you notice in AI coding?
- How did dialogic engagement with AI enhance your analysis?

These memos become part of your audit trail and feed into your eventual write-up.

## The Question of Double-Coding Data Items

Byrne notes (p. 1402) that the same data item can be coded both semantically and latently. He provides a clear example of this with one participant's statement being coded two different ways to capture both levels of meaning.

This is worth emphasizing because it's often misunderstood: coding isn't about assigning each piece of data to ONE code. A single statement can:
- Be coded for multiple semantic meanings
- Be coded for both semantic and latent meanings
- Be relevant to multiple patterns you're identifying
- Contribute to multiple eventual themes

However, be judicious. The question is always: "Is this coding meaningful for my analysis?" Not "Can I code this multiple ways?"

## Before Moving to Phase 3: Quality Check

Before you consider Phase 2 complete and move to theme generation, check your codes against Byrne's key questions (2012, p. 65):

**Is this a code or just a description?** 
- Codes should capture patterns, not just describe individual data items

**If it's a code, what is its quality?**
- Does it tell you something useful about the dataset and research questions?
- Is it informative and clear?

**What are its boundaries?**
- What does this code include and exclude?
- Is it too broad (capturing too much)?
- Is it too narrow (too specific)?

**Is there enough meaningful data?**
- Does the code apply to multiple data items?
- Or is it so specific it only fits one instance?

**Are the data too diverse?**
- Do the data items under this code actually share a coherent pattern?
- Or are you forcing disparate things together?

Additionally, check theoretical alignment:
- Do codes align with your constructionist epistemology?
- Is experiential orientation maintained?
- Is the coding predominantly inductive?
- Is there appropriate semantic/latent balance?
