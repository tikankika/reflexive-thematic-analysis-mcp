# Phase 2a: Initial Coding in Dialogic RTA

## Purpose

Phase 2a is where the researcher generates initial codes through close engagement with the data. In Dialogic RTA, this means either coding independently and using AI for refinement (human-first), or having AI propose codes that the researcher critically evaluates (AI-first). Both approaches are viable. Neither exempts the researcher from interpretive work.

The foundational principles — "proving the obvious," double reflexivity, and AI as heuristic partner — are established in the Methodological Framework document and apply throughout this phase. Phase 2a is where they first become operational.

---

## The Recursive Nature of Coding in RTA

Coding in reflexive TA is fundamentally recursive and iterative. Byrne (2022, p. 1400) is explicit:

> "RTA is a recursive process and it is rare that a researcher would follow a linear path through the six phases... it is very common for the researcher to follow a particular train of thought when coding, only to encounter an impasse where several different interpretations of the data come to light"

Codes evolve. The researcher returns to earlier transcripts and recodes based on new understandings, collapses some codes together, splits others, and reconceptualizes patterns. This is the method, not a failure of it.

Byrne tracked code evolution across multiple iterations (Table 1, p. 1401):

- **Data item one**: Initially "non-wellbeing educator received no wellbeing CPD" → revised to broader "insufficient training in wellbeing curriculum" when the specific distinction became unnecessary
- **Data item three**: Initially descriptive "no assessment of students' wellbeing" → revised to more interpretive "lack of clarity in assessing student wellbeing"
- **Data item five**: Initially very specific "training in attending to students' day-by-day wellbeing required" → subsumed under broader existing code "more training is needed for wellbeing promotion" because the specific code only applied to one data item

These revisions show analytical movement from description to interpretation, from specificity to pattern, from individual data item to dataset-level thinking. Tracking and documenting changes maintains transparency and provides waypoints if the researcher needs to backtrack.

**In dialogic contexts**, recursivity has an additional dimension. When initial codes were co-created with AI, returning to earlier coding means revisiting not just the data but the AI-mediated encounter with that data. The researcher may see things differently the second time — not because the data changed, but because their interpretive capacity has developed beyond what AI proposed.

## Two Approaches to Initial Coding

The literature on dialogic qualitative research (Naeem et al. 2025, NguyenTrung 2025) supports two approaches, both compatible with RTA's reflexive principles if implemented with critical engagement.

### The key distinction is sequencing, not quality.

In **human-first** coding, interpretive work happens during code generation. In **AI-first** coding, interpretive work happens during critical evaluation of AI proposals. The work is the same — or arguably more explicit in AI-first, where every acceptance or rejection requires articulated reasoning.

Neither approach is inherently superior. The choice depends on dataset size, researcher familiarity with data, and practical constraints. What matters is that the researcher exercises genuine interpretive authority, regardless of sequence.

---

## Approach A: Human-First Coding

The researcher codes independently, then optionally uses AI for refinement and blind-spot checking.

### Systematic coding

Work through the transcript systematically, attending to each section with equal consideration. Identify aspects that are interesting and potentially relevant to research questions. Code at both semantic and latent levels where meaningful.

The systematic nature matters. Don't skip sections that seem less interesting initially. Don't be selective. Understanding evolves, and sections that seemed unimportant on first reading may become relevant later.

**How many codes?** There is no prescribed number. Code liberally at this stage — consolidation happens in later iterations. What matters is achieving sufficient depth to examine patterns across the dataset.

### Multiple iterations

Byrne recommends a minimum of three passes:

1. **First iteration**: Initial open coding — code liberally, inductively, preserve participants' language
2. **Second iteration**: Consolidation and refinement — collapse similar codes, check consistency, clarify definitions
3. **Third iteration**: Quality and theory check — alignment with theoretical position, semantic/latent balance, relevance to research questions

### Using AI for refinement

After human coding is substantially complete, AI can serve as a reflexive tool — not to validate the coding, but to challenge it.

**Gap identification**: What patterns or perspectives might the researcher have overlooked? AI's systematic processing can surface aspects that human selective attention missed. But AI's suggestions here carry the "proving the obvious" risk: the gaps AI identifies may be expected patterns from its training data, not genuinely overlooked meanings. The researcher must ask: is this gap real, or is AI filling in what it expects to see? A genuine gap is something the data supports but the researcher's attention skipped. A false gap is something AI's training data expects to find in this type of data regardless of whether it is present.

**Consistency checking**: Are similar phenomena coded similarly across the transcript? AI can help identify inconsistencies, but consistency in coding is not the same as reliability — reflexive TA does not aim for coding agreement, even with oneself across time. Apparent inconsistencies may reflect genuine analytical development. The researcher should ask: does this inconsistency indicate carelessness, or does it indicate that my understanding of this pattern evolved between the first and fifth transcript? The latter is the method working as intended.

**Latent dimension exploration**: AI can suggest potential deeper meanings, but this is where AI is weakest and human interpretation most essential. AI-proposed latent codes should be treated with particular scepticism — they may reflect training-data associations rather than situated meaning. When AI proposes a latent code, the researcher should ask: is this interpretation grounded in what *this* participant communicated in *this* context, or is it a generic deeper reading that AI's training data associates with this type of statement?

**Document every AI interaction**: Which suggestions were accepted, modified, or rejected, and why. This is not administrative overhead — it is the reflexive audit trail.

## Approach B: AI-First Coding

AI generates initial codes; the researcher critically evaluates every code. This approach can be effective for large datasets when the researcher has completed thorough familiarization (Phase 1) and has strong theoretical grounding to guide evaluation.

### Why AI-first can work

The theoretical justification: AI-first is not abandoning human interpretation. It is resequencing it. Instead of interpretation during code generation, interpretation happens during critical evaluation. The interpretive work is the same — or arguably more explicit, because the researcher must articulate why each AI proposal works or doesn't.

The practical advantage: systematic coverage. AI codes the entire transcript, not just sections the researcher would naturally attend to. This can surface patterns in data that human selective attention would miss.

The epistemological requirement: the researcher has completed Phase 1 familiarization and enters AI-first coding from a position of deep data knowledge, not cold.

### Setting up AI for theoretically-grounded coding

Before AI codes anything, orient it to the project's epistemological and methodological commitments. This is not prompt engineering — it is ensuring AI's initial proposals will be shaped by your framework rather than purely by training-data patterns.

AI needs to understand the project's epistemological commitments — that meaning is socially constructed, that participant meaning-making takes priority, that codes should grow from data rather than theory, and that both semantic and latent dimensions matter. It needs the research questions as relevance criteria (not predetermined categories) and sufficient context about the data to produce situated rather than generic proposals. The purpose of this orientation is not to constrain AI but to ensure its initial proposals are shaped by the project's framework rather than purely by training-data patterns — so that the researcher's critical evaluation begins from a higher starting point.

### Critical evaluation: the essential work

This is where the researcher's interpretive authority operates. Every AI-proposed code is evaluated — not spot-checked, not sampled, but individually assessed.

**Four questions for each code:**

**1. Does this code capture situated meaning or lexical pattern?**
AI codes may reflect word-level similarity across its training data rather than what *this* participant means in *this* context. A code that sounds correct at surface level may flatten important nuance. Test: if you read only the codes without seeing the data, would you understand what is *distinctive* about this segment?

**2. Is this code inductively grounded or training-data derived?**
AI's "induction" may be pseudo-induction — patterns from training data masquerading as data-driven discoveries. The researcher asks: does this code emerge from what participants said, or from what AI expects participants to say about this topic? This is the "proving the obvious" question applied to individual codes.

**3. Does this code honour participant meaning?**
Experiential orientation requires that codes capture meaning as participants construct it. AI may impose analytical language that displaces participants' own framings. When a teacher says "det är kaos" (it's chaos), a code should capture the experiential weight of that expression, not translate it into a clinical category.

**4. What am I noticing that AI is not?**
This question activates the researcher's independent interpretive capacity. AI cannot access tone, hesitation, group dynamics, cultural connotation, or the weight of what is *not* said. These are not marginal concerns — they are often where the most analytically interesting data resides.

**Document decisions using a clear framework:**

For each code:
- **Accept**: Code captures genuine meaning. Brief rationale.
- **Modify**: Code is partially right but needs adjustment. What changes and why.
- **Reject**: Code misses the meaning, imposes a pattern, or is redundant. What's wrong and what (if anything) replaces it.

This documentation is the reflexive audit trail. It makes interpretive reasoning explicit and demonstrates that the analysis reflects genuine engagement rather than passive acceptance of AI output.

### Adding the human interpretive layer

Even well-evaluated AI codes typically miss dimensions that require human interpretive work:

**Contextual and cultural meaning.** AI processes text. The researcher knows the institutional culture, policy context, professional discourse norms, and cultural connotations that give text its meaning. When a Swedish teacher says "vi har inte tid" (we don't have time), this functions differently depending on institutional context — it may describe objective reality, perform professional identity, manage accountability, or invoke a shared cultural narrative about teacher workload. AI sees the words. The researcher sees the function.

**Emotional weight and conviction.** Which statements carry strong feeling? Where does the participant show uncertainty, internal conflict, strong commitment? The *way* something is said — tentatively, emphatically, with laughter, after a long pause — is analytically significant but invisible to text-processing AI.

**Contradictions and tensions.** Where participants hold conflicting views, where reasoning shifts mid-sentence, where "yes, but..." moments reveal complexity. These are not noise to be smoothed over — they are often the most analytically productive moments in the data.

**Silence and absence.** What is not said. What topics are avoided. Where conversation redirects away from sensitive territory. AI cannot identify absence.

**The researcher adds codes that capture these dimensions.** This is not optional supplementation — it is where the analysis gains its distinctive analytical depth.

### Iterative refinement

AI-first coding requires the same multiple iterations as human-first coding:

1. **Critical evaluation pass**: Assess every AI-proposed code (accept, modify, reject)
2. **Consolidation pass**: Collapse similar codes, split overly broad codes, check consistency
3. **Human interpretive pass**: Add codes capturing what AI missed — context, culture, emotion, absence, contradiction
4. **Quality and theory pass**: Check alignment with epistemological position, semantic/latent balance, relevance to research questions, and whether the "proving the obvious" problem has been addressed

## Reflexive Documentation

Regardless of approach, reflexive documentation is essential. But reflexive documentation in dialogic analysis requires more than standard research journaling.

### Standard reflexivity (both approaches)

After each coding session, document:
- What patterns are emerging
- What surprised you
- What was difficult to code and why
- What interpretive decisions you made
- Where you are uncertain

### AI-mediation reflexivity (the second layer)

This is the additional requirement for dialogic work:

- Where did you agree with AI and why? Was the agreement based on genuine interpretive assessment, or on AI's proposal sounding plausible?
- Where did you disagree with AI and why? What did you see that AI didn't? What did AI see that you hadn't considered?
- Did AI's proposals influence what you subsequently noticed in the data? Were you drawn to confirming AI's patterns?
- Are there systematic tendencies in AI's coding that you should be aware of — topics it over-codes, nuances it misses, levels it favours?

This double documentation — of your analytical process AND of AI's influence on that process — is what distinguishes methodologically rigorous dialogic analysis from AI-assisted analysis that merely claims human oversight.

### Reflexive notes: genuine thinking, not formulaic completion

Reflexive notes are documentation of the researcher's interpretive process. They should vary in focus and depth based on what the data evokes.

They are **not**:
- A formula applied identically to every session
- Theoretical categorization ("This relates to disruption theory because...")
- A summary restating what the codes already say

They **are**:
- Records of genuine analytical thinking
- Honest about uncertainty and difficulty
- Variable in length — sometimes one sentence, sometimes a full paragraph
- Attentive to both data and process

## The Question of Double-Coding

The same data item can and should be coded both semantically and latently when appropriate (Byrne, 2022, p. 1402). Coding is not about assigning each piece of data to one code. A single statement can:

- Be coded for multiple semantic meanings
- Be coded for both semantic and latent meanings
- Be relevant to multiple research questions
- Contribute to multiple eventual themes

The question is always: "Is this coding meaningful for the analysis?" Not "Can I code this multiple ways?"

## Quality Check Before Phase 3

Before considering Phase 2a complete, check codes against Byrne's quality questions (adapted from Braun & Clarke, 2012, p. 65):

### Code quality

- **Is this a code or just a description?** Codes capture patterns, not just describe individual data items.
- **What are its boundaries?** What does this code include and exclude?
- **Is there enough meaningful data?** Does the code apply to multiple data items, or is it idiosyncratic?
- **Are the data too diverse?** Do data items under this code actually share a coherent pattern?
- **Is it well-named?** Would another researcher understand what it captures?

### AI-specific quality check

- **Are there codes that sound right but lack contextual grounding?** Plausible codes are not necessarily accurate codes. Test each code against your knowledge of the data, not just against its surface-level fit.
- **Has AI imposed expected patterns?** Look for codes that reflect what AI's training data would predict about this topic, rather than what participants actually said.
- **Has the coding captured anomalies, contradictions, and surprises — or only dominant patterns?** If the code list reads like a textbook summary of the topic, the "proving the obvious" problem may be present.
- **Is there appropriate semantic/latent balance?** AI typically over-produces semantic codes and under-produces latent codes. Has the researcher added sufficient latent interpretation?
- **Are in vivo codes preserved?** Participants' own language should be visible in the code list, not displaced by AI's analytical vocabulary.

### Theoretical alignment

- Do codes align with constructionist epistemology — treating data as constructions, not facts?
- Is experiential orientation maintained — honouring participant meaning?
- Is the coding predominantly inductive — growing from data, not imposed?
- Is there a reflexive audit trail documenting interpretive decisions?

## Chunk vs Segment: An Important Distinction

When coding tools process transcripts in portions, two units emerge that must not be confused:

**Chunk** = technical reading unit (60–100 lines). Determined by the tool. A practical device for managing large transcripts.

**Segment** = semantic coding unit (variable size). Determined by content. A meaning-bearing unit that may be:
- A single line (a concentrated, powerful statement)
- 3–5 lines (a statement with context)
- 20+ lines (a sustained argument or narrative)

A chunk may contain multiple segments, or occasionally just one if the entire chunk forms a coherent argument.

**The principle:** Segment by meaning, not by line count. The tool's chunk size is a practical reading unit — it must not dictate where semantic segments begin and end.

## Instructions for the AI Assistant During Coding

These instructions govern AI's behaviour when assisting with coding.

**Vary based on content, not formula:**
- Rich segment → generous coding with many codes
- Simple segment → one or two codes suffice
- Genuine latent meaning present → code latently
- No latent meaning → skip latent coding
- Reflexive notes vary in length and focus based on what the segment evokes

**Do not produce mechanical patterns:**
- Same number of codes per segment
- Always both semantic and latent levels
- Identical reflexive note structure
- Forcing data into predetermined categories

**Ask at uncertainty:**
- Who is speaking? (Speaker numbers can be unreliable in transcripts)
- Moderator or participant?
- Which research question does this most clearly address?
- Should this segment be split or kept together?

**The researcher has interpretive authority.** AI proposes codes. The researcher decides. AI does not make coding decisions autonomously or proceed without researcher approval.

**Epistemic awareness:** AI should be alert to the "proving the obvious" risk in its own proposals. When proposing codes, AI should note where it is most and least confident — not about the correctness of the code, but about whether the code captures something genuinely present in the data versus something AI expects to find based on the topic.
