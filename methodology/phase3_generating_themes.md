# Phase 3: Generating Themes in AI-Augmented RTA
## Based on Byrne (2022)

---

## The Shift from Codes to Themes

Phase 3 marks a fundamental shift in your analytical focus. As Byrne explains (2022, p. 1403):

> "This phase begins when all relevant data items have been coded. The focus shifts from the interpretation of individual data items within the dataset, to the interpretation of aggregated meaning and meaningfulness across the dataset."

You're no longer asking "what pattern does this data item show?" but rather "what patterns exist across my entire dataset when I look at all these codes together?"

This is where the analytical work becomes more explicitly interpretive and creative. You're actively construing relationships between different codes and constructing themes that capture meaningful patterns across your data.

---

## What Themes Are (and Are Not)

Before beginning theme generation, it's crucial to understand what reflexive TA means by "theme"—because this is where significant confusion exists in the literature.

### Themes as Patterns of Shared Meaning

In reflexive TA, themes are **patterns of shared meaning** organized around a **central organizing concept** (Braun & Clarke 2019). They tell a story about a particular pattern you've interpreted across your dataset.

Braun and Clarke (2019, p. 594) emphasize: "themes are not predefining in order to 'find' codes. Rather, themes are produced by organising codes around a relative core commonality, or 'central organising concept', that the researcher interprets from the data."

This is fundamentally different from "domain summaries"—mere summaries of what participants said about a particular topic. Themes in reflexive TA are interpretive, analytical constructions that capture something meaningful about your data in relation to your research questions.

### Themes vs. Codes: The Key Distinction

**Codes** are the building blocks—labels for patterns of meaning you identified in individual data items or across several items.

**Themes** are higher-level constructions—organizing multiple codes around a core concept that tells a coherent story about meaning in your dataset.

Think of it this way: Codes say "this pattern exists in the data." Themes say "here's what this constellation of patterns means and why it matters."

### The Salience of Themes

Just as with codes, the importance or salience of a theme is NOT determined by how many codes it contains or how frequently something appears in your data. Your constructionist epistemology means meaningfulness matters more than frequency.

A theme supported by fewer codes but capturing something particularly meaningful to your research questions might be more important than a theme with many codes that describes something common but less significant.

As Braun and Clarke (2013) note, the researcher must construe the importance of themes based on relevance to research questions, not count codes or data items.

## The Process of Generating Themes from Your Codes

### Step 1: Review and Collate All Your Codes

At this point, you should have completed Phase 2 with a comprehensive list of codes across all your transcripts. Gather these together so you can see the full picture.

Byrne tracked his codes in a spreadsheet, but you might use:
- A large spreadsheet with all codes listed
- Physical post-it notes you can move around
- A digital whiteboard tool
- A simple document listing all codes with brief definitions

Whatever format works for you, the key is being able to see all your codes together and start noticing relationships between them.

### Step 2: Look for Patterns and Relationships

Now begins the active interpretive work of looking for how different codes relate to each other.

**Ask yourself**:
- Which codes seem to be capturing related patterns?
- Which codes share an underlying commonality or core concept?
- Are there codes that seem to speak to the same broader issue or theme?
- Are there tensions or contradictions between codes that might themselves be meaningful?

**Byrne's example** (p. 1403-1404): In his initial candidate themes, he brought together several codes under "factors influencing wellbeing promotion":
- Codes about lack of training
- Codes about time constraints  
- Codes about lack of value placed on wellbeing
- Codes about knowledge of supporting documents

He saw these as related—they all spoke to factors that inhibit wellbeing promotion. So he initially clustered them together as a candidate theme.

However—and this is important—this initial clustering didn't survive to his final thematic framework. During Phase 4 (review), he realized this theme was too dense and unwieldy, and broke it down into three separate themes with fundamentally reconceptualized meanings.

This illustrates the recursive nature of RTA. Your initial theme generation is provisional. You'll refine, restructure, and possibly completely reconceptualize themes in Phase 4.

### Step 3: Start Clustering Codes into Candidate Themes

Begin actively organizing codes into potential themes. This is tentative and exploratory—you're trying out different ways of organizing your codes to see what makes sense.

**Methods for clustering**:

**Visual mapping**: Create a thematic map (like Byrne's Figure 1, p. 1403) showing potential themes, sub-themes, and relationships between them. This can be hand-drawn, created in a mind-mapping tool, or done with post-it notes on a wall.

**Spreadsheet organization**: Create columns for different candidate themes and sort your codes under each theme.

**Narrative writing**: Write brief paragraphs describing each potential theme and which codes it encompasses. Sometimes writing helps you think through whether the clustering makes sense.

**Key questions as you cluster**:
- Does this grouping of codes have internal coherence? (Do they genuinely belong together?)
- Can I articulate a central organizing concept that unites these codes?
- Does this tell a meaningful story about the data?
- Is this relevant to my research questions?

### Step 4: Consider Sub-themes

Some of your themes might be complex enough to warrant sub-themes—smaller patterns within the larger theme that are distinctive enough to be noted separately.

**Byrne's example**: His theme "Best practice in wellbeing promotion" had two sub-themes:
- "The whole-school approach" (involving entire school staff)
- "Active delivery of wellbeing promotion" (practical measures)

These sub-themes both related to best practices, but captured two distinct aspects of that broader theme. They worked together but each had its own story to tell.

**Don't over-use sub-themes**: Not every theme needs them. Use sub-themes only when they genuinely add analytical clarity. Too many sub-themes can make your analysis unwieldy.

### Step 5: Create a Provisional Thematic Map

At the end of this initial phase of theme generation, create a visual representation of your candidate themes. Byrne provides an excellent example in Figure 1 (p. 1403).

Your thematic map should show:
- Your candidate themes (usually represented as ovals or boxes)
- Sub-themes within each theme
- Relationships between themes (shown with connecting lines)
- Any hierarchical relationships

This map is provisional—a working document you'll refine in Phase 4. Its purpose is to help you visualize the structure of your analysis and see how different themes relate to each other.

**Byrne's initial map** (Figure 1) showed four candidate themes:
1. Best practice in wellbeing promotion
2. Recognising student wellbeing
3. Factors inhibiting wellbeing promotion (this one was later broken down)
4. Recognising educator wellbeing

He also showed relationships between themes with dotted lines—for example, how time constraints connected to best practices.

## Working with Theme Boundaries and Relationships

### Internal Homogeneity and External Heterogeneity

Braun and Clarke reference Patton's (1990) "dual criteria for judging categories" which is useful for evaluating your candidate themes:

**Internal homogeneity**: Data and codes within a theme should cohere together meaningfully. There should be a clear pattern that unites them. If you're forcing disparate codes together, the internal homogeneity is weak.

**External heterogeneity**: Themes should be clearly distinguishable from each other. Each theme should capture something distinctive. If two themes are really saying the same thing, they might need to be collapsed into one.

### Relationships Between Themes

Themes don't exist in isolation—they relate to each other and together tell the story of your dataset.

**Consider**:
- Do some themes build on or depend on other themes?
- Are there tensions or contradictions between themes?
- Do themes work together to create a coherent narrative?
- What order might you present themes in to build a logical argument?

Byrne shows these relationships in his thematic maps with connecting lines. In his final map (Figure 2, p. 1405), dotted lines show relationships between different themes—for example, connecting "The influence of time" to multiple other themes it affected.

### The Miscellaneous Category

You'll likely have some codes that don't seem to fit anywhere. Braun and Clarke (2012) suggest creating a miscellaneous theme or category for these.

This serves two purposes:
1. It keeps those codes visible rather than just discarding them
2. Sometimes what seems miscellaneous initially becomes its own theme on reflection, or you later see how it fits into an existing theme

Don't force codes into themes where they don't belong just to avoid having "leftover" codes. It's fine to have a miscellaneous category at this stage.

## Using AI as Dialogic Partner in Theme Generation

If you're using AI-augmentation, Phase 3 is where AI can serve as a particularly useful dialogic partner—helping you explore different ways of organizing themes without imposing structure.

### Potential Uses of AI in Theme Generation

**Identifying potential theme clusters**:
```
I have the following codes from my analysis: [list all codes with brief definitions]

Looking at these codes, what potential patterns or groupings do you notice? 
What codes seem to share underlying commonalities?

Note: I'm not asking you to define my themes—I'm asking you to help me see 
potential relationships between codes that I can then evaluate.
```

**Testing theme coherence**:
```
I'm considering a candidate theme that brings together these codes:
[list codes]

Central organizing concept: [your concept]

Questions:
- Do these codes genuinely cohere around this concept?
- Are there codes here that don't quite fit?
- Is there a more accurate way to articulate the core concept?
```

**Exploring different organizational structures**:
```
I'm trying to decide between two ways of organizing these codes into themes:

Option A: [describe structure]
Option B: [describe structure]

What are the analytical advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
Which better captures distinct patterns vs. forces artificial separation?
```

**Identifying potential sub-themes**:
```
I have this candidate theme with these codes: [list]

This feels dense. Are there distinct sub-patterns here that might warrant 
sub-themes? Or should this be split into multiple separate themes?
```

**Checking for missing connections**:
```
Here's my provisional thematic map: [describe or paste]

Are there relationships between themes I haven't considered?
Are there potential tensions or contradictions I should attend to?
```

### Critical Engagement, Not Passive Acceptance

Just as with coding, do NOT passively accept AI suggestions about themes. AI can help you think, but YOU determine:

- What the central organizing concept of each theme is
- How codes cluster together meaningfully
- Which theme structure best captures your data
- How themes relate to your research questions
- The story each theme tells

**Document your engagement**:
```markdown
## Theme Generation Dialogue with AI

AI suggested clustering: [description]
My evaluation: [accept/modify/reject + reasoning]

Why this works / doesn't work:
- Theoretical alignment
- Internal coherence
- Relevance to RQs
- Captures meaningful pattern vs. artificial grouping

Final decision: [your theme structure]
```

## Common Challenges in Theme Generation

### Challenge 1: Too Many Themes

If you end up with eight or ten candidate themes, your analysis might be unwieldy. More themes isn't better—it often means you haven't done enough interpretive work to see the broader patterns.

**Solution**: Look for themes that might actually be sub-themes of larger themes, or themes that could be meaningfully collapsed together around a broader organizing concept.

### Challenge 2: Too Few Themes

Conversely, if you only have two or three themes, you might not be capturing the depth and complexity of your data.

**Solution**: Look within your themes. Are there distinct sub-patterns that warrant separate themes? Are you collapsing too much together under overly broad concepts?

### Challenge 3: Themes That Are Really Just Topics

If your "themes" are just topic labels like "Teachers' views on AI" or "Workload issues," you haven't done interpretive work yet. These are topics or domains, not themes.

**Solution**: Ask "what is the pattern of meaning here?" Not just "what topic is this about?" Your themes should capture an interpreted pattern, not just label a content area.

**Example**:
- NOT A THEME: "Time constraints" (this is just a topic)
- IS A THEME: "The bidirectional influence of time constraints on wellbeing promotion" (this captures a specific interpreted pattern about how time works)

### Challenge 4: Themes That Don't Address Research Questions

Sometimes you'll identify interesting patterns that don't actually help answer your research questions.

**Solution**: Stay focused on relevance. Interesting but irrelevant patterns should be noted but not developed into full themes. Your themes need to contribute to answering your RQs.

### Challenge 5: Overlapping Themes Without Clear Distinction

If you can't clearly articulate what makes two themes different from each other, you might have external heterogeneity problems.

**Solution**: Either collapse them into one theme, or work to articulate more precisely what the distinctive pattern is in each theme.

- Themes have clear focus and boundaries
- Sub-themes capture distinct aspects within larger patterns

## Theme Naming (Provisional at This Stage)

At this phase, you'll give your candidate themes provisional names. These will likely be refined further in Phase 5 (defining and naming themes), but you need working titles to refer to them.

**At this stage, theme names might be**:
- Descriptive (describing the core pattern)
- Working titles that help you think about the theme
- Somewhat lengthy if needed for clarity

Don't worry yet about creating catchy, concise final names. That's Phase 5 work. Right now, prioritize clarity over creativity.

## Reflexive Questions for Phase 3

As you generate themes, keep asking yourself:

**About theme quality**:
- Does this theme tell a coherent story?
- Is the central organizing concept clear?
- Does it have internal coherence?
- Is it distinctive from other themes?

**About theoretical alignment**:
- Does this theme align with my constructionist epistemology?
- Am I honoring participants' experiential meanings?
- Am I generating themes inductively from the data?
- Am I capturing both semantic and latent levels where appropriate?

**About research questions**:
- Does this theme help answer my research questions?
- Is it relevant and meaningful to my research aims?
- Am I staying focused on what I actually need to address?

**About the overall framework**:
- Do my themes together tell a coherent story about my data?
- Are there gaps—important patterns I'm not capturing?
- Are there redundancies—themes that overlap too much?
- How do themes relate to each other?

## Documenting Your Theme Generation Process

Just as with coding, reflexive documentation is essential.

**After theme generation sessions, record**:

**Theme development process**:
```markdown
Session: [Date]

Codes reviewed: [number and focus]

Initial clustering attempts:
- Tried grouping [these codes] around [concept] - didn't work because [reason]
- Tried alternative structure [describe] - more promising because [reason]

Candidate themes emerging:
[List with brief descriptions]

Relationships between themes:
[How themes connect or contrast]

Uncertainties:
- Not sure about [specific issue]
- Need to think more about [aspect]

Next steps:
[What needs attention]
```

**Reflexive notes**:
```markdown
What interpretive decisions am I making?
Why am I seeing these patterns?
What am I drawn to vs. overlooking?
How am I influenced by my theoretical framework?
Where am I uncertain and why?
```

**If using AI dialogue**:
```markdown
AI suggestions I found helpful: [list]
AI suggestions I rejected: [list + why]
How AI dialogue pushed my thinking: [reflection]
Where I disagreed with AI and my reasoning: [explanation]
```

## Preparing for Phase 4: Review

At the end of Phase 3, you should have:

✓ A provisional thematic map showing candidate themes and sub-themes
✓ Clusters of codes organized under each theme
✓ A clear sense of the central organizing concept for each theme
✓ Notes on relationships between themes
✓ A miscellaneous category for codes that don't yet fit
✓ Reflexive documentation of your theme generation process

**You're ready for Phase 4 when**:
- You can articulate what each candidate theme is about (its central organizing concept)
- You have a visual map showing theme structure and relationships
- You understand this is provisional and will be reviewed/refined
- You've documented your interpretive process

Remember: Phase 4 is specifically about reviewing and refining these candidate themes. Don't expect perfection at this stage. Byrne completely restructured one of his major themes during Phase 4. That's normal and expected.

## A Note on Non-Linearity

Byrne emphasizes repeatedly that RTA is recursive, not linear. Even though we describe it in phases (1, 2, 3, 4...), you'll move back and forth between phases.

You might be generating themes (Phase 3) and realize you need to go back and recode some data (Phase 2). You might be reviewing themes (Phase 4) and realize you need to generate a completely new theme (back to Phase 3). You might even return to familiarization (Phase 1) to check your understanding of context.

This isn't a sign of doing it wrong—this IS reflexive thematic analysis. The phases are guidelines for organizing your work, not rigid sequential steps.

As Byrne notes (p. 1398): "the analysis is not a linear process of moving forward through the phases. Rather, the analysis is recursive and iterative, requiring the researcher to move back and forth through the phases as necessary."

## Moving to Phase 4

Phase 4 involves two levels of review:
1. **Level one**: Reviewing coded data extracts in relation to each candidate theme
2. **Level two**: Reviewing candidate themes in relation to the entire dataset

This is where you'll test whether your candidate themes actually work, and where significant refinement and restructuring often happens.

But that's Phase 4 work. For now, focus on generating those candidate themes and creating your provisional thematic map.
