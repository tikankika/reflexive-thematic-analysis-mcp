# Phase 3: Generating Themes in Dialogic RTA

## Purpose

Phase 3 marks a fundamental shift in analytical focus. The researcher moves from interpreting individual data items to interpreting aggregated meaning across the dataset. As Byrne explains (2022, p. 1403):

> "This phase begins when all relevant data items have been coded. The focus shifts from the interpretation of individual data items within the dataset, to the interpretation of aggregated meaning and meaningfulness across the dataset."

The question is no longer "what pattern does this data item show?" but "what patterns exist across my entire dataset when I look at all these codes together?" This is where the analytical work becomes explicitly interpretive and creative — the researcher actively construes relationships between codes and constructs themes that capture meaningful patterns.

In dialogic contexts, this is also where the "proving the obvious" problem becomes most acute. AI excels at grouping similar codes — but similarity is not meaning. The risk is that AI-proposed theme clusters reflect surface-level lexical grouping rather than genuine patterns of shared meaning constructed through interpretive engagement.

---

## What Themes Are (and Are Not)

### Themes as patterns of shared meaning

In reflexive TA, themes are **patterns of shared meaning** organized around a **central organizing concept** (Braun & Clarke, 2019, p. 594). They tell a story about a pattern the researcher has interpreted across the dataset.

This is fundamentally different from "domain summaries" — summaries of what participants said about a particular topic. Themes are interpretive, analytical constructions. They do not reside in the data waiting to be found. They are built by the researcher through active engagement with codes and data.

**The critical test:** A theme is not a topic. "Teachers' views on AI" is a topic. "AI as threat to professional identity" is a theme — it captures a specific interpreted pattern about how participants construct meaning, organized around a central concept (identity threat).

### Salience and meaningfulness

The importance of a theme is not determined by frequency — how many codes it contains or how often something appears. Constructionist epistemology means meaningfulness matters more than recurrence. A theme supported by fewer codes but capturing something particularly significant for the research questions may be more important than a quantitatively "bigger" theme that describes something common but analytically shallow.

**In dialogic contexts:** AI is biased toward frequency. It will naturally foreground patterns that recur across many codes and many data items. The researcher must actively resist this bias and ask: is this theme meaningful, or just common? A theme supported by three codes from three different participants, each capturing a moment of genuine interpretive tension, may be more analytically productive than a theme supported by thirty codes that all describe the same surface-level observation.

## The Process of Generating Themes

### Step 1: Review and collate all codes

Gather all codes from all transcripts so the full picture is visible. The format matters less than the ability to see all codes together and notice relationships.

### Step 2: Look for patterns and relationships

This is active interpretive work. The researcher looks for how codes relate — which codes share an underlying commonality, which speak to the same broader issue, which create productive tensions.

**The questions:**
- Which codes seem to capture related patterns?
- Which codes share an underlying commonality or core concept?
- Are there tensions or contradictions between codes that might themselves be meaningful?
- What would I expect the themes to be — and what in the codes *doesn't* fit those expectations?

The last question is the "proving the obvious" heuristic applied to theme generation. By making the expected thematic structure explicit, the researcher gains a reference point for identifying what is genuinely interesting in the data.

### Step 3: Cluster codes into candidate themes

Begin organizing codes into potential themes. This is tentative and exploratory — trying different configurations to see what captures meaning most effectively.

**Internal homogeneity:** Do the codes within this cluster genuinely cohere? Is there a central organizing concept that unites them? Or are they merely about the same general topic?

**External heterogeneity:** Is this cluster clearly distinguishable from other clusters? Does each capture something distinctive?

**The central organizing concept test:** Can you articulate in one or two sentences what this theme is *about* — not what topic it covers, but what pattern of meaning it captures? If you cannot, the cluster may not yet be a theme.

### Step 4: Consider sub-themes

Some themes may be complex enough to warrant sub-themes — distinct patterns within the larger theme. Use sub-themes only when they genuinely add analytical clarity. Not every theme needs them, and too many sub-themes make the analysis unwieldy.

### Step 5: Create a provisional thematic map

Visualize your candidate themes, their sub-themes, and the relationships between them. The map should make visible not just the themes themselves but how they relate — connections, tensions, dependencies, hierarchies. This is a working document; Phase 4 will test and revise it.

## AI in Theme Generation: Opportunities and Risks

AI can be useful in theme generation — but the risks are specific and significant.

### What AI does well

**Identifying code co-occurrence.** AI can systematically map which codes appear together, which co-occur across data items, and which form clusters based on content similarity. This is computationally straightforward and can surface relationships the researcher hasn't noticed.

**Testing alternative configurations.** AI can quickly propose multiple ways of organizing codes into themes, allowing the researcher to compare structures and identify which best captures meaning in the data.

**Checking for gaps.** AI can identify codes that don't fit any current cluster, or areas of the data that the current thematic structure doesn't address.

### What AI does poorly — and why it matters

**Constructing meaning.** AI groups by similarity. Themes require meaning. The difference is fundamental. Similar codes may belong to different themes (because their meaning differs in context), and dissimilar codes may belong to the same theme (because they share an underlying organizing concept that isn't lexically visible). AI cannot make these judgments.

**Resisting the expected.** This is where "proving the obvious" is most dangerous. Ask a language model to generate themes from codes about teachers and AI, and it will produce themes that look like a literature review of the topic — because that is what its training data contains. Expected thematic structures (benefits/barriers, adoption/resistance, opportunities/challenges) will surface reliably. Whether these structures actually capture what *this* data says is a different question entirely.

**Recognizing what is interesting.** The analytically productive theme is often not the biggest or most obvious cluster, but the one that captures something surprising, contradictory, or specific to these participants in this context. AI's statistical logic works against this — it foregrounds the typical and backgrounds the unusual.

### The operational principle

Use AI to map the terrain of your codes. Use your interpretive judgment to decide what the map means.

**When AI proposes theme clusters, ask:**
- Is this a meaningful pattern or a lexical grouping?
- Does this theme tell a story, or does it just label a topic?
- Would this theme appear in any study on this topic — or does it capture something specific to *this* data?
- What codes don't fit, and are they analytically interesting precisely because they don't fit?
- What thematic structure would AI *not* propose — and is that structure actually a better fit for the data?

**Document your engagement:** What AI suggested. What you accepted, modified, or rejected. Why. This is double reflexivity in practice — reflecting on both your analytical process and AI's influence on it.

## Common Challenges

### Too many themes

More than six or seven candidate themes usually means insufficient interpretive work. The researcher hasn't yet seen the broader patterns that unite smaller clusters.

*Solution:* Look for themes that are actually sub-themes of larger themes, or themes that can be meaningfully collapsed around a broader organizing concept.

### Too few themes

Two or three themes may not capture the depth and complexity of the data.

*Solution:* Look within themes for distinct sub-patterns that warrant separation. Are you collapsing too much together under overly broad concepts?

### Themes that are topics, not patterns

If your "themes" are labels like "Time constraints" or "AI benefits," you haven't done interpretive work yet. These are content domains, not themes.

*Solution:* Ask what the *pattern of meaning* is — not what topic this is about. "Time constraints" becomes "The bidirectional influence of time on practice" — now it captures a specific interpreted pattern.

### Themes that are expected rather than discovered

If your themes read like the table of contents of a literature review on your topic, the "proving the obvious" problem may be present.

*Solution:* Ask what is *distinctive* about this data. What wouldn't you predict from the literature alone? What surprised you in coding? Those surprises may point to the most analytically productive themes.

### The miscellaneous problem

Some codes won't fit anywhere. Braun and Clarke (2012) recommend a miscellaneous category to keep these visible. Don't force codes into themes where they don't belong — and don't discard them. What seems miscellaneous now may become its own theme on reflection, or may later find a home.

## Byrne's Example

Byrne's initial candidate themes (Figure 1, p. 1403) illustrate what this phase produces:

1. **Best practice in wellbeing promotion** (sub-themes: whole-school approach, active delivery)
2. **Recognising student wellbeing** (sub-themes: sources of negative affect, actions to improve)
3. **Factors inhibiting wellbeing promotion** (sub-themes: lack of training, time constraints, lack of value, knowledge of documents)
4. **Recognising educator wellbeing** (sub-themes: sources of negative affect, actions to improve)

What matters is what happened next. Theme 3 — "Factors inhibiting wellbeing promotion" — was completely broken down in Phase 4 and reconceptualized as three separate themes with fundamentally different organizing concepts. The initial clustering didn't survive review.

This illustrates two things: Phase 3 themes are provisional, and the most obvious cluster (everything that inhibits wellbeing) may not be the most analytically productive organization.

## Avoiding Premature Thematization

Phase 3 generates candidate themes. It does not finalize them. The distinction between Phase 3 (generating) and Phase 4 (reviewing) exists because initial theme construction needs to be tested — against the coded data, against the full dataset, and against the research questions.

**Appropriate in Phase 3:**
- "I notice several codes cluster around X"
- "There seems to be a pattern connecting Y and Z"
- "This grouping has a possible organizing concept of..."

**Premature (save for Phase 4):**
- "This IS my theme about..."
- "These themes are final"
- "I've found the structure"

Hold your candidate themes lightly. Phase 4 will test them.

---

## What You Should Have at the End of Phase 3

- A provisional thematic map showing candidate themes, sub-themes, and relationships
- Codes organized under each candidate theme
- A clear articulation of the central organizing concept for each candidate theme
- A miscellaneous category for codes that don't yet fit
- Reflexive documentation of the theme generation process, including AI interactions
- Awareness that this is provisional and will be reviewed in Phase 4
