# Phase 6: Producing the Report in AI-Augmented RTA
## Based on Byrne (2022)

---

## Writing as Analysis: The Blurred Boundary

Phase 6 is not a discrete stage that begins only when analysis is "complete." As Byrne emphasizes (2022, p. 1409):

> "The separation between phases five and six can often be blurry. Further, this 'final' phase would rarely only occur at the end of the analysis."

In qualitative research, writing IS analysis. Unlike quantitative research where you conduct analysis and then write it up, in RTA "the write-up of qualitative research is very much interwoven into the entire process of the analysis" (Byrne, p. 1409).

You've been writing throughout your analysis:
- Phase 1: Reflexive memos during familiarization
- Phase 2: Code definitions and rationale
- Phase 3: Theme development notes
- Phase 4: Documentation of revisions
- Phase 5: Detailed theme definitions

**Phase 6 is about:** Completing and finalizing the formal report that you've actually been writing all along. It's the final inspection and integration of analytical writing that has evolved recursively throughout the entire process.

---

## The Task of Phase 6: Telling the Complicated Story

As Braun and Clarke (2006, p. 93) describe it, the task of Phase 6 is:

> "to tell the complicated story of your data in a way which convinces the reader of the merit and validity of your analysis."

Your write-up must provide:

**A concise, coherent, logical, non-repetitive and interesting account** of the story the data tell—within and across themes.

**Sufficient evidence of themes within the data**: Enough data extracts to demonstrate the prevalence of the theme (though remember: meaningfulness matters more than frequency).

**Vivid, compelling extracts**: Choose particularly vivid examples or extracts which capture the essence of the point you're demonstrating, without unnecessary complexity. The extract should be easily identifiable as an example of the issue.

**But writing needs to do MORE than just provide data**: Extracts need to be embedded within an analytic narrative that compellingly illustrates the story you're telling about your data.

**Your analytic narrative needs to go beyond description** and make an argument in relation to your research question.

## Analytical vs. Illustrative Write-Up: The Critical Choice

This distinction was introduced in Phase 5 but becomes operational in Phase 6. Byrne provides clear guidance (2022, p. 1407):

### Illustrative Write-Up

**Characteristics**:
- Provides surface-level description of what participants said
- Literature review held until 'Discussion' section
- Extracts illustrate patterns already described
- Test: Extract can be removed and write-up still makes sense

**When appropriate**: Illustrative write-up is "completely acceptable" (Byrne, p. 1407), particularly for:
- Introductory sections
- Establishing context
- Some descriptive themes

### Analytical Write-Up

**Characteristics**:
- Interrogates what has been interpreted as important
- Contextualizes interpretation in relation to literature AS and WHEN extracts are reported in 'Results'
- Extracts are integral to the analysis
- Test: Extract cannot be removed without making write-up incomprehensible

**Why analytical is preferred for RTA**:

As Byrne notes: "RTA is an interpretive approach to analysis and, as such, the overall report should go beyond describing the data, providing theoretically informed arguments as to how the data addresses the research question(s)" (p. 1407).

The narrative should "communicate the complexities of the data, while remaining 'embedded in the scholarly field'" (Braun & Clarke, 2012, p. 69, cited in Byrne, p. 1407).

**The straightforward test** (Terry et al., 2017):
- If extract can be removed and write-up still makes sense → **Illustrative**
- If extract is removed and write-up no longer makes sense → **Analytical**

## The Traditional Structure Problem: Results vs. Discussion

Byrne identifies an important tension (2022, p. 1410):

**Traditional academic convention**: Report results in 'Results' section, then synthesize and contextualize in separate 'Discussion' section.

**RTA analytical approach**: Braun and Clarke recommend "synthesising and contextualising data as and when they are reported in the 'results' section" (Byrne, p. 1410, citing Braun & Clarke 2013; Terry et al. 2017).

### How to Navigate This Tension

**For journal articles**: Check journal guidelines and examples of published RTAs. Many journals now accept integrated analytical approaches.

**For theses/dissertations**: Check institutional requirements. You may need to:
- Adopt fully analytical approach (synthesize in Results section)
- Use hybrid approach (some synthesis in Results, deeper discussion in Discussion)
- Advocate for methodologically appropriate structure with your supervisors

**Key principle**: Whatever structure you use, maintain the analytical depth appropriate to RTA. Don't let structural conventions force you into purely descriptive writing in your Results section.

## Ordering Your Themes: Building a Coherent Narrative

"Themes should connect in a logical and meaningful manner, building a cogent narrative of the data" (Byrne, 2022, p. 1410).

### Principles for Theme Ordering

**Logical progression**: Where relevant, themes should build upon previously reported themes.

**Internal consistency**: Each theme should remain capable of communicating its own individual narrative even if isolated from other themes.

**Meaningful connections**: The order should help readers understand relationships between themes.

### Byrne's Example: Rationale for Theme Order

Byrne reports his five themes in this order (p. 1410), with clear rationale:

**1. Best Practice in Wellbeing Promotion** (First)
- Established the underlying positivity in all participants' accounts
- Strongly influenced by semantic codes
- Participants very capable of describing 'best practice'
- "An easily digestible first theme to ease the reader into the wider analysis"

**2. The Axiology of Wellbeing Promotion** (Second)
- Introduced the reality: despite positivity, participants had numerous concerns
- Participants' attitudes were "generally positive with a significant 'but'"
- Provided good sign-posting for next two themes

**3. The Influence of Time** (Third)
- Established how time constraints negatively affected educator training
- Set up context for next theme

**4. Incompletely Theorised Agreements** (Fourth)
- Built on previous theme: time constraints pushed educators toward atheoretical agreements
- Natural progression from structural constraint (time) to conceptual consequence (lack of theory)

**5. Recognising Educator Wellbeing** (Last)
- Closing commentary: educators' own wellbeing
- "As the purpose of the analysis was to ascertain the attitudes of educators regarding wellbeing promotion, it felt appropriate to offer the closing commentary of the analysis to educators' accounts of their own wellbeing"
- Particularly pertinent with revised sub-themes showing influence of pre-existing issues and wellbeing promotion

**Notice**: This ordering tells a story—from ideal practice, through constraints and consequences, to the human cost.

### Questions to Guide Your Theme Ordering

- What provides the most logical entry point for readers?
- Which themes need to be understood before others make sense?
- What creates the most compelling narrative arc?
- Which theme provides the most satisfying conclusion?
- How do you want readers to experience the unfolding analysis?

## Writing Strong Analytical Narratives

### Moving Beyond Description to Analysis

**Don't just describe**: "The participant said X"

**Analyze and interpret**: "The participant's construction of X as [interpretation] suggests [theoretical insight], which connects to [broader pattern]"

### Elements of Analytical Writing

**Contextualize extracts**: Don't drop quotes in isolation. Set up what the extract demonstrates.

**Interpret, don't just report**: What's interesting about this? Why does this matter? What does this reveal?

**Connect to theory**: How does this pattern relate to existing literature? What theoretical concepts help make sense of this?

**Build arguments**: Each theme section should make a clear argument about what this pattern means and why it matters.

**Show relationships**: How does this theme connect to other themes? How does it help answer your research questions?

### Byrne's Analytical Write-Up Example (Box 4)

Looking at Byrne's analytical excerpt (p. 1408-1409) discussing "appropriate educator":

Rather than just describing what participants said, he:
- Examines HOW participants constructed the criteria (through negative connotations)
- Identifies the pattern of defining appropriateness by what it's NOT
- Connects to theoretical concepts (value dissimilarity, negative perceptions of 'other' group)
- Explores implications for identity boundary construction

This is analytical depth—going beyond surface description to theoretical interpretation.

## Balancing Extracts and Analysis

### How Much Data to Include?

**Sufficient evidence**: Enough extracts to demonstrate the theme exists across your dataset and captures meaningful patterns.

**Multiple perspectives**: Extracts from different participants (or different focus groups in your case) showing diversity within the pattern.

**But not excessive**: Your write-up should not become a series of quotes. The analysis must remain the star.

### The Right Balance

Braun & Clarke's guidance (2006, p. 93): "A good balance between analytic narrative and illustrative extracts"

**Rule of thumb**: Your analytical narrative should substantially outweigh the quoted material. If you removed all the extracts, you should still have a coherent, substantial argument.

### Introducing and Contextualizing Extracts

**Effective framing**:
```
One teacher powerfully captured the tension between wellbeing ideals 
and practical reality:

"[Extract]"

This construction of wellbeing promotion as something that "sounds good 
but doesn't work" reflects a broader pattern across the dataset where...
```

**Weak framing**:
```
A participant said:

"[Extract]"
```

The difference: The strong version tells readers what to see in the extract and why it matters.

## Structure of Your Findings Section

### Recommended Organization

**Introduction to themes**: Brief overview of your thematic framework. How many themes? How they relate? Brief orientation for readers.

**For each theme**:

1. **Theme introduction**: Clear statement of what this theme captures. The central organizing concept.

2. **Theme narrative**: The analytical story this theme tells, woven together with:
   - Multiple data extracts
   - Interpretation and analysis
   - Connections to literature
   - Theoretical contextualization
   - Relationship to research questions

3. **Sub-themes** (if applicable): Follow same pattern for each sub-theme.

4. **Transition**: Brief connection to next theme (where logical).

### Example Structure

```
4. FINDINGS

4.1 Overview of Thematic Framework

[Brief description of five themes and how they work together]

4.2 Theme 1: [Name]

[Central organizing concept]

[Analytical narrative with extracts, interpretation, literature connections]

  4.2.1 Sub-theme 1a: [Name]
  [Analysis]
  
  4.2.2 Sub-theme 1b: [Name]
  [Analysis]

4.3 Theme 2: [Name]

[Continue pattern]
```

## AI-Augmented Considerations for Phase 6

### Transparency About AI Use

**Essential disclosure**: Your Methods section must clearly explain:
- Which AI tools were used at which phases
- What tasks AI performed vs. human work
- How you critically evaluated AI contributions
- Your process for maintaining interpretive authority

**Example transparency statement** (adapted from Naeem et al., 2023):

> "This study employed an AI-augmented approach to reflexive thematic analysis. ChatGPT-4 [or Claude] was used during Phase 2 (initial coding) to generate preliminary codes from Swedish-language focus group transcripts, and during Phase 5 (theme definition) to explore alternative conceptualizations. All AI-generated outputs were critically evaluated by the researcher, who maintained interpretive authority throughout. The researcher refined, rejected, or substantially modified AI suggestions based on deep engagement with the data, constructionist epistemological commitments, and Swedish-language cultural context. Final themes represent the researcher's interpretation, informed by but not determined by AI assistance."

### Documenting the AI-Human Collaboration

Your write-up should make visible:

**What AI helped you see**: Legitimate insights or pattern recognition

**Where you departed from AI**: Critical instances where your interpretation differed

**How human judgment was essential**: Cultural context, latent meanings, theoretical interpretation

**Why this strengthens analysis**: Enhanced rigor through systematic checking, not replacement of human meaning-making

### From Ozuem et al. (2025): Procedural Consistency in AI Contexts

"Researchers working in AI-augmented contexts are therefore advised to document both human-led and AI-supported analytic actions in detail to ensure clarity in how codes and themes were identified, revised, or validated" (p. 9).

This documentation should:
- Be thorough enough for readers to assess methodological integrity
- Show the iterative nature of human-AI collaboration
- Demonstrate how interpretive decisions were made
- Maintain transparency without overwhelming the narrative

## Reflexive Documentation for Phase 6

### Writing as Reflexive Practice

Writing your findings IS reflexive analysis. As you write, pay attention to:

**Interpretive choices you're making**: Why this extract? Why this interpretation? Why this theoretical connection?

**Tensions and contradictions**: Where do themes create productive tensions? Where do data resist neat categorization?

**What the writing reveals**: What are you learning about your analysis AS you write it?

**Your positioning**: How is your perspective shaping the narrative you're constructing?

## Recursive Writing: Expect Multiple Drafts

Phase 6 involves recursive writing. As Byrne notes (p. 1409): "As codes and themes change and evolve over the course of the analysis, so too can the write-up."

**Normal process**:
1. Draft initial theme narratives based on Phase 5 definitions
2. Realize some definitions need refinement → Return to Phase 5
3. Revise theme narratives
4. Notice some codes don't quite fit → Return to Phase 2/3
5. Revise affected sections
6. Continue refining until coherent, compelling narrative achieved

**Document changes**: Keep track of major revisions through:
- Version control (draft 1, draft 2, etc.)
- Change log noting what changed and why
- Reflexive memos about revisions

**This is not failure**: Recursive refinement is evidence of deep analytical engagement, not weak initial analysis.

## Common Pitfalls in Phase 6 and How to Avoid Them

### Pitfall 1: Descriptive Rather Than Analytical

**Problem**: Simply describing what participants said without interpreting what it means.

**Solution**: For every extract, ask: "So what? What's interesting about this? What does this reveal?" Connect to theory and broader patterns.

### Pitfall 2: Extract Overload

**Problem**: Presenting too many quotes without sufficient analysis.

**Solution**: Be selective. Choose the most vivid, compelling extracts. Make your analytical narrative more prominent than quoted material.

### Pitfall 3: Disconnected Themes

**Problem**: Themes presented as isolated findings without showing relationships.

**Solution**: Explicitly connect themes. Show how they build on each other or create productive tensions. Weave a coherent story.

### Pitfall 4: Ignoring Complexity

**Problem**: Presenting themes as if all participants agreed, missing diversity and nuance.

**Solution**: Show range within themes. Acknowledge contradictions. Explore tensions and variations.

### Pitfall 5: Weak Connection to Research Questions

**Problem**: Interesting themes that don't clearly address your RQs.

**Solution**: Explicitly connect each theme back to research questions. Show how the theme helps answer your study's central questions.

### Pitfall 6: Unclear AI Role

**Problem**: Not explaining how AI was used or making it seem like AI did the analysis.

**Solution**: Clear transparency statement in Methods. Throughout findings, make YOUR interpretive authority visible.

## Beyond Phase 6: Discussion and Conclusions

While Phase 6 focuses on the Findings/Results section, remember your write-up also needs:

### Discussion Section

**Synthesize across themes**: What's the overall story these themes tell together?

**Theoretical contributions**: What do your findings add to existing literature?

**Implications**: What do these findings mean for practice? For policy? For future research?

**Methodological reflection**: What did AI-augmented RTA reveal about this approach?

### Limitations Section

**Be honest about**: Sample characteristics, transferability, researcher positioning

**For AI-augmented work**: Acknowledge both affordances and limitations of AI assistance

**But don't undermine**: Frame limitations as inherent to qualitative epistemology, not flaws

### Conclusions Section

**Key findings**: Clear, concise summary

**Significance**: Why these findings matter

**Future directions**: What questions remain? What should future research explore?

## A Final Note on Writing as Analytical Craft

Phase 6 is where your analysis becomes public. The quality of your writing directly affects how your analysis is received and understood.

As Byrne demonstrates through his worked example, strong analytical writing:
- Makes interpretive work visible
- Constructs compelling arguments
- Engages meaningfully with theory
- Presents complex data clearly
- Honors participants' voices while offering analytical insight

Writing qualitative analysis is a craft that improves with practice. Each theme narrative you write teaches you something about analytical writing. Each revision deepens both your analysis and your ability to communicate it.

**The goal**: Tell the complicated story of your data in a way that convinces readers of the merit and validity of your analysis—while maintaining the epistemological integrity of reflexive thematic analysis.

When you've achieved this, Phase 6—and your RTA process—is complete.
