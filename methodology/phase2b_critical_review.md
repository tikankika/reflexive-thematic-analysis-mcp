# Phase 2b: Critical Coding Review — Dialogic RTA

## Purpose

Phase 2b is a **researcher-driven critical review** of AI-assisted coding. It sits between Phase 2a (initial coding) and Phase 3 (theme generation). Its purpose is to ensure that the researcher actively exercises interpretive authority over codes that were proposed or co-created with AI assistance.

Phase 2b is not a rubber-stamp. It is where the researcher becomes an **active analyst** — questioning, revising, and deepening the coding — rather than a passive validator of AI output.

---

## Theoretical Foundation

### Why Phase 2b exists in Dialogic RTA

In traditional (non-AI) reflexive thematic analysis, the researcher codes inductively by reading data closely and generating codes through interpretive engagement (Braun & Clarke, 2006, 2022). The iterative nature of coding — reading, coding, re-reading, revising — naturally builds the researcher's deep familiarity with the data.

When AI assists with initial coding (Phase 2a), a methodological tension arises. The AI has processing capacity — it can propose codes for large volumes of text efficiently. But RTA demands **researcher interpretive authority**: codes must reflect the researcher's analytical engagement, not algorithmic pattern matching (Braun & Clarke, 2019).

Phase 2b is where this tension is resolved. The researcher systematically reviews every coded segment, exercising the interpretive work that AI cannot perform.

### The "proving the obvious" problem

Brailas (2025) identifies a core risk when AI assists with qualitative analysis: the **"proving the obvious" problem** (drawing on Dröge, 2025). Large Language Models generate responses by selecting the most statistically probable continuation based on patterns in training data. This means:

- AI-generated codes tend to **reproduce dominant, expected patterns** rather than surface anomalies, contradictions, or contextually specific meanings
- AI outputs may give an **illusion of analytical depth** while actually reflecting common-sense generalizations already embedded in training data
- AI tends to **smooth over complexity** to produce central tendencies, treating irregular, ambiguous, or contextually unusual data points as statistical outliers

For qualitative research, this is significant because **the purpose is not to mirror common sense but to trouble it** (Brailas, 2025). The richness of qualitative data lies in what resists easy categorization — the awkward quote, the emotional turn, the silence that follows a sensitive question. These moments invite abductive reasoning: shifting attention from what is typical to what is strange, from pattern recognition to meaning reconstruction.

**Practical implication for Phase 2b:** Rather than only asking "did the AI code this correctly?", the researcher should also ask: **"What would the AI expect the data to say, and where does the actual data diverge from that expectation?"** This two-step approach transforms AI's predictive bias into a heuristic device for discovering what is genuinely interesting in the data.

### Double reflexivity

Ozuem et al. (2025) propose the RIPES model (Reflexivity, Interpretation, Procedural consistency, Evaluation, Situatedness) for thematic analysis in AI-driven contexts. A key insight is the need for **double reflexivity** when AI mediates analysis:

1. **Reflexivity toward one's own biases** — the researcher's theoretical positioning, assumptions, and interpretive lens (standard in all RTA)
2. **Reflexivity toward AI mediation** — how the AI tool's processing shapes, filters, or distorts the analytical encounter

This second layer is unique to dialogic work. The researcher must ask: Does this code reflect something genuinely present in the data, or has the AI imposed a pattern from its training data? Has the AI's "way of seeing" privileged formal regularities at the expense of interpretive nuance?

Brailas (2025) frames this as treating AI not as a "neutral instrument" but as an **"intra-active participant"** in meaning construction. The researcher–AI–data triad mutually constitutes the analytical process. Phase 2b is where the researcher makes this mutual constitution visible and exercises critical oversight.

### AI as heuristic partner, not authority

Multiple sources converge on a shared position about AI's proper role:

- Brailas (2025): AI should be used as a **"heuristic device"** and **"conversational partner in the pursuit of the unexpected"**, not an authoritative analyst
- Ozuem et al. (2025): AI can support but **"not supplant, the deeply interpretative, context-sensitive, and reflexive work"** of qualitative analysis
- Al-Fattal & Singh (2025): AI-assisted analysis offers efficiency but **"lacked interpretative depth"** and missed cultural/contextual nuances; best suited as **"a supplemental tool for preliminary theme exploration or as a secondary check on human analysis"**
- Braun & Clarke (2019, 2021): Themes (and by extension, codes) are actively **constructed** through interpretive engagement, not discovered in the data by any tool

Phase 2b operationalizes this position: AI's initial codes are treated as **proposals** that the researcher critically evaluates, revises, and deepens through their own interpretive engagement with the data.

### Connection to Braun & Clarke's iterative coding

Byrne (2022) demonstrates that initial coding in RTA involves **multiple iterations**:

- **Iteration 1:** Initial open coding — code liberally, inductively
- **Iteration 2:** Consolidation and refinement — collapse similar codes, check consistency, clarify definitions
- **Iteration 3:** Quality and theory check — alignment with theoretical position, appropriate semantic/latent balance, relevance to research questions

Phase 2b maps onto iterations 2 and 3 in a dialogic context. Where Byrne's iterations assume the same researcher re-reads and revises their own codes, Phase 2b specifically addresses the situation where initial codes were co-created with AI assistance and require the researcher's active critical engagement before proceeding to theme generation.

Byrne's quality check questions are directly applicable:

- **Is this a code or just a description?** Codes should capture patterns, not merely describe data items
- **What are its boundaries?** What does this code include and exclude?
- **Is there enough meaningful data?** Does the code apply to multiple data items, or is it idiosyncratic?
- **Are the data too diverse?** Do data items under this code share a coherent pattern?

## The Four Review Areas

For each coded segment, the researcher addresses four areas:

### 1. Segment Boundaries

- Is this segment a coherent semantic unit?
- Should it be split into smaller, more focused segments?
- Should it be merged with adjacent content that belongs to the same meaning unit?
- Does the segment boundary cut across a speaker's sustained argument or narrative?

*AI-specific consideration:* AI tends to work within technical chunk boundaries (60–100 lines) rather than meaning-driven boundaries. During Phase 2a, segments should already have been adjusted to content, but Phase 2b catches remaining cases where technical constraints shaped segmentation rather than meaning.

### 2. Code Accuracy

- Do the codes capture the meaning of this segment?
- Are any codes misapplied, too broad, or too narrow?
- Are there codes that describe content without capturing analytical meaning?
- Has the AI produced codes that sound plausible but miss the contextual specificity of what the participant is saying?

*AI-specific consideration:* AI codes may reflect **lexical patterns** (similar words across training data) rather than **situated meaning** (what this participant means in this context). A code that sounds correct at surface level may flatten important nuance. Ask: "If I read only the codes without seeing the data, would I understand what is distinctive about this segment?"

### 3. Code Sufficiency

- Are there meanings in this segment that are not captured by any code?
- Are there latent meanings (underlying assumptions, implicit tensions, unspoken norms) that the AI did not surface?
- Does this segment connect to other coded content in ways that need marking?

*AI-specific consideration:* This is where the "proving the obvious" problem is most acute. AI tends to surface **expected** patterns while missing what is genuinely surprising, contradictory, or contextually specific. Ask: "What would be the *expected* content here? Where does the actual data depart from that expectation?" Anomalies, silences, and contradictions are analytically valuable.

### 4. Analytical Observations

- What patterns am I noticing across segments? (without premature thematizing!)
- What surprises me in this segment?
- What feels uncertain or ambiguous?
- How does this connect to my research questions?
- What assumptions am I making in my reading of this segment?

*AI-specific consideration:* Document both layers of reflexivity — your analytical reactions to the data AND your assessment of how AI mediation may have shaped the coding.

## Avoiding Premature Thematization

Phase 2b is about **reviewing coding**, NOT about grouping codes into themes. That is Phase 3.

### Signs of premature thematization (avoid these in 2b)

- "This connects with X which forms a theme about..."
- "I see a pattern I'll call Y..."
- "These codes cluster around Z..."
- Creating hierarchical code categories

### Appropriate in Phase 2b

- "I notice several segments mention Z" (observation of recurrence)
- "Code X recurs — worth tracking" (pattern awareness)
- "This segment has similar content to segment 45" (connection)
- "This code seems to capture something distinct from code Y, even though they sound similar" (boundary work)
- "I'm surprised how many participants independently used the phrase '...'" (anomaly/convergence awareness)

The distinction is between **noticing** (appropriate) and **organizing** (premature). Phase 2b sharpens individual codes. Phase 3 will work with the relationships between them.

## Reflexive Note Format

For each reviewed segment, the researcher documents their review. The format should support genuine reflexive thinking, not formulaic completion.

### Core structure

```
## WHAT HAPPENS IN THE SEGMENT?
[Brief description — 1-3 sentences maximum. What is being said, by whom, in what context?]

## WHAT DO I NOTICE?
[Genuine analytical observations. What strikes me? What surprises me?
What is uncertain? What assumptions am I making?
Where does AI's coding feel right, and where does it miss something?]

## CHANGES MADE
[List any codes added, removed, or modified, with brief motivation]
[If no changes: "Reviewed — codes confirmed as adequate"]
```

### Guidance for genuine reflexivity

Reflexive notes are NOT:
- A formula applied identically to every segment
- Theoretical categorization ("This RELATES TO disruption theory because...")
- A summary that restates what the codes already say

Reflexive notes ARE documentation of the researcher's interpretive process. They should vary in focus and form based on what the segment evokes.

Questions to draw from (not a checklist — use what fits):
- What STRIKES me in this segment?
- What am I uncertain about?
- What assumptions am I making?
- What surprises me?
- What was difficult to code and why?
- Do I see preliminary patterns BEGINNING to emerge?
- How does this connect to earlier segments?
- What would the AI "expect" here, and where does the data diverge?
- Has my understanding of this topic shifted as I review?

Length varies: sometimes one sentence ("Strong formulation — must return to this"). Sometimes a full paragraph for complex segments. Let the segment dictate the depth.

## Concrete Workflow

### Overview

```
Phase 2a complete (all segments coded)
    │
    ▼
Phase 2b: CRITICAL CODING REVIEW
    │
    ├─ 1. Load first/next coded segment with its codes
    │
    ├─ 2. Read the segment text carefully
    │      (This is close reading, not skimming)
    │
    ├─ 3. Apply the four review areas:
    │      • Segment boundaries correct?
    │      • Codes accurate?
    │      • Codes sufficient?
    │      • Analytical observations?
    │
    ├─ 4. Make changes if needed:
    │      • Add missing codes
    │      • Remove incorrect/redundant codes
    │      • Modify code labels for precision
    │      • Split or merge segments
    │
    ├─ 5. Write reflexive note
    │
    ├─ 6. Move to next segment
    │
    └─ Repeat until all segments reviewed
         │
         ▼
    Phase 3: Generating themes
```

### Pace and depth

Phase 2b should not be rushed. Each segment deserves the researcher's genuine engagement. However, not all segments require equal time:

- **Rich, complex segments** with many codes or ambiguous content may need 5-10 minutes of careful review
- **Straightforward segments** where the coding is clearly adequate may need only 1-2 minutes to confirm
- **Segments where something feels "off"** deserve extra time — trust your analytical instinct

A full transcript with ~40-55 segments might take 3-6 hours for Phase 2b, spread across multiple sessions.

### Session management

Phase 2b can and should be done across multiple sessions. The tool tracks progress (which segments have been reviewed). When resuming:
- Read the last 2-3 reviewed segments' notes to re-orient yourself
- Continue from where you stopped
- It is normal and expected that your perspective may shift as you progress — earlier reviews can be revisited

## Practical Quality Questions (adapted from Byrne 2022)

Before concluding Phase 2b, check the coding against these questions:

### At the code level
- Is each code **analytically meaningful** (not just descriptive)?
- Does each code have **clear boundaries** (what it includes and excludes)?
- Is each code **well-named** (would another researcher understand what it captures)?
- Are in vivo codes appropriately marked and preserved?

### At the segment level
- Is each segment a **coherent semantic unit**?
- Do the codes assigned to each segment **collectively capture** what is analytically important in that segment?
- Are there segments with too many codes (might need splitting) or too few (might need deeper coding)?

### At the transcript level
- Is coding **consistent** across the transcript? (Same phenomena coded similarly in different places)
- Is there appropriate **semantic/latent balance**? (Not all surface-level, not all over-interpreted)
- Are all research questions addressed where relevant?
- Are there sections that were under-coded early on (when the researcher/AI was still finding the approach)?

### AI-specific quality check
- Are there codes that **sound right but lack contextual grounding**?
- Are there places where the AI may have **imposed expected patterns** rather than following the data?
- Has the coding captured **anomalies, contradictions, and surprises** — or only dominant patterns?
- Is there evidence of the "proving the obvious" problem in any coded sections?

---

## Connection to Methodological Integrity

Phase 2b ensures:

- **Credibility** — Codes accurately represent data, verified through researcher engagement
- **Dependability** — Coding process is documented and reviewable through reflexive notes
- **Confirmability** — Researcher decisions are transparent; AI's role is visible
- **Reflexivity** — Both interpretive and AI-mediation reflexivity are documented

The reflexive notes produced in Phase 2b serve as part of the **audit trail** — they document the researcher's analytical reasoning and demonstrate that the coding reflects genuine interpretive engagement rather than passive acceptance of AI output.
