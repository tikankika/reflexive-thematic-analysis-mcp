# Phase 4: Reviewing Themes in Dialogic RTA

## Purpose

Phase 4 is where candidate themes are critically tested and often substantially revised. It is not a confirmation exercise. As Byrne notes (2022, p. 1404):

> "At this phase, it is not uncommon to find that some candidate themes may not function well as meaningful interpretations of the data, or may not provide information that addresses the research question(s)."

The researcher interrogates every candidate theme against both the coded data and the full dataset. Themes that don't work are reconceptualized, collapsed, split, or discarded. Byrne's own example shows this concretely — he completely broke down one of his major candidate themes and rebuilt it as three separate themes with fundamentally different organizing concepts.

In dialogic contexts, Phase 4 carries an additional burden. If AI assisted with coding (Phase 2a) or theme generation (Phase 3), the researcher must now ask whether the thematic structure reflects genuine analytical insight or whether it has been shaped by AI's tendencies — its bias toward expected patterns, its preference for lexical grouping over meaning construction, its smoothing of complexity.

---

## Two Levels of Review

Braun and Clarke describe theme review as involving two levels, both essential.

### Level One: Coded data in relation to each theme

For each candidate theme, gather all the codes and data extracts that inform it. Re-read them together. The question: do these data items genuinely cohere around the central organizing concept?

**Possible outcomes:**

**The theme works.** Data items cohere meaningfully. The central organizing concept accurately captures the pattern. Move to Level Two.

**The theme needs adjustment.** Some data items don't fit. Options: remove misfit codes, recode some data (return to Phase 2), adjust the organizing concept, or split the theme.

**The theme doesn't work.** Data items are too diverse, the organizing concept is forced, or the pattern is actually several distinct patterns masquerading as one. Options: complete reconceptualization, discard and redistribute codes, or return to Phase 3.

**AI-specific concern at Level One:** AI-proposed theme clusters may have internal coherence at the lexical level (codes contain similar words) without internal coherence at the meaning level (codes capture different situated meanings that happen to use similar language). The researcher must read the actual data extracts — not just the code labels — to assess whether coherence is genuine.

### Level Two: Themes in relation to the entire dataset

Step back from individual themes. Consider the thematic framework as a whole. Re-read the entire dataset.

**The questions:**

- Do the themes together provide a convincing interpretation of the dataset?
- Do they address the research questions adequately?
- Are there gaps — important patterns that no theme captures?
- Are there redundancies — themes that overlap without clear distinction?
- Is each theme clearly distinguishable from the others?
- Are theme boundaries clear and defensible?

**AI-specific concern at Level Two:** If AI influenced theme generation, the overall thematic structure may mirror expected frameworks from the literature — the kind of structure AI's training data would predict. The researcher should ask: does this framework capture what is distinctive about *this* data, or does it look like a generic analysis of this topic? The "proving the obvious" problem operates at the framework level, not just the individual theme level.

## Patton's Dual Criteria

Braun and Clarke reference Patton's (1990) criteria for evaluating categories:

**Internal homogeneity.** Within each theme, data and codes should cohere meaningfully. They belong together because they share an underlying pattern — not just because they are about the same general topic.

*Red flags:* You struggle to articulate what holds the theme together. Some codes feel forced. The theme tries to capture too many different patterns.

**External heterogeneity.** Between themes, there should be clear distinctions. Each theme captures something distinctive.

*Red flags:* Two themes overlap significantly. You struggle to distinguish between them. Theme boundaries are fuzzy or arbitrary.

**In AI-augmented contexts:** AI-generated clusters may satisfy internal homogeneity at a surface level (similar words grouped together) while failing at a meaning level. And AI may produce themes that are externally heterogeneous in topic but not in analytical depth — five themes that each describe a different aspect of the phenomenon without any of them interpreting what those aspects *mean*.

## Byrne's Example: Substantial Restructuring

Byrne's Phase 4 demonstrates what genuine review looks like — not confirmation, but reconceptualization.

### Level One revision: Recognising Educator Wellbeing

Original sub-theme structure: sources of negative affect; actions to improve educator wellbeing.

On review, Byrne realized the original structure treated all sources of negative affect as equivalent. But the data showed something more specific: pre-existing work-related factors caused negative affect, and the introduction of wellbeing promotion *compounded* these pre-existing issues. The trigger for reconceptualization was noticing that participants weren't listing separate problems — they were describing a dynamic where one thing made another worse. Wellbeing promotion wasn't just another negative factor — it specifically functioned as something that intensified existing problems.

Revised sub-themes: work-related negative affect; the influence of wellbeing promotion.

The revision captured a more nuanced pattern that the original structure missed.

### Level Two revision: Breaking down "Factors Inhibiting Wellbeing Promotion"

This was Byrne's most dramatic revision. The original theme gathered everything participants saw as barriers — lack of training, time constraints, lack of value, knowledge of documents. It was the biggest and most obvious cluster.

**The problems:** Very dense and unwieldy. Boundaries difficult to identify. Lacked coherence — the sub-themes were quite disparate. Too descriptive — listing barriers without interpreting what those barriers *mean*.

**The solution:** Complete reconceptualization into three separate themes:

1. **"The influence of time"** — moved beyond describing time as a barrier to capturing the bidirectional nature: pre-existing constraints affected wellbeing promotion, and wellbeing promotion created time constraints for other activities. Not just "time is a problem" but "here's how time operates in this context."

2. **"Incompletely theorised agreements"** — reconceptualized "lack of training" and "knowledge of documents" into a pattern: educators engaged with wellbeing promotion in an atheoretical manner, proceeding on practical agreements without theoretical grounding. Far more interpretive than listing what was lacking.

3. **"The axiology of wellbeing promotion"** — reconceptualized "lack of value" into something more complex: different perceptions of value across educators and students, with value judgments that weren't necessarily negative. An examination of value itself, not just its absence.

**What this shows:** The most obvious cluster — everything that inhibits something — was the least analytically productive organization. Breaking it apart and reconceptualizing each piece produced themes with genuine interpretive depth. Phase 4 is where this kind of analytical work happens.

**The AI-augmented lesson:** AI would reliably produce a "barriers" theme. It is the expected structure. The analytical insight was recognizing that "barriers" was not a theme but a topic label containing several distinct patterns that each deserved their own interpretive treatment. This is precisely the kind of reconceptualization that AI will not propose — it requires the researcher to see past the obvious structure to the deeper analytical possibilities.

## The Specific Risk of AI Confirmation Bias

Phase 4 introduces a risk specific to AI-augmented analysis: **AI confirmation of weak themes.**

If the researcher asks AI to evaluate a candidate theme, AI is likely to confirm it — because AI's tendency is to generate coherent, affirmative responses. Ask "does this theme work?" and AI will usually say yes, perhaps with minor suggestions. This is not critical engagement. It is computational agreeableness.

**The problem compounds when AI helped generate the themes.** If AI proposed the clusters in Phase 3 and now "confirms" them in Phase 4, the researcher has not gained an independent check — they have created a feedback loop where AI validates its own output.

**Countermeasures:**

- **Ask AI to argue against the theme.** "What is the strongest case that this theme doesn't work?" forces AI to generate critique rather than confirmation.
- **Ask AI for alternative structures.** "What other ways could these codes be organized?" surfaces possibilities the researcher might not have considered.
- **Use AI to test external heterogeneity.** "What makes Theme A and Theme B genuinely different?" — if AI struggles to articulate the distinction, the themes may overlap too much.
- **Don't ask AI "does this work?"** Ask specific analytical questions that require substantive answers rather than validation.

But the deepest countermeasure is the researcher's own critical engagement with the data. Re-read the actual data extracts, not just code labels. The data itself is the primary check on thematic structure — not AI's assessment of it.

---

## Recursive Movement Between Phases

Phase 4 review often necessitates returning to earlier phases:

- **Return to Phase 3** if themes need fundamental reconceptualization or new themes must be generated
- **Return to Phase 2** if data items need recoding or new patterns emerge requiring additional codes
- **Return to Phase 1** if contextual understanding needs checking

In dialogic contexts, returning to Phase 2 has an additional dimension: re-reading AI-coded segments with the interpretive perspective gained through theme development. The researcher may now see things in the data that neither they nor AI captured in initial coding.

---

## Quality Indicators

Phase 4 is complete when:

**Individual themes:**
- Each theme tells a coherent story (not just labels a topic)
- Central organizing concept is clear and compelling
- Internal homogeneity is genuine (meaning-level, not just lexical)
- Sufficient meaningful data supports each theme
- Theme boundaries are clear and defensible

**The thematic framework:**
- Themes are clearly distinctive from each other
- Together they provide comprehensive interpretation of the dataset
- Framework addresses research questions
- No major gaps or redundancies
- Relationships between themes are clear

**AI-specific quality:**
- The framework does not merely reproduce an expected structure from the literature
- Themes that AI proposed have been tested against the data, not just accepted
- The researcher can articulate what is distinctive about this analysis — what wouldn't be predicted from the topic alone
- Double reflexivity is documented: the researcher has reflected on both their own analytical choices and AI's influence on the thematic structure

---

Phase 5 will define each theme precisely and craft final theme names. But the structural work — building a coherent, analytically productive thematic framework — happens here.
