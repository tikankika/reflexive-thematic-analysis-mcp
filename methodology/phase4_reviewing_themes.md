# Phase 4: Reviewing Potential Themes in AI-Augmented RTA
## Based on Byrne (2022)

---

## The Critical Work of Review and Refinement

Phase 4 is where the significant analytical work of theme refinement happens. As Byrne notes (2022, p. 1404): "At this phase, it is not uncommon to find that some candidate themes may not function well as meaningful interpretations of the data, or may not provide information that addresses the research question(s)."

This is NOT a mechanical checking process. Phase 4 requires you to critically interrogate your candidate themes and, often, fundamentally reconceptualize how you're organizing meaning in your data. Byrne's own example shows this—he completely broke down one of his major candidate themes and reconceptualized it as three separate themes with different organizing concepts.

Don't expect to simply "confirm" your Phase 3 themes. Expect substantial revision, restructuring, and deeper interpretive work.

---

## Braun & Clarke's Key Questions for Theme Review

Before diving into the review process, Braun and Clarke (2012, p. 65) propose a series of key questions you should ask about each candidate theme:

**1. Is this a theme (or is it just a code)?**
Sometimes what you've called a theme is actually just a code—it's too narrow, too specific, or doesn't organize multiple codes around a central concept.

**2. If it is a theme, what is the quality of this theme?**
Does it tell you something useful about the dataset and your research questions? Is it meaningful and informative?

**3. What are the boundaries of this theme?**
What does it include and exclude? Can you clearly articulate where this theme begins and ends? Are the boundaries clear and defensible?

**4. Are there enough meaningful data to support this theme?**
Is the theme "thin" (insufficient data support) or "thick" (rich, detailed data support)? Remember: meaningfulness matters more than frequency.

**5. Are the data too diverse and wide ranging?**
Does the theme lack coherence? Are you forcing together data items that don't genuinely share an underlying pattern?

Keep these questions in mind as you move through the two-level review process.

## Two Levels of Review

Braun and Clarke describe theme review as involving two levels, which Byrne (p. 1404) implements systematically.

### Level One: Reviewing Coded Data in Relation to Each Theme

At this level, you review all the coded data extracts that inform each candidate theme. You're checking whether the data items grouped under each theme actually form a coherent pattern.

**The process**:
1. For each candidate theme, gather all the codes and data extracts that inform it
2. Re-read all these data extracts together
3. Ask: Do these data items genuinely cohere around the central organizing concept?
4. Check for internal homogeneity (do they belong together?) and internal consistency (do they tell a coherent story?)

**Possible outcomes**:

**The theme works well**: The data items cohere meaningfully, and the theme tells a clear story. Move forward with refinement.

**The theme needs adjustment**: Some data items don't quite fit. You might need to:
- Remove data items/codes that don't genuinely belong
- Recode some data items (return to Phase 2)
- Adjust the central organizing concept to better capture the actual pattern
- Split the theme into multiple themes

**The theme doesn't work**: The data items are too diverse and don't form a coherent pattern. You might need to:
- Completely reconceptualize the theme
- Discard the theme and redistribute codes to other themes
- Return to Phase 3 to generate new themes

### Level Two: Reviewing Themes in Relation to the Entire Dataset

At this level, you step back and look at how your themes work in relation to your entire dataset and your research questions.

**The process**:
1. Consider all your themes together
2. Re-read your entire dataset (all transcripts)
3. Ask: Do these themes accurately reflect the meanings evident in the data as a whole?
4. Check for external heterogeneity (are themes distinctive from each other?)

**Key questions for Level Two**:

**About the overall thematic framework**:
- Do the themes together provide a convincing interpretation of the dataset?
- Do they address your research questions adequately?
- Are there gaps—important patterns in the data that no theme captures?
- Are there redundancies—themes that overlap too much?

**About individual themes**:
- Are themes clearly distinguishable from each other?
- Is each theme making a unique contribution to the analysis?
- Are theme boundaries clear and defensible?

**About your theoretical alignment**:
- Do themes honor your constructionist epistemology?
- Is experiential orientation maintained?
- Are themes inductively grounded while remaining relevant to RQs?

**Possible outcomes**:

**The framework works well**: Themes are distinctive, coherent, and together tell a meaningful story about your data. Move to Phase 5 (defining and naming).

**The framework needs refinement**: Some themes need adjustment. You might need to:
- Collapse themes that are too similar
- Split themes that are trying to do too much
- Reorganize sub-themes
- Reconceptualize relationships between themes
- Return to Phase 3 (or even Phase 2) for further work

## Byrne's Example: Substantial Restructuring in Phase 4

Byrne's worked example provides an excellent illustration of how substantial the revisions in Phase 4 can be. Looking at his Figure 1 (initial candidate themes, p. 1403) versus Figure 2 (finalized themes, p. 1405) shows major changes.

### Level One Review: Reconceptualizing "Recognising Educator Wellbeing"

During Level One review of his theme "Recognising educator wellbeing," Byrne made an important reinterpretation (p. 1405):

**Original sub-theme structure**:
- Sources of negative affect
- Actions to improve educator wellbeing

**The problem**: As he reviewed the data extracts, he realized the original structure missed a crucial pattern. Participants communicated that:
1. Pre-existing work-related factors caused negative affect
2. The introduction of wellbeing promotion *compounded* these pre-existing issues

**The revision**: He split "sources of negative affect" into two sub-themes:
- "Work-related negative affect" (pre-existing issues)
- "The influence of wellbeing promotion" (compounding factor)

This revision captured a more nuanced pattern—wellbeing promotion wasn't just another negative factor, it was specifically functioning as something that intensified existing problems. The new structure told a more accurate story about the data.

### Level Two Review: Breaking Down "Factors Inhibiting Wellbeing Promotion"

During Level Two review, Byrne confronted a major problem with one of his candidate themes.

**The problematic candidate theme**: "Factors inhibiting wellbeing promotion"
- Sub-themes: Lack of training, Time constraints, Lack of value, Knowledge of documents
- Brought together everything that participants saw as barriers

**The problems identified**:
- Very dense and unwieldy (too much packed into one theme)
- Boundaries were difficult to identify—what did it include and exclude?
- Lacked coherence—the sub-themes were quite disparate
- Too descriptive—just listing barriers without deeper interpretation

**The solution**: Complete reconceptualization into three separate themes:

**1. "The influence of time"** (new theme)
Moved beyond just describing time constraints as a barrier. Captured the bi-directional nature:
- Pre-existing time constraints affected wellbeing promotion
- Wellbeing promotion *created* time constraints for other activities
This added analytical depth—not just "time is a problem" but "here's how time operates in this context"

**2. "Incompletely theorised agreements"** (new theme)
Took the "lack of training" and "knowledge of documents" sub-themes and completely reconceptualized them. Rather than just describing what was lacking, this theme captured a pattern: educators were engaging with wellbeing promotion in an atheoretical manner—they didn't have theoretical grounding but proceeded anyway based on practical agreements.
More interpretive, more analytical.

**3. "The axiology of wellbeing promotion"** (new theme)
Completely reconceptualized the "lack of value" sub-theme. Through closer examination, Byrne realized:
- This wasn't just about value being lacking
- Participants held different perceptions about value ascribed by educators vs. students
- Value judgments weren't necessarily negative
- The pattern was more complex than original theme suggested

Sub-themes distinguished:
- Students' valuation of wellbeing promotion
- Educators' valuation of wellbeing promotion

**What this example shows**:
- Phase 4 review can lead to fundamental reconceptualization
- Moving from descriptive to interpretive is crucial
- Sometimes you realize your initial organization missed the actual pattern
- This is normal and expected—not a sign of failure

## Observing Patton's Dual Criteria

Braun and Clarke (via Patton 1990) suggest observing two criteria when reviewing themes:

### Internal Homogeneity

**Within each theme**, data items and codes should cohere meaningfully. They should genuinely belong together because they share an underlying pattern, not just because they're about the same general topic.

**Check**:
- Do the codes within this theme actually share a core commonality?
- Or am I forcing disparate things together?
- Is the central organizing concept accurately capturing what unites these codes?

**Red flags**:
- You struggle to articulate what holds the theme together
- Some codes feel like they don't quite belong
- The theme tries to do too much (capture too many different patterns)

### External Heterogeneity

**Between themes**, there should be clear distinctions. Each theme should capture something distinctive. If two themes are really saying the same thing in different words, they should probably be collapsed.

**Check**:
- Is each theme telling a different story?
- Can I clearly articulate how themes differ from each other?
- Are theme boundaries clear and defensible?

**Red flags**:
- Two themes overlap significantly
- You struggle to distinguish between themes
- Theme boundaries are fuzzy or arbitrary

## Practical Strategies for Review

### Strategy 1: Re-read With Fresh Eyes

Distance yourself briefly from your analysis, then return and re-read all coded data for each theme. You might notice patterns or problems you didn't see initially.

### Strategy 2: Write Theme Descriptions

Try writing a paragraph describing each theme—what it's about, what its central organizing concept is, which codes it encompasses. If you can't write a clear, coherent description, the theme probably needs work.

### Strategy 3: Visualize Theme Relationships

Update your thematic map as you make revisions. Seeing the structure visually can help identify problems:
- Themes that overlap too much
- Themes that are too isolated from others
- Gaps in your framework

### Strategy 4: Check Against Research Questions

Explicitly check each theme against your research questions:
- Does this theme help answer my RQs?
- Am I spending analytical energy on themes that don't contribute to my research aims?
- Are there aspects of my RQs that no theme addresses?

### Strategy 5: The "Stand Alone" Test

Each theme should be able to stand alone as a coherent story while also contributing to the overall narrative. Try reading just one theme's data in isolation:
- Does it make sense on its own?
- Does it tell a clear, coherent story?
- Can you articulate its contribution to your analysis?

## Using AI in Phase 4 Review

AI can be particularly useful as a dialogic partner during theme review, helping you interrogate your themes critically.

### Checking Theme Coherence

```
I have this candidate theme:

Theme: [name]
Central organizing concept: [concept]
Codes included: [list]

Please evaluate:
1. Do these codes genuinely cohere around this central concept?
2. Are there codes that don't quite fit?
3. Is the central organizing concept accurately capturing the pattern?
4. What might be a more precise articulation of the core pattern?
```

### Testing Theme Distinctiveness

```
I have these two candidate themes that I'm concerned might overlap:

Theme A: [description + codes]
Theme B: [description + codes]

Questions:
1. Are these genuinely distinct patterns or variations of the same pattern?
2. If they should be collapsed, what broader organizing concept would unite them?
3. If they should stay separate, how would you articulate the key distinction?
```

### Identifying Theme Boundaries

```
I'm struggling to define the boundaries of this theme:

Theme: [description]

What it seems to include: [description]
What I'm uncertain about including: [list items]

Help me think through:
1. What should the boundaries of this theme be?
2. What clear inclusion/exclusion criteria would work?
```

### Checking for Gaps

```
Here are my current themes: [list with brief descriptions]

Given the research questions: [your RQs]

Are there apparent gaps? Patterns or aspects that these themes don't capture but that seem important for addressing the research questions?
```

### Challenging Your Interpretations

```
I've conceptualized this theme as: [description]

Can you suggest alternative ways to interpret this pattern? 
What if I'm organizing these codes in a way that misses the actual underlying pattern?
```

### Critical Engagement Required

As always with AI use, you must critically evaluate all suggestions:

**Document your review dialogue**:
```markdown
## Phase 4 Review: AI Engagement

Theme reviewed: [theme name]

AI suggestions:
- [suggestion 1]
- [suggestion 2]

My evaluation:
[Which suggestions were helpful and why]
[Which suggestions I rejected and why]
[How the dialogue pushed my thinking]

Decisions made:
[Specific revisions to theme based on review]
```

The AI dialogue should push your thinking, not determine your themes. You maintain interpretive authority.

## Recursive Movement Between Phases

Phase 4 review often necessitates returning to earlier phases:

**Return to Phase 3** (theme generation):
- If you realize themes need fundamental reconceptualization
- If you identify gaps and need to generate new themes
- If themes need to be collapsed or split

**Return to Phase 2** (coding):
- If data items need recoding
- If you realize codes weren't applied consistently
- If new patterns emerge requiring additional codes

**Return to Phase 1** (familiarization):
- If you need to check contextual understanding
- If you're uncertain about interpretations and need to revisit raw data

**This is normal and expected**. Byrne emphasizes: "RTA is a recursive process and it is rare that a researcher would follow a linear path through the six phases" (p. 1400).

Don't see returning to earlier phases as failure. It's evidence of deep, thoughtful analytical engagement.

## Quality Indicators: Knowing When Phase 4 is Complete

You're ready to move to Phase 5 when:

**About individual themes**:
✓ Each theme tells a coherent story
✓ Central organizing concept is clear and compelling
✓ Codes within each theme genuinely cohere (internal homogeneity)
✓ Sufficient meaningful data supports each theme
✓ Theme boundaries are clear and defensible

**About the thematic framework**:
✓ Themes are clearly distinctive from each other (external heterogeneity)
✓ Themes together provide comprehensive interpretation of dataset
✓ Framework addresses your research questions
✓ No major gaps or redundancies
✓ Relationships between themes are clear

**About theoretical alignment**:
✓ Themes align with constructionist epistemology
✓ Experiential orientation maintained
✓ Predominantly inductive grounding
✓ Appropriate interpretive depth (not just descriptive)

**About process**:
✓ Both levels of review completed for all themes
✓ Revisions documented thoroughly
✓ Updated thematic map created
✓ Reflexive memos written

**You may still have refinements ahead** (Phase 5 involves defining themes precisely), but the fundamental structure should be solid.

## Common Issues in Phase 4 and Solutions

### Issue 1: Reluctance to Make Major Changes

After investing significant work in Phase 3, it can be psychologically difficult to completely restructure themes.

**Remember**: Major restructuring is evidence of deep analytical thinking, not wasted effort. Byrne completely reconceptualized a major theme. That's normal and valuable.

**Solution**: Trust the review process. If something isn't working, address it now rather than carrying a weak theme forward.

### Issue 2: Losing Sight of Theoretical Framework

In the detailed work of reviewing codes and data, you might lose sight of your constructionist, experiential, inductive framework.

**Solution**: Regularly step back and explicitly check theoretical alignment. Your epistemology should guide interpretation throughout.

### Issue 3: Becoming Too Attached to Provisional Themes

You might resist changing themes because you've become attached to your initial conceptualization.

**Solution**: Remember Phase 3 themes are provisional by definition. They're working hypotheses to be tested and refined, not final answers to defend.

### Issue 4: Over-Relying on AI Suggestions

If using AI, you might lean too heavily on its suggestions rather than your own interpretive judgment.

**Solution**: Remember AI is a dialogic tool, not an authority. Your interpretive engagement with your data is what matters. Document where you disagree with AI and why.

## Byrne's Finalized Thematic Framework

After Phase 4 review, Byrne's final framework (Figure 2, p. 1405) consisted of five themes:

**1. Best Practice in Wellbeing Promotion**
(Survived from Phase 3 with refinements)
- Sub-themes: The whole-school approach, Active delivery of wellbeing promotion

**2. Recognising Educator Wellbeing**
(Substantially revised)
- Sub-themes: Work-related negative affect, The influence of wellbeing promotion

**3. The Influence of Time**
(New theme from reconceptualization)

**4. Incompletely Theorised Agreements**
(New theme from reconceptualization)

**5. The Axiology of Wellbeing Promotion**
(New theme from reconceptualization)
- Sub-themes: Students' valuation, Educators' valuation

**Key changes from Phase 3**:
- One theme (Best Practice) survived relatively intact
- One theme (Recognising Educator Wellbeing) substantially revised
- Original Theme 3 (Factors Inhibiting) completely broken down into three new themes
- More interpretive depth throughout
- Clearer theme boundaries and relationships

This shows how Phase 4 review transforms provisional themes into a robust analytical framework.

## Preparing for Phase 5: Defining and Naming

At the end of Phase 4, you should have:

✓ A refined thematic framework with clear theme boundaries
✓ Documented revisions showing what changed and why
✓ An updated thematic map
✓ Confidence that themes work both individually and as a framework
✓ Reflexive documentation of the review process

**You're ready for Phase 5** when your themes are structurally sound. Phase 5 involves defining each theme precisely (what it's about, what it's not about, what story it tells) and crafting final theme names.

But the hard structural work of building a coherent thematic framework happens in Phase 4. This is where weak themes are strengthened, problematic themes are reconceptualized, and your analysis gains rigor and depth.

---

## A Final Note on the Importance of Phase 4

Byrne's worked example makes clear that Phase 4 isn't a formality or a quick check. It's where substantial analytical work happens. His willingness to completely reconceptualize a major theme demonstrates the depth of critical engagement Phase 4 requires.

This level of review and willingness to make major changes is what distinguishes reflexive thematic analysis from more superficial approaches. The themes you end up with should be genuinely earned through careful, critical, recursive analytical work—not just the first ideas you had in Phase 3.

As Braun and Clarke emphasize, reflexive TA is about "the researcher's reflective and thoughtful engagement with their data and their reflexive and thoughtful engagement with the analytic process" (2019, p. 594).

Phase 4 is where that thoughtful, reflexive engagement becomes especially visible.
