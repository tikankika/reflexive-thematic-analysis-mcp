# AI-Augmented Reflexive Thematic Analysis: Methodological Framework

## Purpose of This Document

This document establishes the epistemological and methodological foundations for conducting Reflexive Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006, 2022) with AI as a dialogic partner. It articulates the principles that govern all six phases of analysis. Individual phase documents (Phase 1–6) operationalize these principles in their specific contexts but do not repeat the foundational arguments made here.

Researchers should read this document before beginning any phase.

---

## What Is AI-Augmented RTA?

Reflexive Thematic Analysis is a qualitative method for constructing themes — patterns of shared meaning — from data through the researcher's active interpretive engagement (Braun & Clarke, 2019, 2022). AI-augmented RTA uses large language models as dialogic partners in this process, while maintaining human interpretive authority throughout.

This is not a neutral addition. AI mediation changes the conditions under which analysis occurs. It introduces a processing layer with its own biases, tendencies, and blind spots that interact with the researcher's analytical work. A responsible methodology must theorize this interaction, not merely acknowledge it.

---

## Three Foundational Principles

### 1. The "Proving the Obvious" Problem

Brailas (2025), drawing on Dröge (2025), identifies a core risk in AI-augmented qualitative analysis. Large Language Models generate responses by selecting the most statistically probable continuation based on patterns in training data. The consequences for qualitative research are significant:

- AI-generated codes and thematic suggestions tend to **reproduce dominant, expected patterns** rather than surface anomalies, contradictions, or contextually specific meanings
- AI outputs may give an **illusion of analytical depth** while reflecting common-sense generalizations already embedded in training data
- AI tends to **smooth over complexity**, treating irregular, ambiguous, or contextually unusual data as statistical outliers rather than analytically interesting moments

For qualitative research, this matters because **the purpose of analysis is not to mirror common sense but to trouble it** (Brailas, 2025). The richness of qualitative data lies in what resists easy categorization — the awkward quote, the emotional turn, the silence that follows a sensitive question. These moments invite abductive reasoning: shifting attention from what is typical to what is strange, from pattern recognition to meaning reconstruction.

**The operational implication is a single question that should accompany every analytical decision across all phases:**

> "What would AI expect the data to say — and where does the actual data diverge from that expectation?"

This question transforms AI's predictive bias from a liability into a heuristic device. By explicitly articulating the expected pattern, the researcher gains a reference point against which to identify what is genuinely interesting, surprising, or contradictory in the data. AI's tendency toward the obvious becomes a tool for discovering the non-obvious.

### 2. Double Reflexivity

Standard reflexive practice in RTA involves the researcher examining their own biases, assumptions, and interpretive lens (Braun & Clarke, 2019, 2022). AI-augmented analysis requires a second layer.

Ozuem et al. (2025) propose that researchers in AI-driven contexts must exercise **double reflexivity**:

1. **Reflexivity toward one's own positioning** — the researcher's theoretical commitments, assumptions, and interpretive habits (standard in all RTA)
2. **Reflexivity toward AI mediation** — how the AI tool's processing shapes, filters, or distorts the analytical encounter

The second layer asks: Does this code, theme, or interpretation reflect something genuinely present in the data, or has AI's "way of seeing" imposed a pattern from its training data? Has AI privileged formal regularities at the expense of interpretive nuance? Has the researcher accepted an AI suggestion because it sounded plausible rather than because it captured meaning?

Brailas (2025) frames AI not as a "neutral instrument" but as an **"intra-active participant"** in meaning construction. The researcher–AI–data triad mutually constitutes the analytical process. Double reflexivity makes this mutual constitution visible and subjects it to critical oversight.

**This applies at every phase:**
- **Phase 2a**: Does this code capture situated meaning or lexical pattern?
- **Phase 2b**: Has AI's coding shaped what I now see in the data?
- **Phase 3**: Are these theme clusters driven by analytical insight or AI's tendency to group similar surface features?
- **Phase 4**: Am I accepting a weak theme because AI "confirmed" it?
- **Phase 5**: Is this theme name analytically precise or generically plausible?
- **Phase 6**: Does this analytical narrative have my voice or AI's?

### 3. AI as Heuristic Partner, Not Authority

Multiple sources converge on a shared position about AI's role in qualitative analysis:

- Brailas (2025): AI as **"heuristic device"** and **"conversational partner in the pursuit of the unexpected"**, not authoritative analyst
- Ozuem et al. (2025): AI can support but **"not supplant, the deeply interpretative, context-sensitive, and reflexive work"** of qualitative analysis
- Al-Fattal & Singh (2025): AI-assisted analysis offers efficiency but **"lacked interpretative depth"** and missed cultural and contextual nuances; best suited as **"a supplemental tool for preliminary theme exploration or as a secondary check on human analysis"**
- Braun & Clarke (2019, 2021): Themes and codes are actively **constructed** through interpretive engagement, not discovered in the data by any tool

The position is clear: AI proposes, the researcher disposes. AI's outputs are starting points for interpretive engagement, not findings. The researcher's interpretive authority is not ceremonial — it is the methodological core of RTA.

This is not a limitation to be managed but a feature to be exploited. The dialogue between researcher and AI — where proposals are evaluated, challenged, revised, and sometimes rejected — makes interpretive reasoning more explicit than purely human coding often does. The researcher must articulate *why* a suggestion works or doesn't, which strengthens the reflexive audit trail.

## Epistemological Foundation

### Constructionist Epistemology

This methodology is designed for constructionist analysis, where meaning is understood as socially produced and reproduced through language — not simply reflected by it (Braun & Clarke, 2006; Byrne, 2022).

When participants discuss their experiences, these accounts are not transparent windows onto objective reality. They are constructions shaped by social interaction, institutional context, cultural frameworks, and the language available to participants. The researcher's codes and themes are likewise constructions — interpretive acts, not discoveries.

**For AI-augmented work, this has a specific implication.** AI's computational pattern recognition operates through statistical probabilities derived from training data. From a constructionist perspective, this risks reifying interpretations as though they were objective or generalizable, "flattening the nuance and plurality that constructivist thematic analysis seeks to preserve" (Ozuem et al., 2025). The methodology must actively resist this flattening.

### Experiential Orientation

An experiential orientation prioritizes participants' own meaning-making and lived experience (Braun & Clarke, 2014; Byrne, 2022). The researcher cedes interpretive authority to participants regarding what their experiences mean to them.

**For AI-augmented work:** AI cannot access experiential meaning. It processes text. The gap between textual patterns and experiential meaning is precisely where the researcher's interpretive work is most essential — and most at risk of being displaced by AI's surface-level pattern matching.

### Predominantly Inductive Approach

A predominantly inductive approach means codes and themes grow from the data rather than from predetermined theoretical frameworks (Byrne, 2022). Research questions define the domain of inquiry but do not prescribe what will be found.

**For AI-augmented work:** AI's training data constitutes a massive implicit framework. When AI codes inductively, it inevitably brings patterns from that framework. This is why "proving the obvious" is an inductive concern, not just a deductive one — AI's "induction" may be pseudo-induction, where training-data patterns masquerade as data-driven discoveries.

### Semantic and Latent Coding

Both semantic coding (explicit, surface meanings) and latent coding (underlying assumptions, implicit tensions, unspoken norms) are employed without prioritizing one over the other (Byrne, 2022).

**For AI-augmented work:** AI performs better at semantic coding than latent coding. Semantic patterns — recurring words, similar phrasings, explicit topic clusters — are computationally accessible. Latent patterns — what is not said, what is assumed, what contradicts the surface — require interpretive sensitivity that AI lacks. Phase 2b's review specifically targets this asymmetry.

## The Six Phases in AI-Augmented Context

RTA comprises six recursive, non-linear phases (Braun & Clarke, 2006, 2022; Byrne, 2022). AI augmentation changes how each phase is conducted but not what each phase must accomplish.

| Phase | Purpose | AI Role | Key Risk |
|-------|---------|---------|----------|
| 1. Familiarization | Researcher establishes intimate knowledge of data | AI is *oriented* to project parameters; scaffolds human immersion | Outsourcing immersion to AI summaries |
| 2a. Initial Coding | Generate codes through close reading | AI proposes codes; researcher evaluates | Accepting plausible but shallow codes |
| 2b. Critical Review | Researcher exercises interpretive authority over coding | AI codes treated as proposals under review | Rubber-stamping AI output |
| 3. Generating Themes | Construct themes from code patterns | AI suggests clusters; researcher constructs meaning | Reproducing expected thematic structures |
| 4. Reviewing Themes | Test themes against data and research questions | AI helps interrogate; researcher decides | AI confirming weak themes |
| 5. Defining & Naming | Articulate what each theme captures | AI assists definition; researcher owns analytical language | Generic AI language displacing researcher's voice |
| 6. Producing Report | Write analytical narrative | AI assists drafting; researcher maintains analytical voice | Flattened, AI-smoothed prose lacking interpretive depth |

**Recursivity is fundamental.** The researcher moves between phases as understanding deepens. In AI-augmented contexts, recursivity has an additional dimension: the researcher may need to revisit AI-coded segments with fresh interpretive eyes, precisely because initial engagement was mediated by AI's proposals.

**Phase 1 is different in kind from Phases 2–6.** It is preparatory, not analytical. It creates the conditions for AI-augmented analysis by establishing the researcher's independent relationship with the data. Phases 2–6 are operational — they describe how analysis proceeds with AI as dialogic partner. Phase 1 ensures the researcher enters that dialogue from a position of interpretive strength.

**Project-specific coding conventions** — code formatting, research question mapping, language rules, and segment structure — are defined in the separate Coding Protocols (see `../protocols/`), which operationalize the principles established here for specific research projects. The phase documents describe *how to think* about analysis; the Coding Protocols describe *how to format* that thinking for consistent implementation.

## What AI Cannot Access

Across all phases, certain analytical capacities remain exclusively human:

- **Contextual and cultural meaning**: Local institutional cultures, policy contexts, professional discourse norms, cultural connotations of specific terms
- **Experiential weight**: Which statements carry strong emotion, hesitation, or conviction; what is said tentatively versus emphatically
- **Group dynamics**: Who speaks and who doesn't, how participants build on or challenge each other, what is safe to say in a given group
- **Silence and absence**: What is not said, what topics are avoided, where conversation redirects
- **Contradictions and ambivalence**: Internal tensions in reasoning, "yes, but..." moments, positions held simultaneously
- **Language beyond text**: Tone, pauses, laughter, emphasis — available from recordings but invisible to text-processing AI

These are not marginal concerns. They are often where the most analytically interesting data resides.

---

## Methodological Integrity in AI-Augmented Analysis

AI augmentation does not weaken methodological integrity — it makes integrity more visible. In traditional RTA, much of the researcher's interpretive reasoning remains implicit. In AI-augmented RTA, the dialogue between researcher and AI externalizes that reasoning: every acceptance, modification, or rejection of an AI proposal is a documented interpretive decision. The reflexive notes produced across all phases — capturing both the researcher's analytical process and AI's influence on it — constitute an audit trail of unusual transparency.

The risk runs the other direction. If AI proposals are accepted without genuine engagement, the audit trail documents compliance, not analysis. The three foundational principles exist to prevent this: "proving the obvious" ensures AI's predictable outputs are treated as reference points rather than findings; double reflexivity ensures the researcher monitors both their own and AI's interpretive influence; and the heuristic partner framing ensures AI remains a dialogic resource rather than an analytical authority.
