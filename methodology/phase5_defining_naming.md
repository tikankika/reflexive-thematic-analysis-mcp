# Phase 5: Defining and Naming Themes in Dialogic RTA

## Purpose

Phase 5 moves from structural soundness (achieved in Phase 4) to analytical precision. The researcher articulates exactly what each theme captures, what story it tells, and what it does not include. Theme names are finalized. Data extracts are selected for the write-up.

As Byrne explains (2022, p. 1407):

> "At this phase, the researcher is tasked with presenting a detailed analysis of the thematic framework. Each individual theme and sub-theme is to be expressed in relation to both the dataset and the research question(s)."

In dialogic contexts, Phase 5 is where a subtle but significant risk emerges: the colonization of the researcher's analytical language by AI. If AI has been a dialogic partner throughout coding and theme generation, its vocabulary — characteristically fluent, generically precise, analytically flat — may have seeped into how the researcher thinks about and names their themes. Phase 5 is where the researcher reclaims full ownership of the analytical voice.

---

## Defining Themes

Defining a theme means being able to articulate clearly and precisely what it captures — and equally important, what it does not capture.

### For each theme, the researcher must answer:

**What is the essence of this theme?** What pattern of shared meaning does it capture? What is the central organizing concept? Can this be articulated in two or three sentences that would make sense to someone who has not read the data?

**What story does this theme tell?** Themes are not labels or summaries. They are interpretive constructions that tell stories about patterns in the data. What is the narrative this theme constructs?

**What are its boundaries?** What does this theme include? What does it explicitly exclude? Where another theme begins is where this one ends — and that boundary should be defensible.

**How does it relate to the research questions?** What does this theme contribute to the analysis? Why does it matter?

**How does it relate to other themes?** Is it standalone, connected, dependent on another theme, in tension with another theme?

### The definition test

If you can remove the theme name and replace it with the definition, and the analysis still reads clearly, the definition is strong. If the theme name is doing work that the definition cannot do, either the name is misleading or the definition is incomplete.

## Naming Themes

Theme names are the first thing readers encounter. They communicate — or fail to communicate — what the analysis has achieved.

### Principles

**Capture the essence, not the topic.** "Time constraints" is a topic label. "The bidirectional influence of time" (Byrne, 2022) is an analytical name — it tells the reader that something specific and interpreted has been found about how time operates.

**Concise but informative.** Brief enough to be memorable, detailed enough to convey the analytical content.

**Distinctive from each other.** If two theme names could be confused, either the themes overlap or the names are imprecise.

**Creative where it serves clarity.** Byrne's "Incompletely theorised agreements" is borrowed from legal/philosophical discourse and immediately communicates a sophisticated concept — people proceeding on practical agreements without theoretical grounding. The name does analytical work. But creativity should not sacrifice clarity — an evocative name that obscures the theme's content fails its purpose.

### The AI language risk

AI-generated theme names tend toward a recognizable style: grammatically correct, topically accurate, analytically generic. "Navigating challenges in AI integration." "Balancing innovation and tradition." "Teacher perspectives on digital transformation."

These names are not wrong. They are empty. They could apply to any study on this topic. They communicate topic but not interpretation.

**The test:** Would this theme name distinguish your analysis from any other analysis of similar data? If not, it is a topic label masquerading as a theme name.

If AI has been used throughout the analysis, its characteristic vocabulary may have infiltrated the researcher's thinking. Phase 5 is the moment to notice this and reassert the researcher's own analytical language — language that reflects their specific interpretive engagement with this specific data.

### Where AI can contribute

AI is not only a risk in Phase 5. Used critically, it can strengthen definitions and names:

**Testing definitions.** Ask AI to apply your theme definition to data extracts it hasn't seen in the context of that theme. If AI can correctly identify which extracts belong and which don't, the definition has clear boundaries. If it misclassifies, the definition may be imprecise — a useful diagnostic.

**Generating alternative names.** AI can produce multiple candidate theme names that the researcher evaluates. The value is not in AI's names themselves (which will tend toward the generic) but in the researcher's reaction to them. Seeing what AI proposes can clarify what the researcher's own analytical language is doing that AI's cannot.

**Identifying boundary cases.** AI can surface codes or data extracts that sit at the edges of theme definitions — cases that could belong to this theme or another. These boundary cases are where definitions need to be sharpest.

In all cases, AI's contribution is instrumental: it tests, provokes, and surfaces — it does not define.

## Selecting Data Extracts

Phase 5 is when the researcher identifies which data extracts will illustrate each theme in the write-up.

### Selection principles

**Vivid and compelling.** Choose extracts that really capture the essence of the pattern. Byrne (2022, p. 1407): extracts should "provide a vivid and compelling account of the arguments being made by a respective theme."

**Multiple extracts across the dataset.** Not just one or two quotes. Use extracts from different participants (or different focus groups) to show the pattern exists across the data and to demonstrate diversity within the pattern.

**Show the range.** Extracts should demonstrate both the coherence of the theme (different data items sharing the core pattern) and the diversity of expression (participants articulating the pattern in different ways).

**Quality over quantity.** Enough to support the interpretation convincingly. Not so many that the write-up becomes a series of quotes rather than analysis.

---

## Illustrative vs. Analytical Write-Up

Byrne (2022, p. 1407–1409) distinguishes two approaches that have direct implications for Phase 5 preparation.

### Illustrative

Data extracts illustrate patterns the researcher has described. The extract supports the argument. If the extract is removed, the write-up still makes sense — but is less compelling.

### Analytical

Data extracts are integral to the analytical argument. The researcher interprets the extract — not just what it says but what it reveals, how it connects to theory, what its deeper significance is. If the extract is removed, the write-up no longer makes sense.

**For reflexive TA, analytical is preferred.** RTA is interpretive by nature. The write-up should demonstrate that the researcher has done more than describe what participants said — they have interpreted what it means.

**The straightforward test** (Terry et al., 2017): if the extract can be removed and the write-up still makes sense → illustrative. If the extract is removed and the write-up no longer makes sense → analytical.

**In dialogic contexts:** AI can produce fluent illustrative write-up — describing what participants said, organized under themes. What AI cannot produce is genuine analytical writing — interpretation that connects specific data to theoretical insight through the researcher's own engagement. Phase 5 preparation should orient toward analytical treatment, because this is where the researcher's interpretive contribution is most visible and most necessary.

## Preparing the Narrative Structure

Theme ordering matters. Themes should connect logically, building a coherent narrative.

### Byrne's rationale for ordering

Byrne reported his five themes in a specific order (2022, p. 1410):

1. **Best Practice** first — established the underlying positivity in all accounts, easing the reader into the analysis
2. **Axiology** second — introduced the "yes, but..." reality, signposting what follows
3. **Influence of Time** third — established structural constraints
4. **Incompletely Theorised Agreements** fourth — showed how constraints led to atheoretical practice (builds on previous theme)
5. **Recognising Educator Wellbeing** last — gave closing voice to educators' own experience

The ordering tells a story: from ideal to reality, from structural constraint to conceptual consequence, to human cost.

### Questions for ordering

- Which theme provides the most logical entry point for readers?
- Which themes must be understood before others make sense?
- What order creates the most compelling narrative arc?
- Which theme provides the most satisfying conclusion?

---

## Quality Check

Before moving to Phase 6:

**Theme definitions:**
- Each theme has a clear central organizing concept articulated in the researcher's own words
- Boundaries are explicit — what each theme includes and excludes
- The definition would make sense to someone who has not read the data

**Theme names:**
- Names capture analytical content, not just topics
- Names are distinctive from each other
- Names reflect the researcher's interpretive language, not AI's generic vocabulary

**Data extracts:**
- Selected extracts are vivid, diverse, and analytically productive
- Multiple extracts from across the dataset for each theme
- The researcher has considered whether to use illustrative or analytical treatment

**Narrative structure:**
- Theme ordering is logical and creates a coherent arc
- Relationships between themes are clear
- The framework as a whole addresses the research questions
