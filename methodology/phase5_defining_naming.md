# Phase 5: Defining and Naming Themes in AI-Augmented RTA
## Based on Byrne (2022)

---

## Moving from Structure to Substance

Phase 5 is where you move from having a solid thematic framework (achieved in Phase 4) to being able to present a detailed, compelling analysis of what each theme means. As Byrne explains (2022, p. 1407):

> "At this phase, the researcher is tasked with presenting a detailed analysis of the thematic framework. Each individual theme and sub-theme is to be expressed in relation to both the dataset and the research question(s)."

You now have themes that work structurally. Phase 5 is about defining precisely what each theme captures, what story it tells, and crafting names that communicate this effectively. This is also where you select the specific data extracts you'll use to illustrate each theme in your write-up.

---

## The Work of Defining Themes

Defining a theme means being able to articulate clearly and precisely what it's about—and equally important, what it's NOT about.

### What Does This Theme Capture?

For each theme, you need to be able to answer:

**What is the essence of this theme?**
What pattern of meaning does it capture? What's the central organizing concept? Can you articulate this in a few clear sentences?

**What story does this theme tell?**
Themes aren't just labels or summaries—they tell stories about patterns in your data. What narrative does this theme construct?

**How does this theme relate to your research questions?**
Why does this theme matter for your analysis? What does it contribute to answering your RQs?

**What are the boundaries of this theme?**
What does it include? What does it exclude? Where does it start and end?

**How does this theme relate to other themes?**
Is it a standalone pattern, or does it connect to other themes? Does it build on another theme? Does it contrast with another theme?

### Byrne's Example: Defining "The Whole-School Approach"

Byrne's sub-theme "the whole-school approach" (part of the larger theme "Best practice in wellbeing promotion") provides a good example of theme definition.

**What it captures**: The pattern that participants emphasized involving entire school staff in wellbeing promotion, not just designated wellbeing educators.

**The story it tells**: Effective wellbeing promotion requires whole-school buy-in and participation, but this creates challenges around who is appropriately positioned to deliver different aspects.

**Key aspects**:
- Collective responsibility across staff
- Importance of having the "right person" for different aspects
- Tensions around appropriateness and capability
- Not just about curriculum delivery but broader school culture

**What it's NOT about**: It's not about curriculum structure or pedagogical methods specifically—it's about the organizational approach to wellbeing promotion.

This level of clear definition allows you to select appropriate data extracts and write a focused analysis.

## Selecting Data Extracts for Each Theme

Phase 5 is when you identify which specific data extracts you'll use to illustrate each theme in your write-up.

### Principles for Selecting Extracts

**Extracts should be vivid and compelling**. Choose quotes that really capture the essence of the pattern you're describing. Byrne notes that extracts should "provide a vivid and compelling account of the arguments being made by a respective theme" (p. 1407).

**Use multiple extracts across the dataset**. Don't just use one or two quotes. Use multiple extracts from different participants (or different focus groups in your case) to show the pattern exists across your data and to demonstrate diversity within the pattern.

**Show the range of the theme**. Your extracts should demonstrate both the coherence of the pattern (how different data items share the core pattern) and the diversity of expressions (how participants express this pattern in different ways).

**Balance is important**. You need enough extracts to support your interpretation convincingly, but not so many that your write-up becomes a series of quotes rather than analysis.

### What Makes a Good Extract?

**Clarity**: The extract should be clear enough to stand alone (though you'll contextualize it).

**Relevance**: It should directly illustrate the point you're making about the theme.

**Richness**: Ideally, extracts have depth—they give you something to analyze, not just describe.

**Representativeness**: While no single extract can represent an entire theme, each extract should feel representative of the broader pattern.

## Illustrative vs. Analytical Write-Up

A crucial distinction in Phase 5 is understanding the difference between illustrative and analytical approaches to presenting your themes. Byrne provides excellent examples of both (Boxes 3 and 4, p. 1407-1409).

### Illustrative Write-Up

An **illustrative approach** presents data extracts as illustrations of the pattern you're describing. The extract is there to show what participants said. The interpretation is relatively surface-level.

**Byrne's example** (Box 3, p. 1408): In discussing the need for an "appropriate educator" to deliver wellbeing curriculum, he presents an extract where a participant describes how a physical education teacher with a military background ran students "ragged" with an emphasis on fitness rather than wellness.

The accompanying text describes what happened—the teacher's approach, the negative impact on students, the complaints from parents. It's illustrative: here's an example of having the wrong person in the role.

**Characteristics of illustrative write-up**:
- Describes what the data extract says
- Provides context and explanation
- Relatively straightforward presentation
- Extract could be removed and write-up would still make sense (but be less compelling)

### Analytical Write-Up

An **analytical approach** goes beyond illustration to deeply analyze what the extract means, contextualizing it theoretically and examining its implications.

**Byrne's example** (Box 4, p. 1408-1409): Using a different extract from the same theme, a participant discusses needing management to be "really careful" about who teaches SPHE (wellbeing curriculum), saying certain teachers don't have the "personality" to deliver content around sex, drugs, and alcohol—they won't have "hard conversations" with youngsters.

Byrne's analytical write-up examines:
- How participants constructed the criteria for "appropriate educator" through negative connotations
- The pattern of defining appropriateness by what it's NOT rather than what it IS
- Connection to theoretical concepts (value dissimilarity, Struch & Schwartz 1989; negative perceptions of the "other" group, Sirin McCreary & Mahalik 2004)
- Implications for how participants were constructing identity boundaries

**Characteristics of analytical write-up**:
- Interprets what the data means, not just what it says
- Contextualizes in relation to theory and literature
- Examines implications and deeper meanings
- Extract is integral—removing it would make the write-up incomprehensible
- Goes to latent level of meaning

### Which Approach Should You Use?

Byrne notes (p. 1407): "While an illustrative write-up of RTA results is completely acceptable, the researcher should remain cognisant that the narrative of the write-up should communicate the complexities of the data, while remaining 'embedded in the scholarly field.'"

**The test**: If an extract can be removed and the write-up still makes sense → illustrative. If removing the extract makes the write-up incomprehensible → analytical.

**For reflexive TA**, an analytical approach is generally more appropriate because:
- RTA is interpretive by nature
- You're capturing latent meanings, not just semantic content
- You want to show the analytical depth of your work
- You're making theoretical contributions, not just describing data

However, you might use illustrative write-up for some parts (perhaps when introducing a theme or providing context) and analytical write-up for deeper exploration.

## The Art of Naming Themes

Theme names are important—they're the first thing readers encounter and should communicate something meaningful about each theme.

### Principles for Good Theme Names

**Concise but informative**: Names should be brief enough to be memorable but informative enough to convey what the theme is about.

**Not too generic**: Avoid names so broad they could apply to anything. "Challenges" or "Experiences" are too generic.

**Not too specific**: Avoid names so specific they sound like codes rather than themes. "Lack of training" is too narrow for a theme (that's a code).

**Capture the essence**: The name should communicate the central organizing concept or key insight of the theme.

**Consider creativity**: Braun and Clarke (2013, 2014, 2020) encourage creativity in naming. Sometimes a catchy phrase can make a theme more memorable and engaging.

### Byrne's Theme Names as Examples

Looking at Byrne's finalized theme names (Figure 2, p. 1405):

**"Best Practice in Wellbeing Promotion"**
- Clear and descriptive
- Signals positive/constructive focus
- Immediately understandable

**"The Influence of Time"**
- More conceptual than "time constraints"
- "Influence" suggests complexity (not just "lack of time")
- Opens space for bidirectional analysis

**"Incompletely Theorised Agreements"**
- Creative and intellectually engaging
- Borrowed from legal/philosophical discourse
- Immediately intriguing—makes you want to know more
- Captures sophisticated concept (people agreeing on practice without theoretical grounding)

**"The Axiology of Wellbeing Promotion"**
- Uses precise theoretical term ("axiology" = study of value)
- Elevated language signals analytical depth
- Not just "value" but examination of value itself

**"Recognising Educator Wellbeing"**
- Clear focus on recognition/awareness
- Parallel structure with "Recognising Student Wellbeing" (creates coherence)

### Finding Creative Names

Braun and Clarke suggest looking within your data for evocative phrases that might punctuate a theme name. Sometimes participants use a phrase that perfectly captures a pattern.

You might also draw from theoretical literature—using a concept that captures your theme's essence (like Byrne's "Incompletely Theorised Agreements").

But creativity shouldn't compromise clarity. The primary purpose is communication, not cleverness.

### Revising Names from Phase 3 and 4

Your theme names from earlier phases were working titles. Now's the time to polish them. Ask:
- Does this name accurately capture what the theme is about?
- Is it clear and memorable?
- Does it communicate something meaningful?
- Is it appropriate for the tone of your research?

Don't be afraid to change names if you find better alternatives.

## Writing Theme Definitions

For each theme, write a clear definition that you might use in your eventual write-up or dissertation. This helps crystallize your understanding.

### Template for Theme Definitions

```markdown
## Theme: [Name]

**Central organizing concept**: 
[1-2 sentences capturing the essence of what this theme is about]

**What this theme captures**:
[Detailed description of the pattern this theme identifies]

**Key aspects of this theme**:
- [Aspect 1]
- [Aspect 2]
- [Aspect 3]

**Relationship to research questions**:
[How this theme contributes to answering your RQs]

**Relationship to other themes**:
[How this theme connects to, builds on, or contrasts with other themes]

**Sub-themes** (if applicable):
- [Sub-theme 1]: [Brief description]
- [Sub-theme 2]: [Brief description]

**Representative data extracts**:
[List key quotes that illustrate this theme]
```

Writing these definitions serves multiple purposes:
- Clarifies your own understanding
- Provides material for your write-up
- Helps you see if definitions are clear and distinctive
- Creates reference document for drafting

## Using AI in Theme Definition and Naming

AI can be a useful dialogic partner in Phase 5, helping you refine definitions and explore naming options.

### Refining Theme Definitions

```
I have this theme:

Name: [current name]
Central concept: [your articulation]
Key aspects: [list]

Help me refine the definition:
1. Is the central organizing concept clearly articulated?
2. Are there aspects I'm missing?
3. Is there a more precise way to capture this pattern?
4. What are the boundaries—what does this include/exclude?
```

### Exploring Theme Names

```
I'm working on naming this theme:

Pattern captured: [description of what theme is about]
Current working name: [name]

Suggest alternative names that:
- Capture the essence of this pattern
- Are concise but informative
- Range from straightforward descriptive to more creative/conceptual

For each suggestion, explain what it emphasizes.
```

### Testing Clarity

```
Here's my theme definition:

[Your definition]

Questions:
1. Is this clear to someone who hasn't seen my data?
2. Does it communicate what's distinctive about this pattern?
3. Is it too broad? Too narrow?
4. How could this be more precise?
```

### Checking Distinctiveness

```
Here are my theme names and brief definitions:

Theme 1: [name + definition]
Theme 2: [name + definition]
Theme 3: [name + definition]

Are the themes clearly distinctive from each other based on these names/definitions?
Are any names or definitions too similar?
```

### Critical Evaluation Required

As always, critically evaluate AI suggestions:
- Do suggested names accurately capture your theme?
- Do refined definitions honor your interpretation?
- Is AI helping you think more clearly or leading you astray?

Document your engagement:
```markdown
AI suggestions for theme naming:
- [suggestion 1]: [your evaluation]
- [suggestion 2]: [your evaluation]

Final decision: [your chosen name]
Rationale: [why this works best]
```

## Preparing the Narrative Structure

Phase 5 is also when you think about how to order and connect your themes in your write-up.

### Ordering Themes Logically

Byrne explains his rationale for theme order (p. 1410):

**He reported "Best Practice" first** because:
- Established the underlying positivity in participants' accounts
- Strongly influenced by semantic codes (easily digestible)
- Eases reader into the analysis

**"The Axiology of Wellbeing" came second** because:
- Introduced the "yes, but..." reality
- Provided signposting for next themes
- Showed complexity beneath positivity

**"The Influence of Time" and "Incompletely Theorised Agreements" followed** because:
- Time theme established how constraints negatively affect training
- This contributed to context in which educators adopt incompletely theorized approaches
- Logical progression building understanding

**"Recognising Educator Wellbeing" came last** because:
- Gave closing voice to educators' own wellbeing
- Showed how wellbeing promotion influenced educators themselves
- Appropriate culmination given research purpose

### Consider Your Narrative Arc

Think about:
- Which theme establishes important context or foundation?
- Which themes build on each other?
- What order creates a compelling argument?
- What order makes sense for your research questions?
- What provides satisfying structure for reader?

Themes should connect meaningfully, building a coherent narrative while remaining internally consistent and capable of standing alone if needed.

## Quality Check: Are Definitions Clear and Comprehensive?

Before moving to Phase 6, check that your theme definitions are solid:

**For each theme, can you articulate**:
✓ What it's about (central organizing concept)
✓ What story it tells
✓ How it relates to research questions
✓ What its boundaries are (what it includes/excludes)
✓ How it relates to other themes
✓ What data extracts best illustrate it

**About theme names**:
✓ Names are concise but informative
✓ Names capture essence of themes
✓ Names are distinctive from each other
✓ Names are appropriate for your research context

**About your framework**:
✓ Themes together tell coherent story
✓ Order of presentation is logical
✓ Relationships between themes are clear
✓ Framework addresses research questions comprehensively

**About write-up approach**:
✓ You've identified key data extracts for each theme
✓ You understand illustrative vs. analytical approaches
✓ You're prepared to write analytical narratives
✓ You have material to connect themes to literature

## Preparing for Phase 6: Writing Up

At the end of Phase 5, you should have:

✓ Clearly defined themes with precise articulation of what each captures
✓ Finalized theme names that are concise, informative, and appropriate
✓ Selected data extracts for each theme
✓ Understanding of illustrative vs. analytical write-up
✓ Plan for ordering and connecting themes in your write-up
✓ Theme definition document serving as reference

**You're ready for Phase 6** (producing the report) when you can clearly articulate each theme and have the material prepared to write a compelling analytical narrative.

Phase 6 is the actual writing, but much of the intellectual work of preparing to write happens in Phase 5. Your clear definitions and selected extracts will make the writing process much smoother.

---

## The Transition to Writing

Byrne notes an important point about Phase 6 (p. 1409): "The separation between phases five and six can often be blurry. Further, this 'final' phase would rarely only occur at the end of the analysis."

In reality, you've probably been writing throughout your analysis—reflexive memos, code definitions, theme development notes. Phase 6 is about producing the formal write-up, but it builds on writing you've been doing all along.

Similarly, Phase 5 definition work will continue as you write. Writing itself is analytical work—as you write, you'll often refine your understanding of themes and discover new connections or nuances.

But having clear, well-defined themes before you begin formal writing makes the process much easier and your analysis more coherent.

## Your Context: Focus Groups and Swedish Codes

**For your focus group data**, theme definitions might attend to:
- How patterns manifest across different groups
- Whether certain themes are more prominent in certain groups
- How group dynamics influenced these patterns
- Diversity and consensus within themes

**For Swedish codes and data**:
- Your theme names might be in English (standard for academic writing) while codes remain Swedish
- Definitions should capture Swedish-language nuances
- Selected extracts will be in Swedish (with translations provided in write-up)
- Consider how translation might affect meaning when selecting extracts

**For AI-augmented analysis**:
- Document how AI contributed to theme definition
- Maintain transparency about human interpretive authority in definitions
- Your clear definitions show your interpretive work, not AI's

---

## A Final Note on Defining and Naming

Phase 5 is where abstract patterns become concrete, articulable themes. The work of precise definition—being able to say exactly what each theme captures and doesn't capture—is intellectually demanding but essential.

Well-defined themes with well-chosen names make your analysis clear, compelling, and defensible. They show you've done deep interpretive work and can communicate it effectively.

As Byrne demonstrates, theme names can range from straightforward and descriptive to creative and conceptually rich. What matters is that they accurately represent your themes and communicate effectively with your audience.

The clarity you achieve in Phase 5 will make Phase 6 (writing) much more straightforward and will result in a more coherent, compelling final product.
